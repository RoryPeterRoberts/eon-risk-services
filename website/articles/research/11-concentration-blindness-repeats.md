# Research Extraction: Article 11 — The S&L Crisis to SVB: How Concentration Blindness Repeats Every 15 Years

## Source: Book Chapters 1, 5, 8, 9, 10, 12, 13, 16

---

## THESIS

Concentration risk — the same fundamental failure mode — keeps recurring across different decades and asset classes because risk identification processes fail to catch it. The book's Industry Loss Database (179 events, $2.3 trillion, 30 countries, 6 decades) shows concentration blindness as "the single most destructive failure mode in the database" appearing "in every decade, every jurisdiction, and across every risk type" (Ch16). The pattern is always the same: an institution accumulates exposure to a single counterparty, sector, geography, or funding source, and the risk identification process either fails to detect the concentration or detects it but classifies it as acceptable.

## ERA 1: THE S&L CRISIS (1980s)

### American Savings & Loan
- **Year:** Seized 1988
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class (high-risk mortgages, speculative securities) and interest rate mismatch
- **How it built up / why missed:** The Depository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act of 1980 and the Garn-St. Germain Act of 1982 removed interest rate ceilings and expanded permissible activities. American Savings responded with aggressive high-risk mortgage origination and speculative securities investments. The risk identification failure was treating deregulation as an opportunity without identifying that interest rate liberalisation had fundamentally altered the asset-liability risk profile. Complacency bred by structural change.
- **Loss figure:** $5.4 billion
- **Footnote:** [^ch16-18]: FDIC, *Managing the Crisis: The FDIC and RTC Experience*, 1998. American Savings was seized by FSLIC in 1988. Depository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act of 1980 (Pub.L. 96-221) and Garn-St. Germain Depository Institutions Act of 1982 (Pub.L. 97-320).
- **What would have caught it:** Phase 1 PESTLE assessment would have identified the macro-economic conditions enabling the crisis. Event-driven trigger framework (Ch13) — deregulation itself should have triggered re-identification of the asset-liability risk profile. The ongoing cycle's quarterly re-identification would have caught the accumulating mismatch.

## ERA 2: THE 2000s — GLOBAL FINANCIAL CRISIS AND PRE-CRISIS BUILD-UP

### Countrywide Financial
- **Year:** Acquired 2008, losses through 2014+
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class (subprime/Alt-A mortgages), with originate-to-distribute illusion masking retained risk
- **How it built up / why missed:** Became America's largest mortgage originator through aggressive subprime and Alt-A expansion. The stated business model was that securitisation transferred risk to investors. The risk identification failure was not recognising that retained tranches, warehouse lines, representations-and-warranties obligations, and reputational attachment meant securitisation transferred the asset but not the risk.
- **Loss figure:** Acquisition by BofA for $4 billion ultimately cost Bank of America over $40 billion in settlements and litigation — a 10:1 ratio demonstrating how badly residual exposure was underestimated.
- **Footnote:** [^ch16-5]: BofA Annual Reports/SEC filings 2008-2014. $4B acquisition announced 11 Jan 2008. Subsequent settlements include $16.65B DOJ National Mortgage Settlement (2014), $8.5B private-label RMBS settlement (2011), among others. FCIC Report, Ch11.
- **What would have caught it:** Enterprise portfolio view (Ch8) requiring explicit assessment of aggregate position against appetite, including concentration analysis across all three forms (Ch10). PESTLE assessment would have classified rapid subprime growth as macro-economic risk factor. SWIFT workshop would have asked: "What are we assuming about the securitisation market that might not be true?" The assumption register is designed to capture and challenge exactly this kind of unexamined premise.

### Northern Rock
- **Year:** Nationalised February 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Funding (75% wholesale funding dependence)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Explicitly chose wholesale-funded rapid growth as competitive model. Board approved it. Regulator was aware. External context was changing throughout 2006-2007 (US subprime delinquencies rising, structured credit market stress, wholesale funding spreads widening). No systematic assessment connected these external changes to Northern Rock's specific business model. Stress testing used benign historical scenarios excluding simultaneous securitisation market seizure.
- **Loss figure:** Approximately GBP 2 billion taxpayer cost
- **Footnote:** [^ch16-6] and [^ch5-11]: House of Commons Treasury Committee, *The Run on the Rock*, Fifth Report of Session 2007-08, HC 56-I, January 2008. [^ch5-12]: Nationalised 22 February 2008 under Banking (Special Provisions) Act 2008. [^ch5-13]: NAO, *HM Treasury: The Nationalisation of Northern Rock*, HC 298, Session 2008-09.
- **What would have caught it:** PESTLE assessment mapping wholesale funding market conditions to institution's specific funding structure. The methodology requires external context findings be mapped to taxonomy categories and assessed against specific exposures. Business model itself was the unidentified risk.

### AIG (Financial Products)
- **Year:** Bailout September 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Counterparty/asset class ($527 billion CDS notional on mortgage-linked CDOs, with $78 billion in multi-sector CDO protection — the lethal concentration)
- **How it built up / why missed:** CDOs insured were rated AAA. Historical default rate on AAA near zero. Internal models calibrated to that data assigned negligible probability to losses. Single-dimensional assessment (financial only) using benign historical data of questionable relevance produced near-zero risk rating. Did not assess: regulatory consequence of ratings downgrade triggering collateral calls; reputational destruction of government intervention; customer/operational impact of counterparty failure cascading through every major bank.
- **Loss figure:** $85 billion Federal Reserve bailout (total government support ultimately exceeded $180 billion)
- **Footnote:** [^ch9-1]: AIG Form 10-K FY2007 ($527B notional). [^ch9-2]: $78B multi-sector CDO protection. FCIC Report pp.265-270. [^ch9-3]: Fed press release 16 Sep 2008 ($85B secured credit facility).
- **What would have caught it:** Four-dimensional impact framework (Ch9). Financial dimension alone was inadequate. Regulatory dimension (collateral call triggers on ratings downgrade) would score Major (4). Reputational dimension (world's largest insurer requiring bailout) would score Extreme (5). Dominant dimension rule produces overall impact 5 regardless of financial assumption. Data Quality Rating would be Low at best (models calibrated to period without relevant stress). Conservatism principle would prevent near-zero assessment. Vulnerability: Very High (5) — no hedging programme, no collateral reserve, no pre-positioned liquidity. Speed of onset: Immediate (5) — triggered by ratings action.

### Citigroup
- **Year:** Bailout 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class (US residential mortgage credit quality across on-balance-sheet, off-balance-sheet, direct, and contingent channels)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Multiple divisions each maintained own risk reporting and assessments — all within acceptable parameters individually. Investment banking had SIVs (off-balance-sheet entities holding subprime CDOs). Treasury reported contingent liquidity commitments. Consumer banking reported mortgage origination. No enterprise view connected CDO warehouse positions, SIV liquidity puts, mortgage origination pipeline, and commercial paper funding model. Off-balance-sheet SIV exposures excluded from risk aggregation because liquidity puts considered remote contingencies.
- **Loss figure:** $45 billion TARP bailout plus government loss-sharing on approximately $301 billion of assets. Over $50 billion in SIV assets brought back on balance sheet.
- **Footnote:** [^ch8-3] through [^ch8-6]: FCIC Report pp.298-303 (divisional structure), pp.299-301 (SIV structure, liquidity puts), p.301 ($49B SIV assets consolidated). US Treasury TARP Monthly Reports ($25B Oct 2008 + $20B Dec 2008).
- **What would have caught it:** Enterprise portfolio view (Ch8) — the five reconciliation steps would have forced the question: "What is our total exposure — direct, indirect, contingent, on-balance-sheet and off — to a decline in US residential mortgage credit quality?" Reconciliation between top-down and bottom-up would have surfaced what no individual business unit captured. Aggregate position against appetite assessment would have shown the total exceeding appetite by a catastrophic margin.

### Bear Stearns
- **Year:** Collapsed March 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class (mortgage-backed securities, structured credit)
- **How it built up / why missed:** In June 2007, two Bear Stearns hedge funds collapsed from CDO exposure. Parent bank treated this as an isolated subsidiary event — a contained loss not requiring re-examination of the bank's own retained structured credit positions. Nine months later, Bear Stearns collapsed from the same concentration risk. The institution went from operational to acquired in a matter of days (speed of onset = Immediate).
- **Loss figure:** $3.6 billion hedge fund loss; institution acquired by JPMorgan at $2/share (later $10), representing near-total value destruction
- **Footnote:** [^ch16-17]: FCIC Report pp.238-242. Hedge funds collapsed June-July 2007. Bear Stearns acquired 16 March 2008. [^ch9-14]: FCIC Report pp.280-291. Liquidity pool effectively depleted by 14 March. JPMorgan acquisition at $2/share (revised to $10).
- **What would have caught it:** Event-driven trigger framework (Ch13) — the collapse of two hedge funds managed by the parent bank, holding positions similar to those on the parent's own balance sheet, is the first of six triggers requiring immediate re-identification. A material loss or near-miss. Not an ambiguous signal — a direct warning. Bear Stearns did not process it.

### Merrill Lynch
- **Year:** Emergency sale September 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class ($40 billion gross subprime exposure, $32 billion in super-senior CDO tranches retained on balance sheet)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Super-senior tranches carried AAA ratings. Risk identification accepted AAA rating as sufficient evidence of near-risk-free status — substituting external agency opinion for independent risk analysis. No independent analysis examined the scenario in which systemic housing collapse would impair even super-senior tranches. An external credit rating is not a risk assessment — it is a single input to a risk assessment.
- **Loss figure:** $51.8 billion in write-downs and emergency sale to Bank of America
- **Footnote:** [^ch9-11]: FCIC Report pp.255-261 ($40B gross, $32B super-senior retained). [^ch9-12]: $51.8B cumulative write-downs across quarters. [^ch9-13]: FCIC Report pp.380-386. Emergency weekend negotiations 13-14 Sep 2008, BofA acquisition at $29/share (~half peak).
- **What would have caught it:** Four-dimensional assessment. Data Quality Rating: Low at best (external ratings from agencies whose revenue depended on CDO issuance, models not validated against nationwide decline). Conservatism principle would have prevented near-zero assessment. Independent challenge requirement (Ch9) — models as one input, not substitute for judgement.

### Lehman Brothers
- **Year:** Bankruptcy September 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class ($85 billion RMBS within $111 billion total real estate portfolio, leveraged 30:1), with cascading interaction across credit, market, liquidity, operational, and reputational risk
- **How it built up / why missed:** Risk function assessed each risk individually, in its own silo, using its own models, reported to its own committee. No one mapped causal chains between them. Repo 105 transactions temporarily removed $50 billion from balance sheet at quarter-end, undermining capital adequacy barrier. The interaction between risks — amplification, feedback loops, cascade — was invisible because methodology did not look for it.
- **Loss figure:** $639 billion in assets, largest bankruptcy in American history
- **Footnote:** [^ch10-1]: Valukas Examiner's Report Vol.1, Section III.A.1 ($85B RMBS, $111B real estate). [^ch10-2]: Net leverage ~16:1 reported, gross >30:1. [^ch10-3]: Ch11 filing 15 Sep 2008, Case No. 08-13555. [^ch10-6]: Repo 105, $50B removed at quarter-end.
- **What would have caught it:** Risk interaction matrix (Ch10) would have shown credit triggering market through MTM, market triggering liquidity through collateral calls, liquidity triggering operational through forced sales, reputational amplifying liquidity through counterparty withdrawal — a documented cascade. Bow-tie analysis would have identified Repo 105 as escalation factor undermining capital adequacy barrier. Concentration analysis would have flagged single-sector, single-asset-class exposure. Enterprise portfolio view and reconciliation would have aggregated the individually assessed risks into visible causal chains.

### Washington Mutual (WaMu)
- **Year:** Seized September 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class (subprime and option ARM mortgages)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Largest savings institution in US ($307 billion assets). Business model built on aggressive origination of subprime and option ARMs, corporate culture called "power of yes." Risk identification outputs existed — internal reports documented deteriorating loan quality, credit metrics showed rising delinquencies. But sales culture systematically prevented data from reaching capital planning. Chief Enterprise Risk Officer marginalised. Risk appetite limits repeatedly raised to accommodate growth targets. Credit risk function lacked authority to decline loans meeting minimum criteria.
- **Loss figure:** Largest bank failure in US history. Banking operations sold to JPMorgan for $1.9 billion.
- **Footnote:** [^ch12-8]: FDIC and OTS records ($307B assets). [^ch12-9]: FDIC PR-79-2008, seized 25 Sep 2008. [^ch12-10]: JPMorgan acquired for $1.888B.
- **What would have caught it:** Integration framework (Ch12) requiring every material risk maps to a capital scenario, with governance preventing management from overriding the connection between identification and capital. Mandatory scenario linkage, data-quality-informed conservatism, Board-level reporting of unlinked material risks would have made WaMu's selective blindness structurally impossible.

### Wachovia
- **Year:** Emergency sale October 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class ($122 billion option ARM portfolio from Golden West Financial acquisition)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Acquisition due diligence accepted Golden West's internal credit quality assessment — loans had performed well historically in California. Evidence basis was historical track record that did not include a single period of nationwide housing price decline. Option ARM product was relatively new. Portfolio was about to be deployed into fundamentally different economic environment. Assessment deserved Data Quality Rating of Very Low — historical performance taken at face value without disclosure of data limitations.
- **Loss figure:** Approximately $25 billion (convergence of operating losses, $18.7 billion goodwill impairment, option ARM writedowns)
- **Footnote:** [^ch9-9]: FCIC Report pp.137-138. $25.5B acquisition of Golden West, $122B option ARM portfolio. [^ch9-10]: FDIC press release 29 Sep 2008. Emergency acquisition by Wells Fargo Oct 2008.
- **What would have caught it:** Data Quality Rating framework (Ch9). Very Low rating would have triggered: conservatism principle requiring higher assumed losses; sensitivity analysis testing default rates exceeding historical record; Board would have seen that the largest acquisition was supported by the weakest possible evidence base.

### Icelandic Banks (Kaupthing, Landsbanki, Glitnir)
- **Year:** All failed within one week, October 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Systemic (entire banking system grew to 10x Iceland's GDP through aggressive international expansion funded by wholesale markets)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Each bank's risk assessment focused on its individual balance sheet — own liquidity, credit quality, capital adequacy. No individual bank's risk function identified the systemic concentration: entire banking system had grown beyond fiscal capacity of the sovereign to backstop. No credible lender of last resort at the scale required. Central Bank's financial stability reports assessed individual metrics without aggregating to system-level question: "Can this country backstop its banking system?"
- **Loss figure:** All three failed. Iceland imposed capital controls, currency collapsed, IMF bailout required ($2.1 billion programme).
- **Footnote:** [^ch10-9]: SIC Report to Althingi, 2010, Ch2. Combined assets ~10x GDP by end-2007. [^ch10-10]: Glitnir (29 Sep), Landsbanki (7 Oct), Kaupthing (9 Oct). Capital controls 28 Nov 2008. IMF Stand-By Arrangement 19 Nov 2008.
- **What would have caught it:** Concentration analysis (Ch10) requires asking not just "where are our internal concentrations?" but "what system-level concentrations is our institution embedded within?" This connects directly to PESTLE assessment (Ch5) — external context should identify system-level vulnerabilities individual assessments cannot see. This is systemic concentration — the third and most dangerous form.

### HSH Nordbank
- **Year:** 2009
- **Type of concentration:** False diversification (shipping finance + US subprime structured products — both correlated to global economic activity)
- **How it built up / why missed:** World's largest shipping lender, also built substantial US subprime structured products portfolio. Two exposures treated as diversified — different taxonomy categories, desks, geographies. Risk assessments internally sound for each. But both correlated to global economic activity. Same crisis impaired CDO valuations and collapsed trade volumes/shipping rates. Losses materialised simultaneously across "diversified" portfolios.
- **Loss figure:** EUR 10 billion state guarantee from Hamburg and Schleswig-Holstein, plus EUR 3 billion capital injection from SoFFin
- **Footnote:** [^ch10-11]: EUR 10B guarantee from Hamburg/Schleswig-Holstein, EUR 3B from SoFFin. European Commission State Aid Decision SA.29338, 20 Sep 2011. [^ch8-7]: HSH Nordbank AR 2007 (~EUR 30B shipping loan portfolio). [^ch8-8]: EC State Aid Decision SA.29338.
- **What would have caught it:** Enterprise portfolio view (Ch8) assessing correlation between apparently unrelated asset classes under stress conditions. Diversification assumption was itself a risk — only enterprise-level analysis could identify and challenge it. Reconciliation's fourth dimension (diversification and correlation) would have asked: "Under what conditions do these exposures move together?"

### Fortis
- **Year:** 2008
- **Type of concentration:** Structural (ABN AMRO acquisition consumed capital buffer at precisely the moment subprime losses required that capital)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Led consortium acquiring ABN AMRO for EUR 71 billion (largest banking acquisition in history). Acquisition funded by leverage, reducing capital buffers. Simultaneously held subprime exposure across both legacy and acquired portfolios. Risk identification assessed acquisition risk and subprime risk separately. Did not identify the interaction: acquisition reduced the capital buffer at the moment subprime losses demanded it. Each risk individually manageable; together they created a compounding spiral.
- **Loss figure:** EUR 11.2 billion government rescue. Broken up — largest corporate failure in Benelux history.
- **Footnote:** [^ch10-7]: RBS-led consortium acquired ABN AMRO 10 Oct 2007 for ~EUR 71B. Fortis share ~EUR 24B. [^ch10-8]: Belgium/Netherlands/Luxembourg injected EUR 11.2B on 28 Sep 2008. Subsequently broken up.
- **What would have caught it:** Risk interaction matrix (Ch10). The two risks were not merely correlated — they amplified each other through a direct mechanism. The acquisition consumed the capital the subprime losses subsequently demanded. Structural concentration in an assumption about capital adequacy.

## ERA 3: EUROZONE SOVEREIGN DEBT CRISIS (2010-2013)

### Alpha Bank (Greece)
- **Year:** Recapitalised 2012-2013
- **Type of concentration:** Sovereign (disproportionate domestic sovereign debt exposure disguised by regulatory framework)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Under Capital Requirements Regulation, eurozone sovereign debt carried zero risk weight — regulatory treatment that told risk identification processes there was nothing to identify. Alpha did not flag domestic sovereign debt as concentration risk because the regulatory framework explicitly stated it was not one. Most insidious form: concentration that regulatory frameworks actively disguise.
- **Loss figure:** Approximately EUR 4 billion recapitalisation through HFSF, part of EUR 30.9 billion across all four Greek systemic banks
- **Footnote:** [^ch16-3]: HFSF Annual Report 2013; EC State Aid Decisions SA.34823, SA.34825, SA.34826, SA.34827 (2012-2013).
- **What would have caught it:** Regulatory versus economic risk gap analysis (Ch12). The four-step gap analysis requires comparing regulatory treatment of every material risk against own economic assessment. A process that simply accepts zero risk weights for home-sovereign debt is not identifying risks — it is transcribing regulatory assumptions.

### Bank of Cyprus
- **Year:** Bail-in 2013
- **Type of concentration:** Sovereign (same zero-risk-weight blind spot as Alpha Bank)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Same regulatory blind spot — zero risk weight for eurozone sovereign debt. Accumulated concentrated Greek sovereign exposure.
- **Loss figure:** Uninsured depositors lost up to 47.5% — largest European bail-in of its time
- **Footnote:** [^ch16-4]: Central Bank of Cyprus, Decree of the Resolution Authority, 29 March 2013. Uninsured deposits above EUR 100,000 converted to equity at rates resulting in losses up to 47.5%.
- **What would have caught it:** Same as Alpha Bank — regulatory vs economic risk gap analysis.

### Bankia (Spain)
- **Year:** Bailout 2012
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class (property developer loans from seven merged cajas), compounded by strategic merger failure
- **How it built up / why missed:** Seven struggling cajas merged in 2010 to create Bankia. Strategic rationale: combining weak institutions would create stronger one. Risk identification reality: each caja held massive property developer loan portfolios inadequately provisioned. Combining them aggregated concentrated property exposures without recapitalisation. No enterprise portfolio view assessed aggregate property exposure. Strategic decision made on operational synergies, not risk identification outputs. Listed through IPO in 2011 with prospectus that materially misrepresented financial health.
- **Loss figure:** EUR 22.4 billion — Spain's largest bank bailout. Former chairman Rodrigo Rato convicted of fraud.
- **Footnote:** [^ch12-11]: Bank of Spain FSR May 2011. Bankia formed from Caja Madrid, Bancaja, and five smaller cajas. [^ch12-12]: EC State Aid Decision SA.35253, EUR 22.4B funded through ESM. [^ch12-13]: Audiencia Nacional, Criminal Chamber, 24 Feb 2017, Rato convicted. [^ch12-14]: CaixaBank merger completed 26 March 2021.
- **What would have caught it:** Risk identification framework integrated with strategic planning (Ch12). M&A due diligence must include full risk identification assessment — mapping target's risk profile against existing inventory, calculating combined enterprise portfolio view, identifying concentrations the combination would create. The seven cajas' property exposures, assessed individually, were each known. Assessed in aggregate, they would have been identified as a concentration the combined capital could not support.

### Dexia
- **Year:** First bailout 2008, second bailout and breakup 2011
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class (structured credit products and sovereign debt) and funding (short-term wholesale markets) — TWICE
- **How it built up / why missed:** First failure: large structured credit positions funded through short-term wholesale markets. After 2008 rescue, reconstituted risk framework. Identified the risks that caused near-failure. Documented maturity mismatch, wholesale funding dependency, structured product concentration. BUT identification outputs not integrated with capital planning with sufficient force to change behaviour. Sovereign debt again treated as effectively risk-free for capital purposes. Three years later: sovereign debt concentration (Greek, peripheral European bonds) funded through same short-term wholesale markets.
- **Loss figure:** First: EUR 6.4 billion capital injection + EUR 150 billion state guarantees. Second: broken up entirely with additional EUR 85 billion guarantees.
- **Footnote:** [^ch12-1]: EC State Aid Decision SA.33760 (2008/C). EUR 6.4B from Belgium/France/Luxembourg + EUR 150B guarantees. [^ch12-2]: EC State Aid Decision SA.33760 (2012/NN). Belgium acquired Dexia Bank Belgium (Belfius), France assumed Dexia Crédit Local.
- **What would have caught it:** Phase 5 integration (Ch12). Mandatory scenario linkage (sovereign concentration and wholesale funding mapped to ICAAP stress scenarios). Regulatory vs economic risk gap analysis (sovereign debt at zero risk weight vs economic reality). Board reporting with enterprise portfolio view showing the institution walking back into the same trap.

## ERA 4: 2020s — MODERN CONCENTRATION FAILURES

### Silicon Valley Bank (SVB)
- **Year:** Failed March 2023
- **Type of concentration:** Dual — asset (long-duration HTM securities, $91 billion) and liability (venture capital sector deposits, 94% uninsured)
- **How it built up / why missed:** During 2020-2021 tech boom, deposits nearly tripled to $189 billion. Management invested surplus in long-duration MBS and Treasuries. HTM portfolio reached $91 billion. As Fed raised rates 500bp over 18 months, unrealised losses exceeded $15 billion — more than total equity. CRO departed April 2022, not replaced until January 2023 — 8 months vacant during most consequential monetary policy shift in a generation. Risk committee met infrequently. The ongoing cycle had degraded to irrelevance. The risk was textbook IRRBB — a standard taxonomy entry every regulator expects banks to identify.
- **Loss figure:** $209 billion in assets. FDIC estimates $16.7-19.4 billion cost to Deposit Insurance Fund.
- **Footnote:** [^ch13-1]: Fed Review Apr 2023, p.6 ($209B assets). [^ch13-3]: $42B withdrawn in single day. [^ch13-5]: $91B HTM portfolio. [^ch13-7]: $15.2B unrealised losses vs $16.0B equity. [^ch13-8]: CRO departed Apr 2022, replaced Jan 2023. [^ch16-19]: SVB 10-K FY2022. Fed Review pp.6,10.
- **What would have caught it:** Quarterly re-identification (Ch13) would have flagged IRRBB as escalating risk from Q1 2022. Each Fed rate increase would appear in PESTLE update. SWIFT prompt "What has changed since last quarter?" would direct attention to growing unrealised losses. Event-driven updates — each rate hike was a significant external environment change, publicly signalled, widely discussed, directly relevant. Monthly KRI monitoring would show unrealised loss drifting from green toward red. Internal environment assessment would surface CRO vacancy.

### Signature Bank
- **Year:** Failed March 2023
- **Type of concentration:** Liability (cryptocurrency sector deposits)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Built significant deposit concentration in cryptocurrency sector. As crypto market deteriorated through 2022 — TerraUSD collapse May, Celsius bankruptcy June, FTX collapse November — each was an event-driven trigger that should have activated re-identification of concentration risk. Three separate warnings in six months. The quarterly cycle should have shown deposit concentration shifting from strategic advantage to existential vulnerability.
- **Loss figure:** $110 billion in assets, third-largest bank failure in US history
- **Footnote:** [^ch13-15]: FDIC PR-17-2023, 12 March 2023. NYDFS closed Signature Bank, $110B assets. [^ch13-16]: TerraUSD lost peg 9 May 2022; Celsius Ch11 filed 13 July 2022; FTX Ch11 filed 11 Nov 2022.
- **What would have caught it:** Event-driven trigger framework (Ch13) — three separate crypto industry failures in six months should each have forced immediate re-identification. Quarterly re-identification would have shown concentration shifting. Monthly KRI monitoring would have tracked deposit volatility.

## ADDITIONAL CASE STUDIES WITH CONCENTRATION ELEMENTS

### Anglo Irish Bank
- **Year:** Nationalised 2009
- **Type of concentration:** Asset class (commercial property lending at extraordinary levels relative to capital), compounded by cultural suppression
- **How it built up / why missed:** Under chairman FitzPatrick, pursued aggressive CRE lending during Celtic Tiger. Concentration visible in financial statements — analysts commented on it. But internal environment made identification impossible: culture of deference to chairman (career-threatening to challenge), growth equated with success, share price reinforced narrative that concentration was competitive advantage not risk. FitzPatrick concealed EUR 87 million in personal loans through temporary transfers to another bank around year-end. Board did not independently challenge. Risk identification treated property portfolio as core business rather than systemic exposure.
- **Loss figure:** EUR 29.3 billion taxpayer cost. Nationalised, renamed IBRC, liquidated 2013.
- **Footnote:** [^ch5-17]: ODCE investigation, Nyberg Commission Ch4. [^ch5-18]: Nationalised under Anglo Irish Bank Corporation Act 2009. [^ch5-19]: EUR 29.3B from NAMA records and Central Bank FSR. [^ch5-20]: IBRC liquidated 7 Feb 2013.
- **What would have caught it:** Internal environment assessment (Ch5) using seven COSO elements would have flagged: risk management philosophy subordinated to growth, board not independently challenging, ethical values compromised by deference, compensation rewarding growth without adjusting for concentration, HR standards penalising dissent.

### Hypo Real Estate / Depfa
- **Year:** EUR 102 billion guarantee 2008, nationalised 2009
- **Type of concentration:** Funding (Depfa's EUR 400 billion balance sheet funded almost entirely through short-term wholesale markets) and maturity mismatch
- **How it built up / why missed:** Acquired Depfa Bank (EUR 400B balance sheet). Depfa's model: lend long to governments, fund short through wholesale interbank. Credit quality excellent, yields thin, maturity mismatch catastrophic. External environment already changing (BNP Paribas fund suspensions Aug 2007, Northern Rock Sep 2007, interbank rates spiking). None of this systematically assessed as part of risk identification and mapped to Depfa's specific vulnerabilities.
- **Loss figure:** EUR 102 billion German government guarantee, nationalised 2009
- **Footnote:** [^ch5-1]: Hypo Real Estate AR 2007, BaFin assessments. [^ch5-4]: EUR 102B guarantee under Finanzmarktstabilisierungsgesetz Oct 2008. [^ch5-5]: Nationalised under Rettungsübernahmegesetz Apr 2009.
- **What would have caught it:** PESTLE assessment (Ch5) would have documented subprime contagion, central bank interventions, bank run, wholesale funding stress visible in LIBOR-OIS spreads. Each finding mapped to taxonomy — funding market stress maps to Liquidity Risk > Wholesale Funding Dependence. Internal context assessment would have documented that EUR 400B wholesale-funded balance sheet fundamentally changed risk profile.

### Credit Suisse / Archegos
- **Year:** March 2021
- **Type of concentration:** Cross-counterparty, cross-product (total return swap exposure aggregated across multiple prime brokers, invisible to any single business unit)
- **How it built up / why missed:** At Credit Suisse, the reconciliation process in 2017 had identified "cross-counterparty concentration in prime brokerage services" as a risk no business unit had captured. The risk existed between business units. Four years later, Archegos's total return swap positions spread across multiple prime brokers — no single broker had visibility of aggregate exposure. Same structural gap that reconciliation had identified was the gap Archegos later exploited.
- **Loss figure:** $5.5 billion
- **Footnote:** [^ch8-1]: Credit Suisse Group, *Report on Archegos Capital Management* (Paul, Weiss, 29 July 2021), p.1. [^ch8-2]: TRS positions spread across multiple prime brokers.
- **What would have caught it:** Reconciliation process (Ch8) — the gap between top-down and bottom-up reveals risks neither track could find independently. Enterprise portfolio view aggregating cross-product, cross-business-unit exposures.

### LTCM (Long-Term Capital Management)
- **Year:** 1998
- **Type of concentration:** Hidden concentration in market normality assumption (convergence trades across global fixed income that were actually a single concentrated bet on stable correlations)
- **How it built up / why missed:** Models calibrated to historical conditions indicated well-diversified convergence trades. Assumed correlations between markets were stable and liquidity always available. When Russian crisis caused correlations to spike to one across all markets simultaneously, entire portfolio moved against it. Positions that VaR showed as diversified turned out to be a single concentrated bet on market normality. VaR indicated max daily loss $35 million; lost $553 million in a single day.
- **Loss figure:** $3.6 billion bailout brokered by Fed with 14 counterparty banks; $125 billion balance sheet
- **Footnote:** [^ch16-7]: Lowenstein, *When Genius Failed*. Fed New York press statement 23 Sep 1998. [^ch1-2]: President's Working Group, *Hedge Funds, Leverage, and Lessons of LTCM*, Apr 1999.
- **What would have caught it:** Independent challenge requirement (Ch9) — models as input not substitute for judgement. Scenario analysis and speed-of-onset dimension designed to identify regime shifts. Enterprise portfolio view would have questioned whether apparent diversification held under stress.

## METHODOLOGY CONTENT: THREE FORMS OF CONCENTRATION

Source: Chapter 10

### 1. Single-Name Concentration
The most visible form: excessive exposure to a single counterparty, sector, geography, or asset class. The interaction matrix often reveals single-name concentration that individual risk assessments missed because the exposure appears in different taxonomy categories. Example: credit exposure to a major technology firm + market risk from equity positions in same firm + operational dependency on firm's cloud infrastructure + conduct risk from firm's distribution partnership = four risks, four taxonomy categories, one underlying concentration.

### 2. Structural Concentration
Less visible but often more dangerous. Concentration not in a single name but in a structural assumption — a funding model, correlation assumption, regulatory interpretation, technology platform. Example: Fortis — acquisition reduced capital buffer at precisely the moment subprime losses required that capital. Two risks amplifying each other through a direct mechanism.

### 3. Systemic Concentration
Extends beyond the institution to the system in which it operates. Example: Icelandic banks — entire banking system grown beyond fiscal capacity of sovereign. Concentration analysis must ask not just "where are our internal concentrations?" but "what system-level concentrations is our institution embedded within?"

**Key insight on hidden concentration:** The most dangerous concentrations are those disguised by apparent diversification. HSH Nordbank believed it was diversified (shipping finance + US subprime = different asset classes, geographies, risk categories). Both correlated to global economic activity. Concentration analysis must go beyond taxonomy-level aggregation to identify underlying drivers connecting risks across categories.

---

## METHODOLOGY CONTENT: ENTERPRISE PORTFOLIO VIEW

Source: Chapter 8

Four assessments:

1. **Common Exposures** — Multiple business units sharing exposure to same underlying driver. Operates at two levels: taxonomy-based aggregation and cross-taxonomy analysis (risks classified under different nodes but sharing same underlying driver).

2. **Simultaneous Crystallisation** — How a single event could trigger multiple risks across the institution at the same time. This is where concentration blindness becomes visible. Scenario-based: plausible stress events traced to see which risks would activate. Purpose is identification, not quantification.

3. **Aggregate Position Against Appetite** — Each business unit may be within its own limits while institution as a whole exceeds appetite. This is the defining insight. Citigroup: SIV exposures distributed across divisions, each individually within limits, collectively catastrophic.

4. **Diversification and Correlation** — Where genuine diversification exists vs where risks are more correlated than individual assessments suggest. Critical: correlations increase under stress — diversification benefits evaporate precisely when needed most. If diversification assumption relies on "historical correlations in normal markets," it has not been stress-tested and must be flagged as concentration risk in disguise.

## METHODOLOGY CONTENT: PESTLE AND SWIFT WORKSHOP APPLICATIONS TO CONCENTRATION

Source: Chapters 5, 6, 16

### PESTLE Application
- PESTLE assessment (Phase 1) maps external environment to institution's specific vulnerabilities
- Economic dimension: GDP trajectories, interest rate cycles, credit cycles — mapped to specific taxonomy categories
- Northern Rock example: wholesale funding market conditions should have been mapped to institution's specific funding structure
- For SVB: each Fed rate increase appears in PESTLE update as significant external environment change
- For S&L crisis: PESTLE would have identified macro-economic conditions enabling the crisis

### SWIFT Workshop Application
- Guide word "What are we assuming about X that might not be true?" — directly surfaces concentration assumptions
- Countrywide: SWIFT would have asked about securitisation market assumptions
- The assumption register captures and challenges unexamined premises
- "Where are the gaps between what we see at business unit level and what we would see at enterprise level?" — this prompt surfaced cross-counterparty concentration in prime brokerage at Credit Suisse
- Quarterly workshops: "What has changed since last quarter that could alter our risk profile?"

---

## METHODOLOGY CONTENT: REGULATORY VS ECONOMIC RISK GAP ANALYSIS

Source: Chapter 12

Four-step process for each material risk:
1. Identify the regulatory capital treatment (Pillar 1 standardised, internal models, Pillar 2A, or not captured at all)
2. Identify the economic risk assessment (four-dimensional score, data quality, interaction analysis)
3. Map the gap (where regulatory treatment less conservative than economic assessment, where regulatory framework does not capture risk at all, where regulatory categories fail to aggregate connected risks)
4. Document and escalate (gaps reported to Board Risk Committee, gaps exceeding thresholds require Board acknowledgement and typically additional capital)

**Key examples:**
- Alpha Bank / Bank of Cyprus: eurozone sovereign debt at zero risk weight under CRR, but economic risk of concentrated peripheral European sovereign exposure was materially higher. Gap analysis would have shown divergence, requiring Board action.
- Dexia (second failure): sovereign debt again treated as risk-free for capital purposes. Gap analysis would have shown economic risk assessment contradicted regulatory treatment.
- Citigroup: off-balance-sheet SIVs excluded from risk aggregation. Gap analysis would have shown regulatory framework not capturing the full economic exposure.

## METHODOLOGY CONTENT: THE BOILING FROG PROBLEM / GRADUAL ACCUMULATION

Source: Chapters 1, 13, 16

### The Complacency Cycle
Extended periods of low losses breed overconfidence and reduced vigilance. The absence of recent failures interpreted as evidence risks do not exist rather than as a period during which risks are accumulating. The pattern is always the same: the ongoing cycle degrades because the institution equates the absence of loss with the absence of risk.

**Key diagnostic (Ch13):** "An institution where no new risks are identified across three consecutive quarterly cycles is either operating in a static environment or running a process that has succumbed to compliance theatre."

### How Concentration Builds Gradually
- Risk identification processes degrade incrementally
- Quarterly re-identification becomes a roll-forward exercise
- Event-driven triggers are not processed
- New risks are not identified because old risks have not crystallised
- Recent track record used as evidence risk is low, when absence of losses is a lagging indicator saying nothing about risks currently being accumulated

### The Methodology's Response
Four mechanisms operating at different frequencies (Ch13):
1. **Quarterly re-identification** — active re-identification, not passive review. "Are there new risks we did not identify last quarter?" not just "have existing risks changed?"
2. **Annual full re-identification** — all six phases from scratch, specifically to prevent institutional complacency
3. **Event-driven updates** — six trigger categories ensuring inventory updated immediately when circumstances demand
4. **Internal audit assurance** — seven areas tested, including whether the ongoing cycle has degraded

### Specific Anti-Complacency Features
- Assumption register revisited quarterly — assumptions reasonable 12 months ago may be untenable now
- Data Quality Ratings tracked for improvement — risk at Low quality for 3 consecutive quarters without improvement plan is itself a risk identification failure
- Sensitivity testing on Low/Very Low data quality — if moving likelihood one level higher crosses materiality threshold, risk treated as potentially material regardless
- Conservatism principle — uncertainty should not reduce attention

## 1. S&L Crisis (1980s-1990s)

### Scale of Failures and Cost
- **1,043 thrift institutions failed** with total assets exceeding **$519 billion**
- The FSLIC closed or resolved 296 thrifts from 1986-1989; the Resolution Trust Corporation (RTC) subsequently closed 747 S&Ls with assets over $407 billion
- **Total cost to taxpayers: ~$124-132 billion** (the GAO estimated $160 billion total, with $132.1 billion from taxpayers). Congress appropriated $105 billion to the RTC; $91.3 billion was ultimately used
- At the 1988 peak, over 40% of thrift failures nationwide occurred in Texas alone

### Concentration in Commercial Real Estate
- After the Garn-St. Germain Act (1982), thrift residential mortgage holdings as a proportion of assets fell from over 80% to less than 60% between 1980 and 1986, as S&Ls shifted into commercial real estate and junk bonds
- During the 1980s, total real estate loans of banks more than tripled. Commercial real estate loans nearly quadrupled. As a percentage of total bank assets, nonresidential and construction loans nearly doubled from 6% to 11% between 1980 and 1990
- Regional concentration was acute in the American Southwest, driven by 1981 tax code changes for real estate and energy price expectations

### Regulatory Response
- **FIRREA (1989):** Abolished the Federal Home Loan Bank Board and FSLIC; created the Office of Thrift Supervision and RTC; provided $50 billion for crisis resolution; raised insurance premiums; limited S&L non-mortgage holdings to 30%; required junk bond divestment
- **FDICIA (1991):** Created the **Prompt Corrective Action (PCA) framework**, forcing regulators and banks to act when capital ratios fell below thresholds; expanded examination authority requiring annual inspections

## 2. Asian Financial Crisis (1997-1998) - Banking Failures

### Currency and Maturity Mismatch Concentration
- Banks and large non-financial firms across Thailand, Indonesia, and South Korea had borrowed massively in US dollars, often on short-term maturities, to fund long-term projects -- a **double mismatch** (currency and maturity)
- **Thailand:** Short-term foreign debt comprised 65% of total international debt by end-1997. The ratio of international reserves to short-term debt stood at only 70.4%. Short-term debt reached 20.8% of GDP in 1996, exceeding warning thresholds. The government closed 58 finance companies in 1997
- **Indonesia:** The banking sector was highly leveraged with loan-to-deposit ratios exceeding 100% in 1997; NPLs reached ~27% of total loans by September 1997. Short-term borrowings funded long-term projects in non-trade sectors
- **South Korea:** The IMF characterized the Korean crisis as a liquidity crisis due to serious mismatches in maturity, currency, and capital structure

### Specific Bank Failures
- **Bangkok Bank of Commerce:** Taken over by Thailand's finance ministry in mid-May 1996/early 1997 to prevent bankruptcy; emblematic of concentrated domestic real estate and connected lending
- **Peregrine Investments Holdings (Hong Kong):** Asia's largest private investment bank collapsed in January 1998. Had US$260 million (one-third of its capital) tied up in a single counterparty -- **Steady Safe**, an Indonesian taxi company connected to the Suharto family. An additional $400 million was concentrated in other Indonesian debt securities. When the rupiah collapsed from 2,400 to over 8,000 to the dollar, Steady Safe could never repay, and Peregrine was forced into liquidation

## 3. Irish Banking Crisis (2008-2010)

### Anglo Irish Bank - Property Lending Concentration
- Anglo was a **monoline bank** with **80-90% of its loan book** related to property investment activities
- Loans surged from EUR 23.7 billion in September 2004 to EUR 72.2 billion in September 2008 -- tripling in four years
- Exposure was concentrated among a small number of property developers without adequate risk mitigants
- Anglo became the "flagbearer" of high-growth, high-concentration strategies; other banks (AIB, BOI) then adopted similar models in a competitive response

### The Nyberg Report (2011) - "Misjudging Risk"
- Found widespread **herding** between institutions and **groupthink** within them, reinforced by a belief in efficient financial markets
- Banks followed the "Anglo model" of concentrated commercial real estate lending with little evidence of original critical risk analysis
- Loan-to-deposit ratios by 2008 ranged between 130-175% across the six covered banks
- Bank executives feared that failing to follow Anglo's expansion strategy would result in loss of customers, declining bank value, and potential takeover
- Full title: *Misjudging Risk: Causes of the Systemic Banking Crisis in Ireland* (Commission of Investigation, March 2011)

### The Honohan Report (2010)
- Found **excessive reliance on a regulatory philosophy that implicitly trusted well-governed banks**, emphasizing process over outcomes and downplaying quantification of risk
- Supervisors did not accumulate enough relevant information; communication between macroeconomic specialists and micro-prudential supervisors was not effective
- Financial stability reporting by the Central Bank **failed to warn** how severe the emerging risks to bank soundness were
- Led to the 2010 Central Bank Reform Act -- a shift to assertive, risk-based supervision

### Total Bailout Cost
- **EUR 64 billion** in gross bank recapitalization commitments
- Anglo Irish Bank: EUR 34.7 billion (55% of total); AIB: EUR 20.7 billion (33%)
- NAMA acquired EUR 74 billion in nominal loans for EUR 32 billion (reflecting property value collapse)
- **Net cost to taxpayers by 2021: EUR 45.7 billion** (per Comptroller and Auditor General)

## 4. Spanish Banking Crisis / Bankia (2012)

### Cajas Concentration in Property/Construction
- Savings banks (cajas) lent heavily to real estate developers and construction firms. Loans to these sectors reached approximately EUR 500 billion by end of 2008 -- equivalent to nearly half of Spain's GDP
- Individual cajas had real estate loan concentrations ranging from just over 10% to almost 50% of their loan books at the 2006 peak
- NPL rates in property developer and construction lending surged from 0.6% in 2007 to over 25% after the bust
- The banking sector set aside EUR 105 billion since 2008 to absorb bad property loans, but total bad property loans exceeded EUR 170 billion

### Bankia Bailout
- Bankia was formed in December 2010 from the merger of seven cajas (including Caja Madrid)
- **Nationalized on 9 May 2012**; announced it required a **EUR 23.5 billion bailout** to cover losses from failed mortgages (commonly cited as EUR 22.4 billion in recapitalization)
- The three most problematic cajas (Bankia, CatalunyaCaixa, Novagalicia) had combined capital deficits of EUR 54 billion -- over 5% of Spain's GDP
- In June 2012, Spain requested up to EUR 100 billion in ESM assistance; EUR 39.47 billion was ultimately disbursed

### Bank of Spain Supervisory Failures
- Supervisory responsibilities were divided between the Bank of Spain (BdE) and regional governments, complicating oversight
- The BIS/FSI report concludes the supervisor, confronted with politically connected ex-politicians running the cajas, chose to look the other way
- Many cajas were subject to little market discipline, poor governance, and unsustainable business models characterized by concentrated real estate exposure and dependence on unstable wholesale funding
- Starting in 2009, Spanish authorities introduced reforms that proved insufficient; only the mid-2012 ESM request triggered deeper reform

## 5. Silicon Valley Bank (2023)

### HTM Securities Portfolio Concentration
- At year-end 2022, SVB held a **$91.3 billion held-to-maturity (HTM) portfolio**
- Unrealized losses on HTM securities exceeded **$15 billion** as of year-end 2022 (due to sustained interest rate increases)
- SVB invested the VC deposit surge into long-dated fixed-income securities, creating a massive duration mismatch
- The bank removed its interest rate hedges as rates began to rise

### Uninsured Deposit Concentration
- **~94% of SVBFG's total deposits were uninsured** as of year-end 2022
- VC-backed companies accounted for **more than half** of SVBFG's deposits; off-balance-sheet client funds were even more concentrated in the same client group
- SVB banked nearly half of all US venture-backed technology and life sciences companies, and ~50% of all startups
- SVB custodied assets for 1,074 PE/VC funds, managing portions of 5,994 funds by year-end 2021
- The bank run was fueled by social media and SVB's concentrated VC network, which withdrew deposits with unprecedented speed

### Fed Vice Chair Barr's Report (April 28, 2023)
- Found SVB was "a highly vulnerable firm" with a "highly concentrated business model" and reliance on uninsured deposits
- **Three supervisory failures identified:** (1) supervisors did not ensure SVB fixed identified problems quickly enough; (2) agency staff delayed action to gather more evidence even as weaknesses were clear; (3) insufficient preparation for rising interest rates
- The 2018-era Tailoring Rules resulted in lower supervisory and regulatory requirements for SVB, which may have contributed to inadequate resilience
- Report title: *Review of the Federal Reserve's Supervision and Regulation of Silicon Valley Bank*

### FDIC OIG Report & Costs
- The Fed OIG Material Loss Review (September 2023) confirmed the material loss to the Deposit Insurance Fund of approximately **$20 billion** (including ~$18 billion to cover uninsured deposits)
- Of this, **$16.3 billion** was attributable to the protection of uninsured depositors through the systemic risk exception
- The FDIC collected this through a special assessment on banks over eight quarterly periods beginning in 2024

## 6. The Cycle Pattern

### Reinhart & Rogoff - "This Time Is Different"
- Covering 66 countries across five continents and eight centuries of financial data, they document that financial crises **strike with surprisingly consistent frequency, duration, and ferocity** and cluster in waves
- The incidence of banking crises is roughly similar for advanced and emerging economies (the post-WWII period is the main exception for wealthy countries)
- Three years after a banking crisis, central government debt increases by an average of 86%
- Real estate price cycles around banking crises average 4-6 year busts
- The core thesis: the belief that "this time is different" is a persistent, dangerous fallacy -- every generation of financiers and policymakers believes structural reforms have eliminated the risk of crisis

### Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis
- Core thesis: **"Stability is destabilizing"** -- prolonged periods of financial stability encourage progressively riskier behavior
- Three categories of financial posture: (1) **Hedge finance** (cash flows cover all obligations), (2) **Speculative finance** (cash flows cover interest but not principal -- must roll over debt), (3) **Ponzi finance** (cash flows cover neither -- dependent on asset appreciation)
- During stable periods, the economy drifts from hedge to speculative to Ponzi finance as confidence builds and risk premiums compress
- When Ponzi financing becomes sufficiently widespread, the inevitable disillusionment triggers systemic collapse
- Published as Levy Economics Institute Working Paper No. 74 (May 1992)

### Why Concentration Builds During Stability
- The Nyberg Report documented the mechanism empirically: banks follow peer institutions in "herding" fashion, with executives fearing loss of market share, declining value, or takeover if they deviate from the prevailing strategy
- The FDIC (Gruenberg, January 2025) identified recurring common causes across three crisis periods: interest rate risk, asset and deposit concentrations, leverage, rapid growth, inadequate capital, poorly understood new products, poor management, and failures of supervision
- Each crisis follows a remarkably similar pattern: new opportunities emerge, concentration builds during the good years, risk identification processes fail to escalate warnings, and supervisors delay action until collapse is imminent

## 7. Regulatory Framework on Concentration

### BCBS Large Exposures Framework
- **1991 Original (BCBS 107):** First Basel Committee guidance on measuring and controlling large credit exposures, setting a 25% of capital limit to a single counterparty. However, inconsistent implementation across jurisdictions due to varying definitions of exposure, capital, and numerical limits
- **2014 Revised (BCBS 283):** Superseded the 1991 standard, effective 1 January 2019. Sets an exposure limit of **25% of Tier 1 capital** to a single counterparty or group of connected counterparties. A tighter **15% of Tier 1** limit applies between G-SIBs. Requires aggregation of exposures to economically connected counterparties

### CRR Article 395 - EU Large Exposures Limits
- An institution shall not incur an exposure to a client or group of connected clients exceeding **25% of Tier 1 capital**
- Where EUR 150 million exceeds 25% of Tier 1, a "reasonable limit" applies, capped at 100% of Tier 1 capital
- Competent authorities may set lower thresholds, informing EBA and the Commission

### Key Limitations of the Large Exposures Framework
- The framework addresses **single-name concentration** (exposure to individual counterparties) but does not effectively capture **sectoral concentration** -- exactly the type of concentration that caused each of the crises documented above
- SVB's problem was not excessive exposure to any single borrower; it was the concentration of its entire deposit base in one sector (VC/tech) and its entire investment portfolio in one asset class (long-duration government bonds)
- Anglo Irish Bank's problem was not any single developer but the aggregation of 80-90% of its loan book in one sector (commercial property)
- The cajas' problem was system-wide herding into property/construction lending
- Large exposures limits do not address funding concentration, deposit concentration, or asset-class concentration

### Post-SVB Regulatory Developments (2024-2026)
- **CRR 3 / CRD 6:** Published June-July 2024; most CRR 3 provisions applying from 1 January 2025, CRD 6 from early 2026. Strengthens prudential requirements but does not fundamentally redesign concentration risk frameworks
- **EBA IRRBB Framework:** Enhanced guidelines on interest rate risk in the banking book (IRRBB) and credit spread risk in the banking book (CSRBB), fully applicable since December 2023, with a heatmap for monitoring published in January 2024
- **FDIC Supervisory Updates (August 2023):** Updated procedures requiring examiners to escalate supervisory concerns that are repeated or uncorrected at the end of an examination cycle
- **Basel Committee 2025-26 Work Programme:** Key themes include Basel III implementation, risk assessment and safeguarding resilience, but no specific overhaul of sectoral concentration frameworks announced
- **EBA 2025-2027 Work Programme:** Includes review of the EU regulatory and supervisory framework for banks, with emphasis on simplifying capital stacking orders but no explicit concentration risk redesign

The Reinhart-Rogoff finding is that these crises cluster and recur because each post-crisis regulatory reform addresses the previous crisis's specific mechanism while leaving the broader phenomenon of concentration buildup -- enabled by herding, groupthink, and supervisory complacency during stable periods -- fundamentally unaddressed.
