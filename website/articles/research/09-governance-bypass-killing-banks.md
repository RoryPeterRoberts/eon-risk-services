# Research File: Article 9 — Barings to Credit Suisse: Why Governance Bypass Keeps Killing Banks

## Article Thesis

The recurring failure mode where risks are identified and escalated through proper channels, but governance structures fail to act on them — or actively suppress them. Governance Bypass, Cultural Suppression, and Information Asymmetry share a common root: the risk was identified or identifiable, but the institutional mechanisms for acting on that identification were broken.

### 1. HBOS — Paul Moore (2004–2008)

**Source:** Chapter 3 (primary), Chapter 16

**The internal warning/identification:**
Paul Moore, appointed Group Head of Regulatory Risk at HBOS in 2004, identified that the corporate banking division under Peter Cummings was pursuing an aggressive commercial real estate and leveraged lending strategy creating dangerous concentrations. Individual loan approvals were bypassing normal credit processes. Growth targets were overriding risk limits.

**Who it went to:**
Moore documented the concern and escalated it to the Board.

**What the response was:**
The Board's response was not to investigate the risk. It was to remove the person who had identified it. Moore was dismissed in 2005. The official reason was restructuring. The actual reason, as Moore later testified to a Parliamentary inquiry, was that his risk identification work was inconvenient. He was telling the Board something it did not want to hear, about a division generating the profits the Board wanted to see.

**The eventual loss:**
HBOS collapsed. Corporate banking division losses exceeded GBP 10 billion. Emergency rescue merger with Lloyds TSB required. Peter Cummings banned by FSA.

**Governance failure type:**
Pure governance bypass — the identification function worked but the governance structure around it was configured to suppress rather than escalate. The escalation path was blocked, the Board received filtered information, the person responsible for independent challenge was removed, and the cultural incentive to suppress uncomfortable findings overwhelmed the structural obligation to act on them.

### 2. Standard Chartered — Sanctions (2012)

**Source:** Chapter 1, Chapter 3, Chapter 16, Chapter 9

**The internal warning/identification:**
The compliance function identified the sanctions risk associated with processing approximately $250 billion in transactions for Iranian clients. Compliance officers flagged the risk at the operational level.

**Who it went to:**
Escalated through the compliance function to senior management in London.

**What the response was:**
Senior management in London overruled the compliance function, treating the Iranian business as commercially important. Risk was identified. Governance failed to act on the identification.

**The eventual loss:**
$667 million combined fine (NYDFS, OFAC, DOJ, FCA, Federal Reserve, accumulated 2012-2019) plus compliance monitorship. Regulatory impact constrained the institution's strategic options for years beyond the fine itself.

**Governance failure type:**
Governance bypass — operational independence existed but was overridden. The risk identification process worked at the first-line/second-line level but was suppressed by the people generating the revenue. Illustrates what happens when structural independence exists on paper but governance fails to protect the identification output.

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch3-11]: NYDFS Consent Order, 6 August 2012 ($340 million); DOJ/Manhattan DA Deferred Prosecution Agreement, 10 December 2012 ($327 million).
- [^ch16-10]: Combined penalties across NYDFS, OFAC, DOJ, FCA, and Federal Reserve totalling $667 million, accumulated 2012-2019.

### 3. Barings — Nick Leeson (1995)

**Source:** Chapter 1

**The internal warning/identification:**
Internal audit identified the segregation-of-duties violation in Nick Leeson's Singapore operation in 1994. Leeson controlled both trading and settlement functions.

**Who it went to:**
Internal audit report to management.

**What the response was:**
Management did not act on the internal audit finding.

**The eventual loss:**
Bank collapsed entirely (GBP 827 million loss). Barings ceased to exist as an independent institution.

**Governance failure type:**
Classic governance bypass — risk was identified at the working level through proper internal audit channels but never resulted in corrective action by the people who could act.

**Footnote citations:**

### 4. Lehman Brothers — CRO Marginalised (2008)

**Source:** Chapter 1, Chapter 16

**The internal warning/identification:**
The Chief Risk Officer raised increasingly urgent warnings about leverage and mortgage exposure. The Repo 105 programme (used to temporarily remove assets from the balance sheet at reporting dates) was known to a small group of executives.

**Who it went to:**
CRO escalated to senior management. Repo 105 information was not shared with the risk function or the Board in a way that allowed assessment of implications.

**What the response was:**
The CRO was first marginalised and then replaced. Internal critics of the leverage strategy were marginalised. Information about Repo 105 was confined to a small executive group.

**The eventual loss:**
Lehman Brothers collapsed — largest bankruptcy in US history ($613 billion in assets).

**Governance failure type:**
Combination of governance bypass (CRO removed), cultural suppression (internal critics marginalised), and information asymmetry (Repo 105 known only to small group, not shared with risk function or Board).

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch1-11]: Report of the Examiner (Anton R. Valukas) in the Chapter 11 proceedings of Lehman Brothers Holdings Inc., 11 March 2010. Repo 105 examined in Sections III.A.4 and III.A.5.

### 5. Wells Fargo — Unauthorised Accounts (2016)

**Source:** Chapter 1, Chapter 3, Chapter 9

**The internal warning/identification:**
Front-line employees knew that aggressive cross-selling targets were driving fraudulent account openings. Whistleblower complaints were filed. An estimated 3.5 million potentially unauthorised accounts were opened.

**Who it went to:**
Complaints were filed through internal channels.

**What the response was:**
Complaints were treated as HR issues — employee dissatisfaction — rather than risk events. The sales culture was so dominant that the risk function could not challenge it. The governance structure absorbed the complaints rather than escalating them through the risk identification process.

**The eventual loss:**
$3 billion DOJ settlement. Federal Reserve asset-growth restriction imposed in 2018 (not lifted until 2025). CEO resignation. Sustained Congressional scrutiny. Customer attrition.

**Governance failure type:**
Cultural suppression — the institutional culture treated risk identifiers as obstacles rather than performing an essential function. Front-line risk observations were channelled into HR rather than the risk identification process.

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch1-5]: Wells Fargo independent directors' Sales Practices Investigation Report, April 2017; CFPB Consent Order No. 2016-CFPB-0015, 8 September 2016.
- [^ch3-8]: Wells Fargo Board of Directors, Sales Practices Investigation Report, 10 April 2017 (3.5 million accounts figure).
- [^ch3-9]: DOJ press release 20-146, 21 February 2020 ($3 billion settlement).
- [^ch3-10]: Federal Reserve enforcement action, 2 February 2018 (asset cap lifted 3 June 2025).

### 6. Credit Suisse — Archegos (2021)

**Source:** Chapter 1, Chapter 6

**The internal warning/identification:**
No structured workshop methodology existed that would have asked the right questions about concentrated family office leverage across multiple prime brokers. The risk of a single client holding equivalent or larger positions across every prime broker was not surfaced. Margin calls were rising. Position sizes were growing. Concentration in single names was visible on each desk's books.

**Who it went to:**
Information existed within each institution but was never aggregated or escalated. The workshop process did not include prompts designed to surface cross-counterparty, cross-institution concentration risk.

**What the response was:**
The risk culture was described by regulators as one where commercial considerations consistently overrode risk management. No one asked the question that mattered: what is this client's aggregate position across all brokers?

**The eventual loss:**
$5.5 billion loss to Credit Suisse alone. Approximately $10 billion in net losses across all prime brokers. Contributed materially to Credit Suisse's eventual demise.

**Governance failure type:**
Combination of governance bypass (commercial considerations overriding risk), information asymmetry (no visibility into aggregate client positions), and process failure (no SWIFT-style questioning methodology to force the right questions).

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch1-6]: Paul, Weiss report on Archegos Capital Management, 29 July 2021. $20 billion in concentrated equity positions.
- [^ch6-1]: Credit Suisse Special Committee Report (Paul, Weiss), 29 July 2021. $5.5 billion loss.
- [^ch6-6]: SEC Charges Archegos Capital Management, 27 April 2022. Approximately $10 billion net losses across counterparties.

### 7. Credit Suisse — Mozambique Hidden Debt

**Source:** Chapter 16

**The internal warning/identification:**
The risk identification framework never had the chance to engage. Credit Suisse bankers arranged $2 billion in hidden loans to Mozambican state entities — loans involving military-connected borrowers, kickback payments, and secrecy requirements. Red flags included: involvement of intermediaries, military nature of borrowers, secrecy requirements, unusual fee structures.

**Who it went to:**
The sovereign lending risk identification process focused on credit analysis of the Mozambican state. It did not identify the corruption risk embedded in the transaction structure.

**What the response was:**
No structured new-product approval process surfaced the red flags. The Mozambique loans were a new structure that was never assessed through risk identification.

**The eventual loss:**
$475 million coordinated global settlement across SEC, DOJ, and FCA. One of several scandals contributing to Credit Suisse's eventual demise.

**Governance failure type:**
Governance bypass through structural absence — the risk identification framework was bypassed because no event-driven trigger or new-product assessment process existed to catch the transaction. Individual bankers could structure transactions that bypassed every control.

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch16-11]: DOJ press release, 3 January 2019 (indictments); SEC Litigation Release No. 25252, 19 October 2021.
- [^ch16-12]: DOJ press release, 19 October 2021 ($475 million settlement). SEC Order No. 3-20625. FCA Final Notice 2021.

### 8. Banca Monte dei Paschi di Siena

**Source:** Chapter 3

**The internal warning/identification:**
The governance failure went deeper than suppression — senior management actively used complex derivative transactions ("Santorini" and "Alexandria") to conceal hundreds of millions in losses (approximately EUR 730 million).

**Who it went to:**
The escalation path was compromised because the people who should have been receiving the escalation were the ones generating the risk.

**What the response was:**
Management was complicit. Normal governance channels were captured by the people creating the exposure.

**The eventual loss:**
Approximately EUR 730 million in concealed losses. Criminal convictions of former CEO (Giuseppe Mussari) and former GM (Antonio Vigni).

**Governance failure type:**
Governance bypass at its most extreme — when management itself is complicit, the governance structure must provide an alternative path (direct Board access, independent audit, or external whistleblowing) that bypasses the compromised chain entirely.

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch3-12]: Tribunale di Milano, sentencing judgment, October 2014 (Santorini and Alexandria transactions). Bank of Italy supervisory findings; ECB Comprehensive Assessment, October 2014.

### 9. Cooperative Bank — Board Competence Failure (2009)

**Source:** Chapter 3

**The internal warning/identification:**
When the Co-op Bank attempted to acquire Britannia Building Society in 2009, the merger due diligence failed to identify the true scale of Britannia's commercial property loan impairments.

**Who it went to:**
The Board was responsible for oversight and challenge of the due diligence.

**What the response was:**
The Co-op's mutual governance model had placed individuals without banking expertise in Board oversight roles. The chairman had no financial services background. The Board could not challenge what it could not understand.

**The eventual loss:**
GBP 1.5 billion rescue. Bondholder bail-in. Loss of the bank's independence.

**Governance failure type:**
Governance failure through Board incompetence — not suppression but inability. Board competence in risk matters is a governance prerequisite, not a nice-to-have.

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch3-7]: The Kelly Review (Sir Christopher Kelly), Failings in Management and Governance, 30 April 2014.

### 10. Societe Generale — Jerome Kerviel (2008)

**Source:** Chapter 3, Chapter 7

**The internal warning/identification:**
The control framework had defined roles and responsibilities. However, the risk identification process did not contemplate a trader who knew both sides of the control chain (Kerviel had previously worked in the back office and understood precisely which controls to circumvent and when).

**Who it went to:**
The assumptions underlying control effectiveness were embedded in the governance framework — no one challenged the assumption that a junior trader could not systematically exploit the full front-to-back verification process.

**What the response was:**
The governance framework failed because the assumptions underlying its role definitions proved wrong. No mechanism existed for identifying this as a risk.

**The eventual loss:**
EUR 4.9 billion. EUR 50 billion in hidden positions on European equity index futures through fictitious offsetting trades.

**Governance failure type:**
Governance failure through embedded assumptions — the risk identification process assumed certain risk scenarios were implausible. The process did not ask: what if someone in the front office has detailed knowledge of back-office procedures?

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch3-14]: Societe Generale Mission Green Report, 20 May 2008; Inspection Generale des Finances report, February 2008. Kerviel convicted 5 October 2010. EUR 4.9 billion loss announced 24 January 2008.

### 11. Wirecard — Inverted Identification Ecosystem (2020)

**Source:** Chapter 6

**The internal warning/identification:**
Short-sellers and Financial Times journalists identified the fraud through forensic analysis of publicly available data. Specific, documented concerns about fabricated revenues and phantom bank balances were raised externally from 2015 onward.

**Who it went to:**
Concerns were raised publicly and to the German financial regulator BaFin.

**What the response was:**
BaFin responded not by investigating Wirecard but by filing criminal complaints against the Financial Times journalists and imposing a two-month short-selling ban on Wirecard shares. The regulator defended the fraudster and prosecuted those who identified the risk.

**The eventual loss:**
EUR 1.9 billion in fabricated cash balances. Complete corporate collapse and insolvency.

**Governance failure type:**
The most extreme form of governance inversion — internal identification was captured by the fraud itself, and the external regulatory governance actively suppressed external identifiers.

### 12. AIB/Allfirst — John Rusnak (2002)

**Source:** Chapter 7

**The internal warning/identification:**
Allfirst operated with significant autonomy from AIB Dublin headquarters. Weak back-office confirmation processes for FX options were exploitable. Rusnak created fictitious option trades to conceal $691 million in losses over several years.

**Who it went to:**
AIB's central risk function relied on local management assurances that risks were being properly identified and controlled. No standardised bottom-up identification process with central oversight existed.

**What the response was:**
Local management assurances were accepted without independent verification. The subsidiary's risk reporting used locally developed formats that did not capture granular driver and control information.

**The eventual loss:**
$691 million. Allfirst subsequently sold to M&T Bank. Rusnak sentenced to 7.5 years.

**Governance failure type:**
Governance gap through geographic separation — the risk identification process had no mechanism for central challenge of subsidiary assurances. No standardised template, no independent control effectiveness assessment, no specialist traded risk assessment integrated into central process.

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch7-6]: AIB public disclosure, February 2002; Ludwig Report (Promontory Financial/Wachtell Lipton), 12 March 2002.
- [^ch7-7]: US v Rusnak, Case No. 1:02-cr-00049, 7.5 years.
- [^ch7-8]: AIB sale of Allfirst to M&T Bank, April 2003.

### 13. UBS — Kweku Adoboli (2011)

**Source:** Chapter 7

**The internal warning/identification:**
The RCSA for the Delta One desk had not identified that the control framework assumed trade authenticity rather than testing it. The control assessment said reconciliation processes existed but did not ask whether those processes could detect deliberately fabricated trades. Occurred AFTER UBS's $37 billion structured credit losses, AFTER the Swiss National Bank SPV bailout, and AFTER a comprehensive risk management overhaul.

**Who it went to:**
Internal RCSA process and control assessment.

**What the response was:**
The bottom-up identification process still contained the same fundamental gap: fictitious trades booked against genuine positions were not independently verified. The RCSA that UBS conducted had not identified that its control framework assumed trade authenticity.

**The eventual loss:**
$2.3 billion. FSA fine of GBP 29.7 million for systems and controls failures. Accelerated UBS's strategic exit from large parts of investment banking.

**Governance failure type:**
Governance failure through RCSA compliance theatre — the process existed but assessed controls against design specification rather than against determined circumvention. External loss data (Kerviel, Rusnak) was available but not incorporated into the RCSA challenge process.

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch7-9]: UBS press release, 15 September 2011; FSA Final Notice, 26 November 2012.
- [^ch7-10]: Southwark Crown Court, R v Kweku Adoboli, 20 November 2012.
- [^ch7-13]: FSA Final Notice to UBS AG, 26 November 2012 (GBP 29.7 million penalty).

### 14. Silicon Valley Bank (2023)

**Source:** Chapter 13 (primary), Chapter 16

**The internal warning/identification:**
Interest rate risk in the banking book was accumulating for two years in plain sight. $91 billion HTM portfolio. Unrealised losses exceeded $15 billion (more than total equity). Every Federal Reserve rate increase was a publicly available signal.

**Who it went to:**
The CRO departed in April 2022 and was not replaced until January 2023 — eight months during the most consequential monetary policy shift in a generation. Risk committee met infrequently.

**What the response was:**
The ongoing cycle — quarterly re-identification, event-driven updates, continuous recalibration — had degraded to the point of irrelevance. Internal risk reports continued to circulate but active re-identification did not occur.

**The eventual loss:**
$209 billion bank failure. Largest American bank failure since Washington Mutual. Collapsed in approximately 36 hours.

**Governance failure type:**
Governance failure through ongoing cycle degradation and CRO vacancy. The governance mechanism (Phase 6) that should have kept risk identification current stopped functioning during the most critical period.

### 15. Bear Stearns — Hedge Fund Warning Ignored (2007-2008)

**Source:** Chapter 9, Chapter 13, Chapter 16

**The internal warning/identification:**
In June 2007, two Bear Stearns hedge funds collapsed from CDO exposure. The parent bank held positions similar to those on the collapsed funds' balance sheets.

**Who it went to:**
This was visible to senior management as a direct, explicit warning about the parent bank's own retained structured credit positions.

**What the response was:**
The parent bank treated this as an isolated subsidiary event — a contained loss that did not require re-examination of the bank's own balance sheet. The methodology's event-driven trigger framework defines a material loss or near-miss as the first trigger requiring immediate re-identification.

**The eventual loss:**
Bear Stearns collapsed nine months later from the same concentration risk. Acquired by JPMorgan Chase at $2/share (later revised to $10/share) in March 2008. Speed of onset was Immediate — went from operational to acquired in days.

**Governance failure type:**
Complacency and event-driven trigger failure — a direct, unambiguous warning from a subsidiary was treated as isolated rather than as an event-driven trigger requiring immediate parent-level re-identification.

**Footnote citations:**
- [^ch1-10]: FCIC Report, January 2011, pp. 238-242.
- [^ch9-14]: FCIC Report, pp. 280-291 (rapid loss of counterparty confidence, March 2008).

### 16. National Australia Bank — Peer Event Ignored (2020)

**Source:** Chapter 13

**The internal warning/identification:**
In 2018, Commonwealth Bank of Australia received a record AUD 700 million AUSTRAC fine for failing to report over 53,000 suspicious cash transactions. This was a publicly available event-driven trigger for every institution operating similar technology.

**Who it went to:**
The event was publicly available to NAB's risk function and management.

NAB failed to identify that its own transaction monitoring systems had analogous gaps. A peer institution's public, catastrophic failure did not trigger re-identification of the same risk within NAB's own operations.

**Governance failure type:**
Event-driven trigger failure — material events at peer institutions must be treated as triggers for internal re-identification. NAB failed to ask the critical question: what specific aspects of our risk landscape share the structural characteristics that produced that failure?

## METHODOLOGY CONTENT

---

### Three Forms of Independence

**Source:** Chapter 3

1. **Structural independence.** The Risk Identification Lead and the CRO function must not report to the business. The CRO should report to the CEO with a direct line to the Board Risk Committee, or preferably report directly to the Board with administrative reporting to the CEO. The Risk Identification Lead reports to the CRO. At no point should a business unit head have the authority to overrule, modify, or suppress a risk identification finding.

2. **Operational independence.** The risk identification process must be able to operate without requiring business unit cooperation to function. The Risk Identification Lead must have access to data, systems, and information independently of business unit gatekeepers. If the only way to assess a business unit's risks is through information the business unit chooses to provide, the process is not independent — it is captive.

3. **Intellectual independence.** The people conducting risk identification must be free to reach conclusions that the business does not agree with. The social and career pressures to produce risk assessments that align with management's preferred narrative are enormous. The governance structure must protect the Risk Identification Lead's ability to do their job without career consequence.

---

### Risk Identification Lead Mandate

**Source:** Chapter 3, Chapter 6

The Risk Identification Lead:
- Designs and executes the communication plan for each cycle
- Coordinates top-down SWIFT workshops and Delphi exercises
- Manages the bottom-up template completion across all business units
- Drives the reconciliation between top-down and bottom-up inputs (where most difficult governance conversations happen)
- Maintains the central risk inventory
- Conducts the annual lessons-learned review
- Ensures specialist sub-processes are integrated into the unified framework
- Has the mandate to record risks in the inventory regardless of business line objections, with escalation to CRO and ultimately the Board
- Must have clear mandate from the CRO, visible support from the Board Risk Committee, and willingness to have uncomfortable conversations with senior people

The role requires both technical expertise and political skill — understanding the methodology in detail while being able to facilitate workshops with reluctant senior bankers, challenge business units diplomatically but firmly, and navigate institutional dynamics.

---

### Principal Risk Report Flow

**Source:** Chapter 3

The Board Risk Committee receives a comprehensive risk report at each meeting containing:
- All material risks with current residual risk scores
- The consequence dimension driving each impact score
- Trend direction for each risk
- Risks approaching or breaching appetite
- New or emerging risks
- Risks removed or reclassified
- KRI dashboard
- Data quality distribution across the portfolio
- Enterprise portfolio view showing aggregate position against appetite
- Process performance indicators showing whether identification process is functioning

This report is drawn directly from the risk inventory. Every data point is traceable. Every score has a documented basis. The Board reads the risk landscape, not an interpretation of it.

**Critical design features:**
- Direct access: CRO and Risk Identification Lead must have standing access to Board Risk Committee without requiring CEO permission
- Anonymous reporting channels feed into risk identification process (not HR/compliance alone)
- Challenge sessions built into reconciliation process — structured challenge, not consensus-building

---

### Event-Driven Triggers

**Source:** Chapter 13

Six categories of event trigger immediate risk identification update outside any regular cycle:

1. **Material loss event or near-miss** — requires both root cause analysis (backward-looking) and forward-looking assessment of consequences
2. **Significant change in external environment** — regulatory announcements, macroeconomic shifts, geopolitical events, technology disruptions
3. **New business, product, or market entry** — risk identification is a precondition for launch, not a retrospective exercise (per EU AMLD6)
4. **Acquisition, divestiture, or restructuring** — must recalculate enterprise portfolio view for combined entity
5. **Material control failure identified by internal audit** — changes residual risk score for every risk depending on that control
6. **Material outsourcing arrangement change** — entered into, significantly changed, or terminated

Key principle: Material events at peer institutions must be treated as event-driven triggers for internal re-identification. The question is not "could that happen to us?" but "what specific aspects of our risk landscape share the structural characteristics that produced that failure?"

---

### Governance Framework Requirements

**Source:** Chapter 3

**Seven roles that must be defined with clarity:**
1. Board Risk Committee — approves taxonomy/appetite, receives principal risk report, challenges outputs, ensures independence
2. Chief Risk Officer — owns process end-to-end, protects integrity from commercial pressure
3. Risk Identification Lead — operational heart, execution and challenge role
4. Business Unit Heads — own risks assigned to their area, participate in both tracks
5. Risk Assessors — designated individuals at all levels, report to BU head and Risk Identification Lead
6. Risk Owners — named individuals (not committees) accountable for each risk
7. Front-Line Employees — earliest sensors; require clear channels, escalation paths independent of immediate supervisor, protection from retaliation

**Process frequency requirements (four frequencies):**
1. Annual full re-identification — complete process from scratch
2. Quarterly re-identification — active re-identification (not merely re-assessment), consistent with Fed SR 15-18 (CCAR)
3. Monthly KRI monitoring — early warning system, breaches trigger investigation
4. Event-driven updates — immediate response to six trigger categories

**The governance test (from Chapter 3):**
Every governance structure looks adequate on paper. The real test is what happens when the process produces a finding that someone does not want to hear.

---

### Structured Escalation Framework

**Source:** Chapter 3

The most critical governance question: how do findings reach the people who can act on them — without being filtered by the people who generated them?

Four reinforcing mechanisms:
1. **Principal risk report** — drawn directly from inventory, traceable, not filtered through management
2. **Direct access** — CRO and Risk Identification Lead have standing access to Board Risk Committee without CEO permission. Rarely used but its existence changes behaviour.
3. **Anonymous reporting** — channels for all employees, feeding into risk identification process (not just HR), reviewed by Risk Identification Lead, material concerns escalated to CRO and Board
4. **Challenge sessions** — formal reconciliation debates, structured to surface disagreements and test assumptions, not consensus-building exercises

## CROSS-CUTTING THEMES

**1. The Identification-Action Gap:**
In every case study above, the problem was not the absence of risk identification but the failure of governance mechanisms to protect the identification output and ensure it resulted in action. The risks were identifiable. Many were actually identified. The governance failed.

**2. Revenue Protection as Suppression Mechanism:**
Standard Chartered (Iranian business commercially important), HBOS (corporate division generating profits the Board wanted), Credit Suisse/Archegos (commercial considerations consistently overriding risk), Wells Fargo (cross-selling targets driving culture) — the pattern is consistent: revenue generation creates the political power to suppress risk findings.

**3. The Career Risk Paradox:**
The people who identify risks face career consequences (Moore fired at HBOS, Lehman CRO marginalised and replaced). The methodology's response: structural independence, direct Board access, anonymous reporting, and a governance structure that makes suppression visible rather than invisible.

**4. Compliance Theatre as Governance Bypass:**
A documented record showing identification occurred — when it has not — is more dangerous than no process at all, because it provides false assurance. Credit Suisse bottom-up templates unchanged for three years. UBS RCSA that did not test against circumvention. SVB risk reports circulating while active re-identification had stopped.

**5. The Recurring Nature:**
The same governance failures repeat across decades, jurisdictions, and institution types. From Barings (1995) to Credit Suisse (2023), the pattern is identical: risk identified, governance fails to act, institution suffers catastrophic loss. The failure mode is effectively predictable.

## 1. Barings Bank (1995)

### The Internal Warning

In August 1994, Barings' Internal Audit department conducted an audit of Barings Futures Singapore (BFS) covering the period 19 July to 1 August 1994. The audit report explicitly identified a critical control weakness: Nick Leeson controlled both the front office (trading) and the back office (settlement) at BFS. The report recommended that "responsibility for the two offices should be separated to reduce the possibility of error and fraud." A further audit in October 1994 raised similar concerns.

### Who Received It

The August 1994 internal audit report was circulated to Barings' senior management in London, including the heads of the settlements and compliance functions.

### The Response

Barings management failed to implement the recommendation. The bank chose to retain Leeson's dual role because of his perceived experience and -- critically -- because of the profits he was generating. The 88888 error account, which Leeson used to conceal losses, was never investigated despite its anomalous balance. Concerns also circulated in the wider market in January and February 1995 about Barings' very large positions on the Osaka Securities Exchange (OSE), but management's focus remained on Leeson's apparent profitability.

### The Loss

GBP 827 million (approximately USD 1.4 billion) -- twice Barings' available trading capital. The bank was declared insolvent on 26 February 1995 and subsequently sold to ING for GBP 1.

### Key Official Report

The Board of Banking Supervision inquiry, set up by the Chancellor of the Exchequer on 27 February 1995 and published 18 July 1995, concluded that the losses were caused by "unauthorised and concealed trading activities" and that "the true position was not noticed earlier because of serious failures of controls and managerial confusion within the Barings group."

## 2. Societe Generale / Jerome Kerviel (2008)

### The Internal Warnings

Between June 2006 and January 2008, Societe Generale's internal control systems generated at least **75 separate alerts** flagging anomalies in Kerviel's trading activity. These red flags were raised by accountants, risk-control officers, and compliance staff. The alerts included transactions that appeared to settle on a Saturday, trades where the counterparty was not named or listed as "pending," and trades that exceeded authorised limits.

More specifically, between July 2006 and September 2007 alone, internal control systems raised **24 alerts** when the value of Kerviel's trades exceeded authorised limits.

### Who Received Them

Alerts were directed to back/middle-office operations, the global equity derivatives department, the accounting group, and the group risk office. Kerviel's direct supervisor also received information about anomalous positions.

### The Response

The bank's risk monitoring unit attributed the anomalies to "recurrent problems with the way the trading software recorded operations" and asked Kerviel's superiors to ensure he did not exceed limits again. Staff did not systematically conduct in-depth investigations when warning flags were raised. The "Mission Green" report (the General Inspection department's internal investigation, commissioned 24 January 2008) found that Kerviel used a variety of concealment techniques -- lies, forged documents (including seven forged emails), and rapid cancellation or modification of anomalous transactions -- that exploited a lack of cross-functionality in the organisation of control functions.

### The Loss

EUR 4.9 billion (approximately USD 7.2 billion at the time), realised when SocGen unwound Kerviel's positions over three days beginning 21 January 2008. Kerviel's peak unauthorised exposure reached EUR 49.9 billion -- more than the bank's entire market capitalisation. The French Banking Commission fined Societe Generale EUR 4 million for control failures on 4 July 2008.

## 3. UBS / Kweku Adoboli (2011)

### The Control Failures

The UK Financial Services Authority (FSA) found that UBS had systemic weaknesses in its systems and controls that allowed Adoboli to conduct unauthorised trading over a period of more than three years (from at least late 2008). Specific failings included:

- The trade capture and processing system had significant deficiencies: trades could be booked to an internal counterparty without sufficient details; there were no effective methods to detect trades at material off-market prices; and there was a lack of integration between systems.
- Front office supervision was "poorly executed and ineffective."
- UBS failed to question the increasing revenue of Adoboli's desk and failed to ensure corresponding increases in controls.
- Risk limits were repeatedly breached without adequate challenge.

### Who Should Have Acted

UBS management at the Global Synthetic Equities (GSE) desk and the Investment Bank's risk and compliance functions. The FSA found failures at multiple levels of the organisation.

### The Response (or Lack Thereof)

The inadequate controls persisted for years. The FSA's Final Notice described persistent failures in supervision, trade booking, and position reconciliation. Revenue growth was celebrated rather than questioned.

### The Loss

USD 2.3 billion from unauthorised trading. The FSA fined UBS GBP 29.7 million (approximately USD 47.6 million) on 26 November 2012 -- the third-largest fine in the FSA's history at that time.

## 4. JPMorgan Chase -- London Whale (2012)

### The Internal Warning (and Suppression)

The US Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations released a 300-page bipartisan report on 15 March 2013 titled "JPMorgan Chase Whale Trades: A Case History of Derivatives Risks and Abuses." The investigation reviewed over 90,000 documents and conducted over 50 interviews. Key findings:

- As early as January 2012, the rapid growth of the Synthetic Credit Portfolio (SCP) in JPMorgan's Chief Investment Office (CIO) breached Value-at-Risk (VaR) limits.
- After a 4-day breach was reported to top bank officials including CEO Jamie Dimon, who approved a temporary limit increase, CIO employees then **hurriedly pushed through approval of a new VaR model that overnight dropped the CIO's purported risk by 50%.**
- The new model was implemented without a truly independent review, without the standard parallel run of old and new models during a probationary period, and with the analytics suite remaining untested.
- The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) was regularly informed of the risk limit breaches and was notified in advance that the CIO VaR model change was projected to drop results by 44%, yet raised no concerns.

### The Pattern of Governance Bypass

The Senate report documented that when the CIO's SCP breached five key risk limits, rather than reduce risky trading activities, JPMorgan either: (a) increased the limits; (b) changed the risk models that calculated risk; or (c) turned a blind eye to the breaches. Additionally, the CIO mismarked its trading book to hide losses and misinformed investors, regulators, and the public.

### The Loss

USD 6.2 billion in trading losses from the Synthetic Credit Portfolio.

## 5. Wells Fargo (2016)

### The Internal Warnings

Internal warnings at Wells Fargo were extensive and sustained over many years:

- **EthicsLine complaints:** From 2006 through 2014, nearly half of all EthicsLine complaints investigated by the Corporate Investigations unit related to sales integrity violations -- employees manipulating or misrepresenting sales to meet targets. By Q4 2009, Wells Fargo had received 667 EthicsLine cases and 242 related terminations/resignations connected to gaming of sales incentive programmes.
- **Employee retaliation:** Employees who reported concerns through the ethics hotline were fired. Bill Bado, a banker in Pennsylvania, refused to open phony accounts, called the ethics hotline, and emailed HR in September 2013 flagging unethical practices. He was terminated eight days later.
- **5,300 employees** were fired over the course of the scandal for sales integrity violations, yet the underlying business model and sales pressure persisted.
- **Customer complaints:** From December 2013 through September 2015, the bank received at least 5,000 customer complaints about lack of consent for account openings.

### Who Received the Warnings

The Shearman & Sterling independent investigation (April 2017) found that Carrie Tolstedt, head of the Community Bank division, and other Community Bank leaders were aware of sales practice problems but were "unwilling to change the sales model or recognise it as the root cause of the problem." They "resisted and impeded scrutiny or oversight from corporate risk management and the Board." Former Chairman and CEO John Stumpf was found to have been "too slow to investigate or critically challenge the sales practices."

### The Shearman & Sterling Report (10 April 2017)

A 110-page report produced by a committee of independent directors, assisted by Shearman & Sterling LLP. The investigation involved 100 interviews and review of over 35 million documents. Root cause finding: "The distortion of the Community Bank's sales culture and performance management system, which, when combined with aggressive sales management, created pressure on employees to sell unwanted or unneeded products to customers."

### Total Penalties and Settlements

- **September 2016:** USD 185 million (CFPB, OCC, City of Los Angeles)
- **February 2020:** USD 3 billion (DOJ/SEC global settlement for criminal and civil violations)
- **December 2022:** USD 3.7 billion (CFPB -- the largest fine in CFPB history, comprising USD 1.7 billion civil penalty plus USD 2 billion consumer redress, covering 16 million affected accounts)
- **Additional settlements:** USD 480 million (shareholder class action); USD 575 million (50-state Attorneys General); USD 1 billion (April 2018, CFPB/OCC for auto-loan and mortgage issues)
- **Cumulative total (scandal-related):** Approximately USD 7--8 billion in direct fines and settlements

## 6. Credit Suisse -- Archegos (2021)

### The Internal Risk Warnings

The Paul, Weiss Special Committee report (published 29 July 2021, based on 80+ interviews and 10+ million documents) documented a pattern of persistent and escalating internal risk warnings that were overridden or ignored by the prime brokerage business:

- **Margin erosion:** In 2019, Archegos asked Credit Suisse to materially lower its swap margins from the 15--25% initial margin range to 7.5%. Credit Suisse agreed.
- **Scenario limit breaches:** By 16 July 2020, Archegos had over USD 600 million in net scenario exposure -- more than 240% of the USD 250 million scenario limit. By 22 July 2020, this had jumped to USD 828 million (330% of the limit). **From that point on, Archegos remained in breach of its scenario limits virtually every week until its March 2021 default.**
- **Stress loss projections:** In February 2021, scenario analysis predicted stress losses in the range of USD 1.4 billion under two scenarios, significantly exceeding the entity's USD 800 million scenario limit.
- **Dynamic margining blocked:** Risk managers intended to ask Archegos for additional margin to reflect the increased credit risk, but were **prevented from doing so** as this was "deemed not to be in the interests of the bank" by those responsible for managing the relationship.
- **Temporary risk appetite increases:** Archegos was awarded several temporary bespoke risk appetite increases, including one in October 2020 of USD 900 million.

### Who Received the Warnings

The Investment Bank's Prime Services business heads and the second-line risk function. The report found failures across both the first and second lines of defence, as well as a lack of escalation to senior management and the board.

### The Response

The Paul, Weiss report concluded that "the business was focused on maximising short-term profits and failed to rein in and, indeed, enabled Archegos' voracious risk-taking." Credit Suisse failed to address "a culture that encouraged aggressive risk-taking and injudicious cost-cutting, as well as a complex and silo-ed organisational structure that impeded the swift identification, understanding, and escalation of risk."

### The Loss

USD 5.5 billion when Archegos defaulted on 25 March 2021.

## 7. Credit Suisse -- Greensill (2021)

### The Internal Warnings

Credit Suisse launched four supply chain finance funds from 2017 in collaboration with Greensill Capital, ultimately holding approximately **USD 10 billion** of client money. The FINMA investigation (concluded February 2023), drawing on reports by Swiss law firm Wenger Plattner based on over 700 pages of internal documents, found:

- **Minimal due diligence:** In 2017, Greensill filled out a brief questionnaire that investigators described as "inadequate." Clear contradictions and red flags in Greensill's submissions were never scrutinised.
- **Risk manager recommendation overruled:** A Credit Suisse risk manager responsible for a loan to Greensill identified a number of risks in Greensill's business model and recommended internally that the bank **not grant the loan**. A senior manager overruled this recommendation.
- **Diligence report dismissed:** Credit Suisse's trade-finance bankers commissioned a Diligence report and escalated it to the bank's Swiss general counsel, Thomas Grotzer, who forwarded it to Michel Degen and Luc Mathys, writing that "the issues raised here should be clarified in detail." In mid-2019, a memorandum by Mathys and portfolio manager Lukas Haas dismissed the Diligence report's allegations as "unsubstantiated and incorrect."
- **Little knowledge or control:** FINMA's investigation showed that Credit Suisse's asset management company had "little knowledge and control over the specific claims" in the funds. It was not Credit Suisse as asset manager that selected and reviewed claims, but Greensill itself.

### The Loss / Investor Exposure

Approximately **USD 10 billion** in total investor exposure at the time of fund closure in March 2021. Credit Suisse has recovered approximately USD 7.4 billion. FINMA concluded that Credit Suisse "seriously breached its supervisory obligations" regarding risk management and organisational structures.

## 8. Academic and Regulatory Framework

### 8.1 Diane Vaughan -- "Normalisation of Deviance"

Sociologist Diane Vaughan coined the term in her 1996 book *The Challenger Launch Decision: Risky Technology, Culture, and Deviance at NASA* (University of Chicago Press). She described it as "the gradual process through which unacceptable practice or standards become acceptable. As the deviant behaviour is repeated without catastrophic results, it becomes the social norm for the organisation."

Key insight: "No fundamental decision was made at NASA to do evil; rather, a series of seemingly harmless decisions were made that incrementally moved the space agency toward a catastrophic outcome." Vaughan argued it was **conformity**, not deviance per se, that caused the Challenger disaster.

**Application to banking:** The concept applies directly to the cases documented above. In each instance, risk limit breaches, control deficiencies, and warning signals became normalised through repetition without immediate catastrophic consequences. The 75 ignored alerts at Societe Generale, the persistent scenario limit breaches at Credit Suisse/Archegos, and the years of EthicsLine complaints at Wells Fargo all exhibit classic normalisation of deviance -- each individual exception seemed manageable, until the accumulated risk became unmanageable.

### 8.2 BCBS Corporate Governance Principles for Banks (July 2015)

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision published 13 revised corporate governance principles in July 2015 (document reference: d328). Key provisions relevant to governance bypass:

- **Principle 1 (Board's overall responsibilities):** The board should monitor management adherence to the risk appetite and tolerance statement, and provide for effective issue resolution processes including systems to "report, track and, when necessary, escalate issues to ensure resolution."
- **Principle 6 (Risk management function):** The risk management function is responsible for developing and implementing the enterprise-wide risk governance framework, with board review and approval.
- **Risk culture and escalation:** The bank's corporate values "should recognise the critical importance of timely and frank discussion and escalation of problems to higher levels within the organisation."
- **Whistleblower protections:** "Employees should be encouraged and able to communicate, confidentially and without the risk of reprisal, legitimate concerns about illegal, unethical or questionable practices."

### 8.3 FSB Framework for Assessing Risk Culture (April 2014)

The Financial Stability Board published "Guidance on Supervisory Interaction with Financial Institutions on Risk Culture: A Framework for Assessing Risk Culture" in April 2014. Key elements:

- **Rationale:** "Weaknesses in risk culture were a root cause of the global financial crisis, as they led to failures in compliance."
- **Three foundational elements** of sound risk culture: risk governance, risk appetite, and compensation.
- **Definition:** Risk culture comprises "the institution's norms, attitudes and behaviours related to risk awareness, risk taking and risk management."
- The guidance does not define a "target" culture but provides a framework for supervisors to assess whether risk culture is sound.

### 8.4 Recent Regulatory Publications (2024--2026)

**ECB Draft Guide on Governance and Risk Culture (July 2024):**
The European Central Bank published a draft Guide on governance and risk culture in July 2024, replacing the 2016 SSM supervisory statement. The Guide sets out supervisory expectations regarding how management bodies and committees should be composed and function, the roles of internal control functions, the importance of risk culture, and expectations regarding risk appetite frameworks. Public consultation closed 16 October 2024.

**BCBS Working Paper 45 -- Lessons on Supervisory Effectiveness (2025):**
A Basel Committee working paper that reviews lessons from bank failures and supervisory practices. Key finding: since banks' "qualitative weaknesses" (flawed risk management and business models) were root causes of bank failures, quantitative regulatory requirements need to be complemented by timely qualitative supervisory measures. The paper acknowledges that "impediments to effective supervision persist, limiting the will and ability of supervisors to act."

## The Common Failure Mode

Across all seven cases, the same pattern recurs:

1. **Risk is identified internally** -- by auditors, control systems, risk managers, or front-line employees.
2. **The warning reaches a decision-maker** -- but the decision-maker has a revenue interest, a relationship interest, or institutional inertia that conflicts with acting on the warning.
3. **The governance mechanism fails** -- the warning is rationalised away, the risk model is changed, the whistleblower is punished, or the escalation pathway is blocked by the very people it is designed to challenge.
4. **Normalisation sets in** -- the breach becomes the new baseline. Each day without a catastrophe reinforces the decision not to act.
5. **The loss materialises** -- and post-event investigations invariably find that the information needed to prevent it was already inside the organisation.

This is not a failure of risk identification. It is a failure of governance to act on identified risk. As the BCBS Working Paper 45 (2025) acknowledges, the fundamental problem is that "impediments to effective supervision persist, limiting the will and ability" to act -- whether those impediments sit inside banks or inside regulators.
