The Delusion of Discovery: Why Most Banks Can't Identify Their Own Risks
Introduction: The Epistemological Blind Spot in Modern Banking
In the aftermath of successive global financial crises, the international banking sector has invested heavily into the expansion of risk management infrastructure. Financial institutions have established sprawling three-lines-of-defense models, hired legions of risk professionals, and implemented sophisticated Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) software platforms. Yet, despite this unprecedented allocation of capital and human resources to the discipline of risk, highly capitalized and heavily regulated financial institutions routinely find themselves blindsided by existential threats. The root cause of this systemic vulnerability is rarely a failure of complex mathematics, nor is it a lack of computational power or algorithmic sophistication. Rather, it is a fundamental epistemological failure rooted in corporate bureaucracy. Most banks suffer from a profound inability to identify their own risks, primarily because they conflate the administrative maintenance of a "risk register" with the dynamic, investigative discipline of "risk identification."
At the heart of this systemic vulnerability is what practitioners refer to as the inherited risk register pathology. In practice, the annual risk identification exercise at many global financial institutions is little more than a bureaucratic rollover. Risk practitioners take the previous year’s taxonomy of risks, adjust the probability and impact scores marginally to reflect recent macroeconomic data, archive a handful of obsolete operational risks, and present the refreshed spreadsheet to the board of directors and regulatory supervisors. This administrative activity is universally termed "risk identification," but it is entirely devoid of actual discovery. It is an exercise in risk assessment and risk monitoring, applied strictly to a static universe of known, historical variables.
However, identification—the act of discovering what risks exist, acknowledging the unknown, mapping emerging threat vectors before they materialize, and piercing the veil of institutional complexity—is a fundamentally different discipline from assessment. When institutions fail to separate the discovery of risk from the measurement of risk, they create a perilous and seductive illusion of control. They build highly sophisticated predictive models to assess the exact velocity, trajectory, and financial impact of risks they already know, while remaining completely blind to the exogenous shocks and endogenous structural vulnerabilities that actually threaten their solvency. The distinction between possessing a risk register and executing a risk identification process is not a matter of semantic pedantry; it is the definitive boundary between institutional resilience and institutional failure.
This report provides an exhaustive, expert-level analysis of the structural, regulatory, and cognitive gaps between risk identification and risk assessment. By synthesizing global regulatory findings, academic research, maturity surveys, and post-mortem analyses of recent institutional failures, this document demonstrates why the transition from a static risk register to a continuous risk discovery apparatus is the most critical imperative for Chief Risk Officers (CROs), Heads of Risk, and risk identification practitioners today. Throughout this analysis, comprehensive bibliographic details of the underlying research and regulatory frameworks are woven directly into the narrative and structured tables to provide practitioners with the authoritative sourcing required to challenge legacy risk paradigms.
The Ontological Divide: Academic and Industry Frameworks
To understand why banks consistently fail to identify novel risks, one must first examine the formal definitions that govern the global risk management discipline. Across international standards, industry association guidelines, and academic literature, risk identification is explicitly categorized as a distinct, foundational phase that must precede analysis, evaluation, and treatment. The routine conflation of these phases in modern banking is a severe deviation from established frameworks, resulting in a systemic blinding of the first and second lines of defense.
The Standardization of Discovery in ISO Frameworks
The International Organization for Standardization (ISO), specifically in the widely adopted ISO 31000:2018 (Risk Management – Guidelines), draws a strict ontological boundary between identification and subsequent risk phases. The standard explicitly deconstructs the overarching risk assessment process into three distinct, sequential components. According to the framework published at www.iso.org, the process begins with Risk Identification (Clause 6.4.2), moves to Risk Analysis (Clause 6.4.3), and concludes with Risk Evaluation (Clause 6.4.4).1
ISO 31000 mandates that risk identification must be a formal, structured, and continuous process designed to "find, understand, and describe risks" that could either hinder or help an organization in achieving its strategic objectives.3 This phase requires the systematic exploration of tangible and intangible sources, events, their root causes, and their potential consequences.1 The cognitive approach required here is divergent and investigative. This is fundamentally different from Risk Analysis (Clause 6.4.3), which takes the output of the identification phase and utilizes convergent, analytical thinking to determine the likelihood, impact, and velocity of those specific events.2 If the identification phase is bypassed or treated as a mere administrative formality, the subsequent analysis is performed on an incomplete data set, rendering the entire ISO 31000 framework ineffective.
The Perspectives of the IRM and RIMS
The world's leading risk management professional bodies—the Institute of Risk Management (IRM) and the Risk and Insurance Management Society (RIMS)—heavily reinforce this dichotomy between discovery and measurement. In publications addressing strategic and enterprise risk, the IRM defines risk identification as the exhaustive "inventory and classification of all risks the central bank [or financial institution] is exposed to," which must be completed entirely before "risk assessment—the analysis and measurement of the identified risks" can occur.5
The IRM stresses that risk identification is about "exposing and recording all foreseeable risks... together with information on their cause(s) and possible effect(s)," whereas assessment is strictly about statistical modeling, prioritization, and severity estimation.6 Similarly, RIMS publications on Enterprise Risk Management emphasize that risk identification must be an "ongoing and continuous process, with the organization's risk register updated with each newly identified risk" before any assessment or scoring takes place.7 The RIMS Risk Maturity Model highlights that organizations frequently suffer a capability gap precisely because they leap to risk assessment tools (like surveys and interviews focusing on threats) without employing collaborative methodologies designed to unearth previously unrecognized vulnerabilities.8
Academic and Practitioner Consensus on the Identification Gap
Academic and practitioner research confirms that treating risk identification as a mere prerequisite to assessment, rather than a separate and rigorous scientific discipline, compromises the entire risk framework. In a highly influential 2015 white paper titled Risk Identification: What Have Banks Been Missing?, authored by D. Haselkorn, I. Khaykin, and R. Eaton of Oliver Wyman (available via www.oliverwyman.com), the researchers argued forcefully that legacy risk identification processes completely fail to serve institutions' capital and stress testing needs.10
The Oliver Wyman paper highlights that traditional identification processes "do not incorporate enough different perspectives on risks from across the organization; effectively distinguish the most significant risks from more minor risks; or sufficiently consider the underlying drivers of risks and how they could interact and amplify risks".10 The authors note that as institutions adopt stress testing-based approaches to capital management, risk identification must be elevated to better inform scenario design and vulnerability mapping.10
When banks merge identification with assessment, they suffer from a form of institutional confirmation bias. Because the tools of assessment—such as Value at Risk (VaR) models, Expected Credit Loss (ECL) calculations, and Monte Carlo simulations—require structured historical data, the risk management function becomes heavily biased toward identifying only those risks that can be easily and mathematically measured. Risks that lack clean historical data sets—such as novel geopolitical realignments, unprecedented climate transition pathways, or complex supply chain finance frauds—are frequently excluded from the register. Consequently, the boundary of what the bank considers a "risk" is dictated not by the objective reality of the macroeconomic environment, but by the limitations of the bank's own assessment models.

	The Pathology of the "Inherited Risk Register"
The most visible and dangerous symptom of an institution's failure to separate identification from assessment is the persistence of the "inherited risk register." Across the global banking sector, it is standard practice for the enterprise risk register to be built from scratch only once—often during the initial implementation of a new Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) software system, or following a major regulatory remediation program—and then never fundamentally rebuilt again.
The Mechanics of the Rollover Delusion
In practice, the inherited risk register problem manifests most acutely during the annual risk review cycle. Instead of asking business line leaders and front-line risk owners to identify the risks inherent in their current operations from a blank slate, the second line of defense distributes the existing, historical risk register. Business leaders are asked to "refresh" the entries. Human psychology, cognitive load, and corporate incentive structures heavily dictate the outcome of this administrative exercise. Confronted with a spreadsheet containing hundreds of legacy risks, managers will typically tweak the probability or impact scores by a fraction to demonstrate active management, perhaps archive a few obsolete operational risks, and promptly return the document.
This process completely bypasses genuine risk identification. It rests on the highly flawed assumption that the underlying business model, the macroeconomic environment, the regulatory perimeter, and the technological landscape have not fundamentally altered the institution's ontology of risk. When banks operate with inherited registers, they are effectively managing today's complex business model using yesterday's threat taxonomy. Evidence of this pathology frequently surfaces in governance audits across various sectors; for instance, regulatory audits by the Care Quality Commission (CQC) in the UK have explicitly cited the danger of an "inherited risk register that had not been fit for purpose and required extensive review" as a primary driver of deep-seated organizational blindness and governance failure.12 While drawn from the public health sector, the exact same bureaucratic mechanism plagues tier-one financial institutions.
The Federal Reserve's Antidote: SR 15-18
Regulatory bodies are acutely aware of the inherited risk register problem and have begun explicitly legislating against it to force banks out of their complacency. In the United States, the Federal Reserve’s SR 15-18: Federal Reserve Supervisory Assessment of Capital Planning and Positions for Firms Subject to Category I Standards (published in 2015 and updated subsequently, available via www.federalreserve.gov) represents one of the most direct regulatory attacks on static risk identification.13
SR 15-18 mandates that a firm's risk identification process must include a "comprehensive assessment of risks stemming from its unique business activities and associated exposures," explicitly requiring the capture of on-balance sheet assets, off-balance sheet exposures, and the vulnerability of the firm's earnings under stress.14 Crucially, the Federal Reserve enforces a strict temporal requirement specifically designed to break the cycle of annual administrative roll-overs. The guidance dictates that senior management of a firm subject to Category I standards must establish a formal risk identification process and evaluate its material risks at least quarterly.13
This explicit requirement for quarterly re-identification—not merely quarterly re-assessment of existing scores—forces large financial institutions to continuously scan for new vulnerabilities. Furthermore, SR 15-18 demands that this identification process seek active input from multiple stakeholders across the entire organization, including front-line business leaders and finance professionals, rather than leaving risk discovery solely in the hands of the second line of defense.13 The Federal Reserve’s explicit requirement to capture risks that only materialize or become apparent under stressful conditions acts as a direct, regulatory-mandated countermeasure against the static nature of inherited registers.
Global Regulatory Findings on Risk Identification Weaknesses
The failure of financial institutions to maintain an independent, dynamic risk identification process is not merely a theoretical framework flaw; it is the primary driver of supervisory enforcement actions globally. Regulators in the European Union, the United Kingdom, and the United States have consistently and aggressively highlighted risk identification gaps as a severe, systemic threat to the safety and soundness of the financial ecosystem.
European Central Bank (ECB): SREP Aggregate Findings
The European Central Bank's Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP) provides a comprehensive, aggregate view of risk management maturity across European Union banks. The ECB SREP Aggregated Results for 2024 and 2025 (published by ECB Banking Supervision at www.bankingsupervision.europa.eu) reveal pervasive, persistent, and highly concerning weaknesses specifically centered on risk identification.16
In the 2024 SREP cycle, the ECB issued qualitative measures across 97 banks, with a significant concentration focused on the failure of institutions to identify and aggregate their risks effectively.16 The distribution of these qualitative measures provides a stark illustration of where banks are failing their identification mandates:
* Credit Risk (29% of qualitative measures): The ECB found severe deficiencies in early warning systems, particularly among Corporate and Wholesale lenders. The inability of early warning systems to flag deterioration before it becomes a non-performing loan is fundamentally a risk identification failure.16 Furthermore, Diversified Lenders showed explicit deficiencies in risk identification and monitoring, particularly concerning collateral revaluation and IFRS 9 expected credit loss modeling, proving that banks could not identify the true underlying value of their exposures.16
* Internal Governance (23% of qualitative measures): The ECB cited the lack of effectiveness of management bodies and an insufficient challenge culture as critical drivers of poor scores.16 A critical component of this governance failure was the persistence of fragmented IT landscapes leading to long-standing deficiencies in Risk Data Aggregation and Risk Reporting (RDARR) capabilities.16
* Capital Adequacy (11% of qualitative measures): Within this specific category, 15% of all new supervisory measures were strictly focused on "risk identification, aggregation and measurement methodologies and procedures," proving that regulators are directly targeting the methodologies banks use to discover risk.16
The 2025 SREP results confirmed that these issues are deeply systemic rather than isolated incidents. The ECB noted that while many banks have established seemingly mature Risk Appetite Frameworks (RAF), there remains a critical and dangerous misalignment between the risks banks actually identify in the real world and their formal risk appetite statements.17 Furthermore, RDARR deficiencies accounted for a massive 20% of new qualitative measures in 2025, demonstrating that banks still lack the foundational data architecture required to identify risk concentrations across disparate business units.17 The ECB has explicitly stated its intention to move up the "escalation ladder" and impose binding enforcement measures if these long-standing risk identification deficiencies are not urgently remediated.16
European Banking Authority (EBA): Peer Reviews on Risk Governance
The European Banking Authority (EBA) has conducted numerous peer reviews that corroborate the ECB's findings regarding systemic risk governance weaknesses. In the EBA's publications on supervisory convergence (accessible via www.eba.europa.eu), the authority has repeatedly highlighted the failure of institutions to build robust identification frameworks.
For example, the EBA's peer reviews on ICT risk assessment under the SREP (EBA/Rep/2022/25) and the supervision of non-performing exposures management (EBA/Rep/2022/12) point to a recurrent theme: banks struggle to identify risks that fall outside traditional credit and market risk paradigms.19 Furthermore, in the EBA's Risk Assessment Report (RAR) for 2024 and 2025, the authority noted that while geoeconomic factors and geopolitical risks are increasingly influencing risk management, the identification of these risks remains highly theoretical.20 Banks are struggling to translate macroeconomic uncertainty—such as global value chain complexity, tariffs, and rising sovereign debt premiums—into specific, identifiable risk events on their corporate registers.20
Prudential Regulation Authority (UK): Dear CEO Letters and Climate Risk
The Bank of England's Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) has utilized "Dear CEO" and "Dear CFO" letters extensively as a supervisory tool to highlight risk identification failures, particularly concerning emerging, non-linear threats such as climate change and private credit exposures.
In the PRA's 2024 and 2025 supervisory priorities and corresponding Dear CEO letters (published at www.bankofengland.co.uk), the regulator noted a frustrating reality: while firms have made administrative progress, their core, fundamental capabilities to actually identify and manage climate-related risk remain in their infancy.21 The PRA explicitly observed in consultation papers that many firms incorrectly conclude that climate-related risk is not a material risk to their specific business model; however, the regulator noted that this conclusion "is not based on an adequate assessment of climate-related risk exposures".21 In other words, because the risk was never properly identified, mapped, or sized in the first place, the bank falsely and dangerously assumed it did not exist.
To combat this, the PRA's Supervisory Statement SS5/25 (which updates and replaces the foundational SS3/19 framework) forces boards and senior executive management to integrate climate risks directly into strategy and risk appetite.23 To inform this integration, the PRA strictly expects firms to undertake a "board-approved risk identification and materiality assessment," requiring firms to actively identify new transmission channels for physical and transition risks across all exposures, rather than relying on historical risk typologies.23 Furthermore, in the context of funded reinsurance, the PRA issued a Dear CEO letter warning firms to pay particular attention to identifying exposures to private credit assets, citing risks identified by the Financial Policy Committee regarding opaque vulnerabilities that only manifest in stressed conditions.25
US Regulators: OCC Heightened Standards and Punitive Consent Orders
In the United States, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) has aggressively targeted risk identification failures through both prescriptive regulatory frameworks and highly punitive public enforcement actions. The OCC's "Heightened Standards" guidelines (12 C.F.R. Part 30, Appendix D: OCC Guidelines Establishing Heightened Standards for Certain Large Insured National Banks, Insured Federal Savings Associations, and Insured Federal Branches) mandate highly detailed risk governance frameworks for large institutions.26
These standards compel front-line business units to take absolute accountability for identifying the risks they generate. This regulation is designed to prevent the extremely common structural failure where the revenue-generating business line assumes that risk identification is solely the responsibility of the independent, second-line risk management function.28 The OCC demands that the risk governance framework cover specific risk stripes and be composed of highly detailed parts, including risk limits, talent management, and rigorous data aggregation.27

	The Basel Committee (BCBS 239): The Data Aggregation Bottleneck
The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) regulation 239, formally titled Principles for effective risk data aggregation and risk reporting, was established in 2013 directly following the 2008 financial crisis. The regulation was born from a simple, terrifying realization: during the crisis, global banks were fundamentally unable to identify their total aggregate exposures to specific counterparties or risk types because their data was fragmented.33 Risk identification is mechanically impossible if the underlying data is trapped in isolated, incompatible silos.
More than a decade after its initial publication, the Bank for International Settlements (BIS) published the 2023 BCBS 239 Progress Report: Progress in adopting the Principles for effective risk data aggregation and risk reporting (published February 2024 at www.bis.org).35 The report revealed a damning reality for the global banking sector: out of 31 global systemically important banks (G-SIBs) rigorously assessed, only two were found to be fully compliant with the data aggregation principles.35
The BIS noted that the overall pace of implementing sustainable risk data aggregation has been "slower than foreseen, due, in large part, to persistent challenges with fragmented IT landscapes, legacy systems, and manual processes that are not fit for purpose".33 A bank cannot discover or identify a risk concentration if that concentration is obscured by thousands of manual spreadsheets and disconnected, legacy databases. Consequently, the reliance on inherited risk registers is often a psychological and administrative coping mechanism for banks whose data architectures are simply too primitive to support dynamic, enterprise-wide risk discovery.
Empirical Evidence: Survey Data on Risk Identification Maturity
Extensive industry survey data confirms that the distinction between risk identification and risk assessment remains poorly understood by practitioners. This widespread confusion leads to a dangerous overestimation of risk maturity among bank executives. When banks are surveyed by major consulting firms, they frequently report high levels of maturity in their risk management programs; however, deeper analysis of the underlying data reveals that this perceived maturity applies almost exclusively to risk measurement and reporting, not to risk discovery.
Deloitte Global Risk Management Survey
The 13th edition of the Deloitte Global Risk Management Survey (and subsequent updates regarding Third-Party Risk Management), which gathered detailed insights from Chief Risk Officers and executives at large financial organizations across the globe, exposed a massive cognitive dissonance regarding true risk capability.36
The survey found that while an overwhelming 88% of executive respondents recognized value creation as a key goal of their strategic risk frameworks, a mere 40% believed their organizations had been successful in actually creating value through risk management.36 A primary driver of this failure, according to the respondents, is the stubborn persistence of organizational silos, which Deloitte cited as a top risk-related failure causing organizations to struggle with strategy implementation.36
Silos represent the absolute death of risk identification. Risks rarely manifest neatly within the boundaries of a single corporate business unit; they are generated in the opaque spaces between units. For example, a new digital banking product launch might carry credit risk (managed by the lending team), operational and cyber risk (managed by IT), and compliance risk (managed by legal). If these highly specialized groups do not collaborate on a holistic, blank-slate risk identification exercise, the aggregate, compounding threat is never mapped. The inherited risk register remains completely blind to the interaction of these distinct risk types, capturing only isolated fragments of the true threat landscape.
EY/IIF Global Bank Risk Management Survey
The 13th Annual EY/IIF Global Bank Risk Management Survey, published jointly by the Institute of International Finance (IIF) and Ernst & Young (EY) in 2024, similarly highlights the severe friction between the rapid evolution of external market threats and the highly static nature of internal risk identification processes.39
In recent editions, this sweeping survey has focused heavily on the rapid adoption of Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning (AI/ML) within financial services. The explosive integration of Generative AI and Large Language Models (LLMs) has catapulted novel, unprecedented risks to the absolute top of the C-suite agenda. Banks are desperately trying to balance the competitive risk of not adopting AI against the need to control model hallucination, explainability deficits, and data poisoning risks.40
However, the survey data implies a structural failure: many institutions are attempting to govern these completely unprecedented technologies using legacy risk registers. As the IIF and EY noted, managing the third-party risks associated with cloud-deployed AI models requires a complete re-identification of the bank's operational resilience perimeter.40 Banks that merely update the probability scores on their existing "IT security" or "Vendor Management" risk register entries to account for Generative AI are fundamentally failing to identify the novel transmission channels that these technologies introduce. They are assessing an old risk, rather than identifying a new one.
Anatomy of Failure: Unidentified Risks vs. Ignored Risks
To fully appreciate the absolute necessity of dedicated, independent risk identification processes, it is essential to distinguish between banks that failed because a risk was genuinely unidentified (a true identification failure) and those that failed because an identified risk was ignored (a management, cultural, and governance failure). The conflation of these two distinct typologies of failure often leads regulators, boards of directors, and risk practitioners to prescribe the wrong medicine following a crisis.
The Ignored Known: Archegos Capital Management (Credit Suisse)
The spectacular collapse of Archegos Capital Management, which resulted in a catastrophic $5.5 billion loss for Credit Suisse in 2021, is frequently mischaracterized in the financial press as a failure of risk identification. In reality, it was a profound, systemic failure of risk management, behavioral culture, and governance.
The highly detailed independent report compiled by the law firm Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison LLP (published by Credit Suisse to the SEC via www.sec.gov) explicitly absolved the bank's risk identification architecture. The report categorically stated that the Archegos risks were "identified and were conspicuous".41 The investigators confirmed that this was not a scenario where the bank's existing risk systems failed to operate sufficiently to identify critical vulnerabilities.41
By March 2021, the bank's risk systems had accurately and thoroughly mapped the portfolio's dimensions. The systems clearly identified extreme single-issuer concentrations representing more than 8% of the outstanding float, and noted that the top five issuers represented 3 to 7 days’ total market trading volume.41 The identification phase worked flawlessly. The failure was entirely behavioral. The business and risk personnel "had all the information necessary to appreciate the magnitude and urgency of the Archegos risks, but failed at multiple junctures to take decisive and urgent action to address them".41
Despite these clearly identified concentration red flags appearing on internal reports, the Capital Province Operations Committee (CPOC) exhibited a highly "muted" reaction. In a display of profound management failure, they requested a mere $250 million in margin from Archegos against a required $1.3 billion day-one step-up.41 The Archegos disaster underscores a critical lesson: a perfect risk identification process is completely useless if the institution's culture permits "Success Bias" and "Relationship Override" to suppress the mitigation of known threats.41
The Unidentified Unknown: Silicon Valley Bank (SVB)
Conversely, the historic collapse of Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) in 2023 serves as a quintessential, textbook example of an existential risk identification failure. Prior to its rapid implosion, SVB was highly profitable, revered in the technology sector, and considered well-capitalized under standard regulatory metrics. The bank possessed extensive risk registers, held regular Asset Liability Management (ALM) committee meetings, and utilized complex interest rate risk models.
However, SVB fundamentally failed to identify the lethal, compounding interaction between two highly concentrated structural vulnerabilities: a massive portfolio of long-duration, fixed-rate securities and a deposit base that was overwhelmingly uninsured and highly networked within the insular venture capital ecosystem.43 The bank assessed its interest rate risk in strict isolation (which showed manageable accounting losses if the bonds were held to maturity) and assessed its liquidity risk in strict isolation (assuming standard, historical deposit stickiness).
What SVB failed to identify was the novel transmission mechanism connecting the two: in a rapidly rising rate environment, the mark-to-market losses on the securities portfolio would trigger an immediate, socially-networked panic among a highly homogenous group of uninsured depositors. These depositors possessed the capability to withdraw billions of dollars instantly via digital banking channels. This specific, compounding, non-linear risk scenario was missing from their risk register entirely. Because the risk was never conceptually identified, the subsequent assessment models relied on deeply flawed assumptions about deposit flight velocity, leading directly to the bank's sudden insolvency.42
The Complexity Blindfold: Greensill Capital
The multi-billion dollar failure associated with Greensill Capital provides another distinct lens into risk identification breakdowns, particularly concerning complex, structured financial products. Greensill operated in the Supply Chain Finance (SCF) sector, a space characterized by credit-guaranteed financing, purchase order financing, and factoring.45 The diverse and opaque range of these models significantly complicates risk identification and assessment for partner financial institutions.45
Institutions that were heavily exposed to Greensill failed to identify the true, underlying nature of the credit exposures. They assessed the assets as low-risk, short-term trade receivables based on the labels provided. However, their risk identification processes failed to pierce the veil of product complexity to discover the operational and structural realities: that Greensill was effectively providing unsecured, long-term working capital to highly concentrated, risky entities, while masking the true exposure through complex securitization vehicles and trade credit insurance wrappers.45
When the trade credit insurance was abruptly withdrawn, the underlying, massive credit risk materialized instantly. The failure here was one of epistemological mapping; the banks' risk identification processes were too superficial to unbundle the complex structures and catalog the true counterparty risk.47 The risk register simply stated "Supply Chain Finance," entirely missing the reality of the threat.
Strategic Imperatives for Genuine Risk Discovery
For Chief Risk Officers, Heads of Risk, and risk practitioners reading the regulatory tea leaves, the supervisory findings and post-mortem analyses dictate an urgent, non-negotiable strategic pivot. Financial institutions must deliberately dismantle the inherited risk register pathology and implement structural, cultural, and technological mechanisms dedicated exclusively to genuine risk discovery.
1. Decouple Identification from Assessment Procedurally
The first imperative is organizational and procedural. The process of identifying risks must be separated chronologically and procedurally from the process of assessing them, aligning with ISO 31000 standards. If a risk workshop is convened to identify risks, practitioners must be strictly forbidden from discussing probability, velocity, or financial impact during that session. Introducing assessment metrics too early stifles the divergent thinking required for discovery. The sole goal of the identification phase is to build an exhaustive, creative ontology of vulnerabilities, including tail risks, emerging threats, and seemingly "implausible" scenarios. Only after this universe of threats is comprehensively mapped should the quantitative modeling teams begin to apply statistical analysis and prioritize the register.
2. Implement Quarterly, Blank-Slate Discovery Mandates
Following the strict mandate of the Federal Reserve’s SR 15-18, all banks—not just Category I institutions—should abolish the administrative practice of distributing legacy risk registers for "refreshing." At least quarterly, front-line business leaders and independent risk managers must be forced into a room and asked to articulate the risks inherent in their operations from a completely blank slate. They should be prompted with targeted, forward-looking investigative questions: What new third parties are we relying on today that we weren't a year ago? How has client digital behavior shifted in the last 90 days? What specific macroeconomic assumption is our current profitability entirely dependent upon, and what happens if it breaks? By removing the psychological anchor of the historical risk register, banks force their personnel to actively scan the horizon.
3. Invert Scenario Analysis via Reverse Stress Testing
Traditionally, banks use scenario analysis to test the financial resilience of known risks against hypothetical macroeconomic shocks (e.g., assessing the existing commercial real estate portfolio against a 30% drop in property prices). To foster true risk identification, this process must be inverted. Banks should heavily utilize "reverse stress testing," a methodology where the institution starts with the outcome of complete institutional failure or severe capital depletion, and works backward to creatively identify the specific chain of events that could cause that outcome. This methodology actively forces the institution to discover hidden correlations, networked vulnerabilities, and undocumented transmission channels that are currently missing from the standard, historically-biased risk register.
4. Deploy Behavioral Risk Management
As the Credit Suisse / Archegos failure so starkly demonstrated, even perfect identification processes are frequently subverted by human biases and toxic corporate cultures. CROs must implement behavioral risk management frameworks designed to identify soft signals that precede formal limit breaches. This includes actively monitoring for "Success Bias" (where highly profitable trading units are subjected to less risk scrutiny), tracking unusual or aggressive justifications for exceeding risk limits, and identifying structural resistance to independent oversight from the second line of defense.42 By treating human behavior, corporate culture, and compensation incentives as identifiable risk vectors, institutions can capture vulnerabilities long before they manifest as quantitative financial losses.
Conclusion
The distinction between possessing a risk register and executing a robust risk identification process is not a matter of semantics; it is the fundamental boundary between institutional survival and catastrophic failure. A risk register, in its traditional form, is merely a historical artifact—a catalog of threats that the bank has already encountered, fully understood, and mathematically quantified. Risk identification, however, is a forward-looking, highly investigative, and deeply analytical scientific discipline.
When financial institutions conflate the two, they engage in a dangerous administrative pantomime. They spend millions of dollars updating the statistical likelihood of known threats while leaving their flanks completely exposed to the compounding, non-linear, and highly complex vulnerabilities of the modern global financial system. The regulatory data is unambiguous: from the ECB's damning SREP findings, to the OCC's punitive consent orders, to the Federal Reserve's prescriptive SR 15-18 mandates, global supervisors are demanding that banks immediately cease the bureaucratic rollover of inherited registers.
By formally and procedurally decoupling risk identification from risk assessment, enforcing continuous blank-slate discovery exercises, inverting scenario analysis, and acknowledging the deep psychological barriers to recognizing novel threats, financial institutions can finally cure their epistemological blindness. Only by actively and relentlessly hunting for the unknown can banks secure themselves against the next inevitable crisis. The era of the static risk register is over; the era of continuous risk discovery must begin.
