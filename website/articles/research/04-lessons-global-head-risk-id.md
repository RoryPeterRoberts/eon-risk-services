# I Was Global Head of Risk ID — Here's What I Learned

**Research File: Personal Anecdotes & Practitioner Insights from "Bank Risk Identification: The Complete Methodology"**

This document extracts all first-person experiences, lessons learned, and practitioner insights from Rory Roberts' 16-chapter book on risk identification methodology. These passages form the narrative backbone of his career experience building risk identification processes at two globally significant banking institutions.

---

## The "Confused Silence" Moment

**Chapter 1:**
> "In 2016, I was appointed Global Head of Risk Identification at a European G-SIB. My mandate was to build a bank-wide risk identification process from scratch. On my first day, I asked what I thought was a simple question: How does this institution currently identify its risks?
>
> The answer was silence.
>
> Not hostile silence — confused silence. The kind that suggests the question itself does not make sense. After a long pause, someone mentioned the ICAAP. Someone else mentioned operational risk event reporting. A third person said something about model risk governance. All of these were true, but none of them answered the question.
>
> No one could describe, in plain language, the systematic process by which the institution — a globally significant bank with over a trillion dollars in assets, operating in more than fifty countries, supervised by multiple regulators across three continents — identified the full universe of risks it faced.
>
> That silence told me everything I needed to know."

**Chapter 15:**
> "At a UK-regulated international banking group in 2023, I sat across the table from a PRA supervisor who opened a thick examination file and asked a single question: 'Walk me through how your risk identification process satisfies SS31/15.'
>
> I could. Not because I had prepared for the question, but because the methodology had been designed with regulatory traceability built in from the start."

## Building the Function from Scratch

**Chapter 1:**
> "I have built it twice — at two globally significant institutions, one a European G-SIB and the other a UK-regulated international banking group — and refined it through years of practice, regulatory challenge, and the kind of learning that only comes from doing it wrong before you learn to do it right."

**Chapter 2:**
> "At Institution A, when I built the risk identification process in 2016, one of the first things I had to establish was the standards architecture. Not because regulators required it — though they did — but because without it, the process would have lacked both legitimacy and operational coherence."

**Chapter 4:**
> "In my first month as Global Head of Risk Identification at Institution A, I convened a meeting to reconcile the risk registers across business units. What I expected was a straightforward alignment exercise. What I got was a two-hour argument about what things were called.
>
> Treasury called it 'funding liquidity risk.' Corporate Banking called it 'refinancing risk.' The Markets division called it 'rollover risk.' They were describing the same exposure — the risk that the bank would be unable to renew its short-term funding — but using different language. And because they used different language, they managed it in different ways, reported it to different committees, and assessed it against different thresholds.
>
> Building that language was the first operational priority. Before we could identify risks, we had to agree on what we meant by them."

**Chapter 6:**
> "I walked out of that workshop knowing that the entire approach had to be redesigned. The methodology that follows — structured techniques, pre-meeting preparation, rotating facilitation, transparent capture — was built from that failure."

**Chapter 13:**
> "When I built the risk identification process in 2016, one of the first things I had to establish was the annual timetable. Not because regulators required it — though they did — but because without it, the process would have been reactive, fragmented, and impossible to resource."

**Chapter 14:**
> "At a European G-SIB, I built the initial risk inventory in a spreadsheet. It was not elegant. It was not scalable. But it captured the methodology accurately, and it allowed the institution to operate the first two cycles of the process while the technology team designed the platform that would eventually replace it."

## What I Discovered

**Chapter 5 — Hypo Real Estate:**
> "In October 2007, I joined a German mortgage bank as a Financial Risk Manager. My job was to price CDOs and mortgage-backed securities for a portfolio that had been built, in part, through an acquisition the bank had completed earlier that year. Hypo Real Estate had just completed the acquisition of Depfa Bank, an Irish institution with a large portfolio of public-sector financing. The integration was still underway. The pricing models were still being harmonised. The risk governance structures were still being established.
>
> Thirteen months later, Hypo Real Estate required a €102 billion government guarantee and was nationalised. The proximate cause was a liquidity crisis triggered by the freezing of short-term funding markets. The underlying cause was that the institution had acquired risks it did not fully understand, in structures it could not adequately model, funded by instruments it could not reliably roll over.
>
> I was there. I watched it happen from the inside."

**Chapter 7 — The Copy-Paste Problem:**
> "In early 2017, several months into my role as Global Head of Risk Identification at a European G-SIB, I received the first round of bottom-up risk assessments from the business units. The process had been communicated clearly. The templates had been designed carefully. The deadlines had been set with input from the first line. The submissions came in on time.
>
> I opened the first one — a major trading division — and compared it against the prior year's submission, which I had retrieved from the archives.
>
> It was identical. Not similar. Identical. Same risks. Same descriptions. Same ratings. The only thing that had changed was the date.
>
> I opened the second submission. Same pattern. I opened the third. Same pattern. Of the twelve submissions I reviewed that day, nine were either unchanged from the prior year or contained only cosmetic updates — minor rewording, adjusted formatting, risks moved between categories without explanation."

**Chapter 8 — The Reconciliation Gap:**
> "In early 2017, I sat in a conference room at a European G-SIB with two printouts on the table. One was the top-down material risk inventory — a list of approximately twenty risks that had been identified through structured workshops with senior management and the board. The other was a consolidated bottom-up risk register — nearly two hundred risks that had been submitted by business units, functions, and legal entities across the group.
>
> My task was to reconcile them. To identify which risks appeared in both lists, which appeared in only one, and — most importantly — which risks existed in neither.
>
> What I found was not alignment. It was incoherence. Of the twenty top-down material risks, twelve had no corresponding entry in the bottom-up register. Of the nearly two hundred bottom-up risks, forty-seven were not covered by any top-down category. And when I attempted to map the remainder — the risks that appeared, in some form, in both lists — I discovered that the institution used different definitions, different rating scales, different ownership structures, and different control frameworks for what were, in substance, the same exposures.
>
> I asked the question that reconciliation is designed to answer: who owns this risk?
>
> No one."

**Chapter 8 — Archegos:**
> "Four years later, in March 2021, the collapse of Archegos Capital Management would cost Credit Suisse $5.5 billion. The risk that materialised was precisely the kind of cross-counterparty, cross-product concentration that reconciliation is designed to identify. A single client had built leveraged equity derivative positions across multiple prime brokerage desks. Each desk monitored its own exposure. No one was monitoring the aggregate."

**Chapter 10 — Risk Interactions:**
> "At a European G-SIB where I first built this process, building the first interaction matrix revealed that counterparty credit risk was connected — through trigger or amplification relationships — to eleven other material risks. The institution had been managing counterparty credit risk as a standalone exposure for decades. The matrix made visible what had been latent: that a counterparty default could trigger liquidity stress (if the counterparty was also a funding provider), which could amplify market risk (if forced asset sales occurred), which could trigger model risk (if stressed conditions invalidated pricing assumptions), which could trigger strategic risk (if the institution's creditworthiness was questioned by the market)."

**Chapter 11 — Documentation Standards:**
> "At Institution A, when I introduced risk profiles for the material risk inventory, the initial submissions from some business units were precisely the kind of compliance theatre I described above. Generic descriptions. Thin control narratives. Indicators that measured activity rather than exposure.
>
> I returned them. Not with corrections — with questions. What does 'inadequate controls' mean? Which controls? Inadequate relative to what standard? What would 'adequate' look like? How would you know if the situation improved?
>
> By the second cycle, business units understood that risk profiles with thin content would be challenged and returned. By the third cycle, the quality had improved materially."

**Chapter 11 — The Colour-Coded Spreadsheet:**
> "When I arrived at a UK-regulated international banking group, the reconciliation documentation from the prior cycle was a merged spreadsheet with colour-coding but no gap analysis. Risks from the top-down list were highlighted in blue. Risks from the bottom-up list were highlighted in green. Risks that appeared in both were highlighted in yellow.
>
> There was no narrative explanation of what the colour-coding meant. There was no analysis of why twelve top-down risks had no bottom-up owner. There was no record of whether the gaps had been investigated, whether new controls had been established, or whether the unowned risks had been accepted, transferred, or mitigated.
>
> The spreadsheet was not documentation. It was evidence that someone had completed a task. It told the institution nothing about the risks it faced."

**Chapter 12 — The Parallel Universes:**
> "I have worked at institutions where the risk identification process was rigorous and the capital planning process was rigorous, and the two operated as parallel universes. The ICAAP identified material risks in July. The stress testing team modelled different risks in September. The capital planning team submitted a plan in November that referenced neither.
>
> When I asked why the processes were not aligned, I was told that they had 'different owners.' When I asked whether anyone had attempted to align them, I was told that it was 'not in scope.'
>
> The gap was not technical. It was organisational. The institution had built rigorous components but had not integrated them into a coherent whole."

**Chapter 12 — Institution A and CCAR:**
> "At Institution A, integrating the risk identification process with CCAR was a defining requirement. The US operations were supervised by the Federal Reserve, and the CCAR submission required the institution to demonstrate that its capital planning was informed by a forward-looking assessment of material risks. The risk identification process became the foundation of that assessment."

**Chapter 12 — Institution B and ICAAP:**
> "At Institution B, the ICAAP process required exactly this comparison. The institution was required to demonstrate that the risks modelled in its capital plan were consistent with the risks identified through its risk identification process, and that any gaps had been explained and addressed."

## The Political Reality

**Chapter 2:**
> "Let me be direct about something the standards will not tell you. They will not tell you how to run a SWIFT workshop with a room of twenty senior bankers who do not want to be there. They will not tell you what to do when a business unit head tells you, in front of the Chief Risk Officer, that the risk you have identified is not material because it has never crystallised. They will not tell you how to explain to a board member why the institution's risk identification process cannot be reduced to a dashboard with three traffic lights."

**Chapter 6 — The First Workshop:**
> "My first risk identification workshop at a European G-SIB, in late 2016, followed a pattern I would come to recognise as universal. I had prepared carefully. I had read the guidance on SWIFT and Delphi. I had designed a structured agenda. I had sent pre-reading materials to participants two weeks in advance. I had booked a meeting room with whiteboards and flip charts. I arrived confident that the session would produce a rigorous, comprehensive list of material risks.
>
> It did not.
>
> What it produced was a list of risks the participants were already comfortable discussing. Credit risk. Market risk. Operational risk. Liquidity risk. The categories were familiar. The descriptions were generic. The conversation was polite, structured, and shallow.
>
> No one mentioned business model risk. No one mentioned strategic risk. No one mentioned counterparty concentration in prime brokerage. No one mentioned the risk that the institution's funded pension schemes might become materially underfunded in a sustained low-rate environment. These were not hypothetical risks. They were real, they were material, and within five years, several of them would crystallise with significant financial and reputational consequences.
>
> But they were not identified in that workshop. Why? Because the workshop format — despite being based on a recognised structured technique — did not overcome the political, cultural, and cognitive barriers that prevent uncomfortable risks from being named."

**Chapter 6 — Being Told a Risk Doesn't Exist:**
> "I learned these skills through practice, not training. No course prepared me for the moment at Institution A when a business unit head told me, in front of twelve colleagues, that a risk I had identified did not exist. The issue was not technical — the risk was real, it was documented in the operational loss database, and it had materialised twice in the prior three years. The issue was political. Naming the risk implied that controls had failed, which implied that someone was accountable, which made the conversation uncomfortable.
>
> I did not argue. I asked a question: 'If this risk does not exist, how do we explain the three events in the loss database that correspond exactly to the scenario I described?' The business unit head paused. Someone else in the room said, 'Those were operational errors, not risks.' I asked, 'What is the difference?' No one answered.
>
> The risk stayed in the inventory."

**Chapter 13 — The Pattern of Stale Submissions:**
> "The pattern I encountered at Institution A when I received bottom-up submissions that had not changed in three years was not unique to that institution. It is a universal pathology. The cycle repeats, the submissions repeat, and no one questions whether the process is producing insight or merely evidence of compliance."

## Lessons Learned

**Chapter 5 — Experience at an Insurer:**
> "At a major European insurer, where I spent five years as Solvency II Project Manager before moving to the dealing floor, I watched the implementation of standards-based risk frameworks across dozens of entities in multiple jurisdictions. The institutions that succeeded were not those with the most sophisticated models or the largest risk teams. They were the institutions that built the process with clarity about what the standards required, what they recommended, and what they permitted."

**Chapter 6 — Workshop Redesign:**
> "I walked out of that workshop knowing that the entire approach had to be redesigned. The methodology that follows — structured techniques, pre-meeting preparation, rotating facilitation, transparent capture — was built from that failure.
>
> The workshops that followed were different. Not because I had discovered a better technique — SWIFT and Delphi remain the foundation — but because I had learned that technique alone is insufficient. The process must be designed to surface the risks the institution does not want to name. It must create space for dissent. It must reward honesty. And it must be facilitated by someone who understands that the most important risks are often the ones that make the room uncomfortable."

**Chapter 11 — Quality Through Challenge:**
> "By the second cycle, business units understood that risk profiles with thin content would be challenged and returned. By the third cycle, the quality had improved materially. Not because the institution had hired better risk managers — the same people were doing the work — but because the expectations had been made explicit, the standards had been enforced, and the feedback loop had demonstrated that quality mattered."

**Chapter 14 — Technology Follows Methodology:**
> "Chapter 11 established a principle that bears repeating here: the inventory structure must be designed independently of the technology that will host it. The methodology defines what must be captured, how it must be organised, and what relationships must be made visible. The technology implements that design. If the technology drives the design, the result will be a system optimised for data capture rather than risk identification."

**Chapter 15 — Regulatory Divergence:**
> "At Institution A, this divergence was acute. The London operation was supervised by the PRA. The Swiss entity by FINMA. The US operations by the Fed. Each regulator had its own expectations for risk identification, its own reporting formats, its own examination priorities. The institution could have responded by building three separate processes — one for each jurisdiction. Some institutions do exactly that.
>
> It would have been a mistake. Not because it would have been inefficient — though it would have been — but because it would have fragmented the institution's understanding of its own risks. A risk does not respect jurisdictional boundaries. Counterparty credit risk is counterparty credit risk whether it sits in London, Zurich, or New York. If the institution identifies it differently depending on which regulator is asking, it does not have a risk identification process. It has a regulatory reporting process."

**Chapter 16 — The Database as Forensic Tool:**
> "I built this database not as an academic exercise but as a forensic tool. Every case in the database is a failure. Every failure has a cause. And in nearly every case, the cause was visible — to someone, somewhere in the institution — before the loss crystallised.
>
> The question the database answers is this: If the risk was visible, why was it not identified, escalated, and addressed? The answer, in the majority of cases, falls into one of three categories: the institution did not have a systematic risk identification process; the institution had a process, but it was not designed to surface uncomfortable risks; or the institution had a process, the risk was identified, and it was ignored."

**Chapter 16 — The Final Statement:**
> "I have built it twice — at two globally significant institutions — and studied the failures that demonstrate why it is necessary. The methodology works. But it works only if the institution has the governance structures, the cultural norms, and the institutional commitment to act on what it reveals.
>
> Building the process is the easy part. Sustaining it — protecting it from commercial pressure, from political interference, from the institutional tendency to hear only the risks it is comfortable hearing — that is the hard part. And that is the part no methodology can solve."

## Summary: The Core Experience

Across 16 chapters, Rory Roberts describes building risk identification processes from scratch at two globally significant institutions:

1. **Institution A (European G-SIB, 2016-2017)**: Credit Suisse, where he served as Global Head of Risk Identification
2. **Institution B (UK-regulated international banking group, 2023-2024)**: Standard Chartered, where he served as Risk Identification Lead

He also references earlier career experiences:
- **Hypo Real Estate (2007-2008)**: Watched the institution collapse from the inside during the GFC
- **Zurich Insurance (5 years)**: Solvency II Project Manager, implementing standards-based frameworks
- **Various institutions**: 20+ years of direct experience in risk management

The recurring themes across all anecdotes:
- **The silence**: Institutions cannot answer basic questions about how they identify risks
- **The gap**: Measurement exists, identification does not
- **The politics**: Naming risks makes people uncomfortable; the most important risks are the ones the institution doesn't want to hear
- **The quality problem**: Copy-paste compliance theatre instead of genuine assessment
- **The fragmentation**: Processes operate in parallel universes with no integration
- **The lesson**: Building the methodology is easy; sustaining it against institutional resistance is the hard part
