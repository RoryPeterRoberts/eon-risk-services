# Research Extraction: Article 2
# "The One Thing Regulators Always Find Missing"
# Category: Regulatory | Slug: what-regulators-always-find-missing

**Source:** *Bank Risk Identification: The Complete Methodology* (sanitized chapters 01, 02, 03, 07, 15, 16)

**Article Thesis:** Across multiple jurisdictions (PRA, EBA, ECB, Fed, OCC), the same finding keeps appearing in supervisory reviews -- banks cannot demonstrate a documented, repeatable methodology for how they identify risks. They can show the risk register. They can show assessment scores. They cannot show the process that produced them.

---

## 1. Regulatory Framework References

### 1.1 BCBS Corporate Governance Principles (BCBS 328, July 2015)

**Principle 7** -- the foundational global mandate for risk identification:

> "Risks should be identified, monitored and controlled on an ongoing bank-wide and individual entity basis."
> -- Ch15, quoting BCBS 328, Principle 7, paragraph 100

The author's analysis of the four operative words (Ch15):

> "Four words in that sentence do significant work. *Ongoing* means not annual -- it requires continuous or at minimum quarterly identification. *Bank-wide* means consolidated, across every legal entity, subsidiary, and branch. *Individual entity basis* means the enterprise view does not excuse the absence of entity-level identification. And *identified* is listed first, before monitoring and controlling, because identification is the prerequisite for everything that follows."

**Principle 7 also requires** identification of risks arising from mergers and acquisitions, new products, and changes to organisational structure (BCBS 328, paragraphs 101-103). These are event-driven triggers, not discretionary. (Ch15)

**Principle 7 paragraphs 107-122** detail risk management requirements; **Principle 1 paragraphs 23-43** cover the Board's overall responsibilities including approval of risk management strategies; **Principle 8 paragraphs 123-128** require risk communication to the Board in a timely, accurate, and understandable manner; **Principle 9 paragraphs 129-138** require identification of compliance risks. (Ch2)

> "Together, these principles create a comprehensive regulatory expectation for risk identification that goes well beyond what most banks currently deliver." -- Ch2

The BCBS principles also require the risk management function's **independence** from business lines:

> "For risk identification, this creates a structural requirement: the people conducting the identification must have the authority and independence to identify risks that the business might prefer to ignore." -- Ch2

### 1.2 BCBS 239 -- Principles for Effective Risk Data Aggregation and Risk Reporting (January 2013)

Published with a compliance deadline of January 2016 for G-SIBs. Remains one of the most frequently cited areas of supervisory concern a decade later. (Ch15)

> "The reason is straightforward: if data cannot be aggregated accurately and quickly, risks remain invisible." -- Ch15

**Principle 4 (Completeness)** requires banks to capture and aggregate risk data across business line, legal entity, asset type, industry, region, and other groupings. (Ch15)

**Principle 7 (Accuracy)** requires data reliability. (Ch15)

The standard requires **ad-hoc reporting capability** -- generating aggregate risk information during stress periods. (Ch15)

### 1.3 BCBS Principles for the Sound Management of Operational Risk (PSMOR)

Originally BCBS 195 (June 2011), revised March 2021 (BCBS d515).

Mandates specific identification instruments: RCSA, KRIs, external loss data analysis, business process mapping, and event management (Principles 5-7). (Ch15)

The 2021 revision introduces a **near-miss requirement** -- identifying events that did not result in financial loss but exposed a vulnerability (Principle 6, paragraph 6.3):

> "This shifts identification from lagging indicators to leading indicators." -- Ch15

Heavy emphasis on ICT and third-party risk identification in the 2021 revision. (Ch15)

### 1.4 PRA SS31/15 -- ICAAP Supervisory Statement

Originally issued November 2015, most recently updated December 2023. (Ch15)

The PRA views stress testing as a **primary method of risk identification**, not merely a capital calculation tool:

> "By subjecting the business model to severe but plausible scenarios, banks identify latent risks invisible in benign conditions. Reverse stress testing -- identifying the scenarios that would render the business model non-viable -- is explicitly expected." -- Ch15

**Capital consequences for deficient identification:**

> "The PRA can impose Pillar 2A capital add-ons with scalars up to 40% of the Pillar 2A requirement for firms whose risk identification is assessed as inadequate. This is not a theoretical penalty. Institutions I have worked with have received supervisory feedback citing specific gaps in their identification process -- risks that should have been in the inventory but were not, risk categories that lacked adequate coverage, enterprise-level concentration risks that were identified at entity level but not aggregated." -- Ch15

### 1.5 Fed SR 15-18 -- CCAR Capital Planning (December 2015)

> "Its requirements transform risk identification from a periodic exercise into a continuous discipline with direct capital consequences. Unlike many other jurisdictions where annual identification cycles are the norm, SR 15-18 expects a dynamic, quarterly risk identification process that feeds directly into capital planning." -- Ch15

The **Material Risk Inventory** is the centrepiece:

> "For every risk in the inventory, the bank must map it to the stress test: is it captured in the scenario? Is it captured in the P&L model? Or does it require a separate capital add-on? This 'map to capital' requirement ensures no identified risk is left unfunded." -- Ch15

The consequence of failure is the **CCAR qualitative objection**:

> "A qualitative objection restricts capital distributions: share buybacks and dividend increases are blocked. For a publicly traded US bank, this is a market event." -- Ch15

### 1.6 OCC Heightened Standards (12 CFR Part 30, Appendix D)

Effective 1 January 2014.

Two distinctive features (Ch15):

1. **Front-line units are responsible for assessing material risks in their activities.** They cannot delegate identification to the risk function.
2. **Effective challenge** -- Independent Risk Management must identify and assess material aggregate risks independently. If its assessment diverges from the front line's, the divergence must be reported.

> "This dual identification system is structurally equivalent to the methodology's top-down/bottom-up reconciliation." -- Ch15

The OCC codifies the Three Lines Model with "three units" (front line, independent risk management, internal audit) with explicit risk identification duties for each line (12 CFR Part 30, Appendix D, Section III). (Ch3)

### 1.7 EBA Internal Governance Guidelines (EBA/GL/2021/05)

Effective 31 December 2021. Three direct requirements (Ch15):

1. **Holistic view** -- Section 17 (paragraphs 72-74) requires risk identification to aggregate across legal entities and risk types to identify cross-cutting themes.
2. **Detection of unapproved exposures** -- Section 20.4 (paragraph 119) places a specific duty on the risk management function to identify instances where the business has taken risk outside agreed appetite.
3. **ML/TF risk integration** -- The 2021 revision explicitly integrates financial crime risk into the general risk management framework.

Also mandates risk identification in M&A and new product approval contexts. (Ch15)

### 1.8 ECB SREP Methodology

> "The ECB examines not only what risks have been identified but how the institution determined which are material, what data quality underpins the assessments, and whether the identification process captures all relevant risk categories." -- Ch15

> "Institutions that cannot demonstrate compliance face direct supervisory consequences: increased capital requirements, restrictions on activities, and mandatory remediation programmes." -- Ch15

### 1.9 ISO 31000:2018

**Clause 5 (Process)** -- Section 5.4.2 (Risk Identification) requires identification of sources of risk, areas of impacts, events and their causes, and potential consequences. Aims to generate a comprehensive list of risks. Requires risks be identified whether or not their source is under the control of the organisation. (Ch2)

**Principle e** -- "Is systematic, structured and timely":

> "Ad hoc identification is not compliant. The process must be repeatable, documented, and executed on a defined schedule." -- Ch2

**Principle j** -- "Is dynamic, iterative and responsive to change":

> "Risk identification is not a one-time activity. The standard requires a process that responds to changes in the internal and external environment -- which means event-driven updates, not just scheduled cycles." -- Ch2

### 1.10 ISO 31010:2019

**Section 5.2** requires that the output of risk identification includes a structured record not just of the risks identified but also of the information used, the assumptions made, and the limitations of the analysis:

> "This requirement for transparency about methodology limitations is critical -- it is the difference between a process that can be audited and improved, and one that produces an opaque list of risks with no traceability." -- Ch2

### 1.11 COSO ERM (2004/2017)

**Component 3 -- Event Identification**: Requires identification of internal and external events that affect the achievement of objectives. Distinguishes between risks (negative impact) and opportunities (positive impact). Requires identification of event interdependencies. (Ch2)

**Four Objective Categories** (Strategic, Operations, Reporting, Compliance):

> "Most banks, when they conduct risk identification, focus primarily on financial risks -- credit, market, liquidity -- and operational risks. They frequently miss risks to strategic objectives (business model disruption, competitive erosion) and risks to reporting integrity (data quality degradation, regulatory reporting errors) because their identification process does not systematically ask: What could prevent us from achieving our strategic objectives? What could compromise the reliability of our reporting?" -- Ch2

### 1.12 FCA Five Conduct Questions

> "Where prudential frameworks ask 'what could go wrong financially?', the FCA asks 'what incentives could lead to poor customer outcomes?'" -- Ch15

Requires business-level identification of conduct drivers at desk-by-desk granularity. (Ch7, Ch15)

### 1.13 Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SM&CR)

Implemented for banks from 7 March 2016; extended to all FCA solo-regulated firms 9 December 2019. (Ch15)

> "If a risk materialises in a business unit and the Senior Manager responsible cannot demonstrate reasonable steps to identify it, they face individual enforcement action -- fines, prohibition from the industry, and public censure. The defence is proving 'reasonable steps' -- and a robust, documented risk identification process is the primary evidence of reasonable steps." -- Ch15

### 1.14 DORA (EU 2022/2554)

Entered into force January 2025.

> "Article 8 requires financial entities to 'identify, classify and adequately document all ICT-supported business functions, roles and responsibilities... and identify all ICT assets.' This is not risk-level identification -- it is asset-level identification." -- Ch15

### 1.15 PRA Step-In Risk (PS5/25, 2025)

> "Requires the explicit identification of financial support obligations that extend beyond contractual requirements. These are the reputational compulsions to support unconsolidated entities -- sponsored funds, structured vehicles, affiliated entities -- during stress. Identifying these non-contractual obligations is difficult precisely because they do not appear in legal agreements or financial statements." -- Ch15

### 1.16 EBA ESG Risk Guidelines (EBA/GL/2024/01)

Applicable from 2026/2027:

> "The core identification requirement is the mapping of transmission channels: exactly how physical risks (floods, storms) and transition risks (policy changes, technology shifts, market preferences) impact counterparties' ability to repay or the institution's own operations. This requires new data -- geolocation data for flood risk, carbon intensity data for transition risk -- and new time horizons. Risk identification must span short-term (1-3 years), medium-term (3-10 years), and long-term (10-30 years) horizons." -- Ch15

## 2. The Gap: What Regulators Expect vs What Banks Actually Have

### 2.1 The Fundamental Gap -- No Process Exists

The author's first-day experience at a European G-SIB (Ch1):

> "On my first day, I asked what I thought was a simple question: How does this institution currently identify its risks? The answer was silence. Not hostile silence -- confused silence. People looked at each other. Someone mentioned the risk register. Someone else pointed to the annual ICAAP process. A third person described a workshop they had run two years ago. But no one could describe a coherent, end-to-end process for how the bank identified, catalogued, and validated the risks it was exposed to. There was no methodology. No documented process. No named owner. No audit trail showing how the risk register had been built or why certain risks were on it and others were not."

> "This institution was, at the time, one of the largest banks in the world. And it could not explain how it identified its risks."

This was not an isolated case (Ch1):

> "I wish I could say this was unusual. It was not. When I moved to a UK-regulated international banking group in 2023 as Risk Identification Lead in the Enterprise Risk Management department, the situation was better -- there were elements of a process -- but the same fundamental gaps existed. When I speak to CROs, risk leads, and consultants across the industry, the story is almost always the same. Most banks have a risk register. Very few have a risk identification process. And the difference between those two things is the difference between surviving a regulatory review and failing one."

### 2.2 The Investment Gap

> "If you look at where banks invest their time, talent, and technology, risk identification barely registers. The measurement functions are well-resourced: credit risk modelling teams with dozens of quants, market risk systems running millions of simulations per day, operational risk databases painstakingly cataloguing loss events. Capital calculation engines are sophisticated. Reporting frameworks are elaborate. Stress testing programmes consume thousands of person-hours per year." -- Ch1

> "But the question that precedes all of this -- What are the risks? -- is typically addressed in a two-day workshop once a year, run by someone who has never been trained in facilitation techniques, using a brainstorming methodology that produces groupthink rather than genuine risk intelligence. The output is a spreadsheet. The spreadsheet becomes the risk register. The risk register is updated annually -- which in practice means it is updated when someone remembers to do it, or when a regulator asks." -- Ch1

> "Banks have invested billions in measuring and managing risks, but almost nothing in finding them in the first place. It is as if a hospital had the world's best surgeons and diagnostic equipment but no process for examining patients. The treatments are excellent. The diagnosis is guesswork." -- Ch1

### 2.3 The Framework Gap -- Missing Organisational Infrastructure

> "This is where most banks fall down. They may have a reasonable risk identification methodology on paper, but the framework is missing. There is no named owner. No allocated budget. No integration with strategic planning. No mechanism for monitoring whether the process is actually working. The methodology exists in a vacuum, and the framework that should sustain it does not exist." -- Ch2

### 2.4 The Standards Test -- What Regulators Actually Check

> "In every regulatory examination I have sat through -- PRA, FINMA, the Fed, the EBA -- the conversation about risk identification follows the same pattern. The supervisor opens a copy of the institution's risk identification process document, turns to the first page, and asks a deceptively simple question: What standards is this process built on?" -- Ch2

> "The question is not academic. It is a test. The regulator is establishing whether the institution's process is grounded in internationally recognised standards or whether someone has invented something from scratch -- and whether the people responsible for the process understand the difference." -- Ch2

### 2.5 The Traceability Gap

> "Most institutions cannot do this. They have a risk identification process. They have a long list of regulations they are subject to. What they lack is the bridge between the two -- a systematic mapping that demonstrates, for each applicable framework, exactly how the process satisfies its requirements." -- Ch15

At the PRA examination (Ch15):

> "At a UK-regulated international banking group in 2023, I sat across the table from a PRA supervisor who opened a thick examination file and asked a single question: 'Walk me through how your risk identification process satisfies SS31/15.'"

> "The supervisor was not asking whether we identified risks. Every bank identifies risks. She was asking whether the process that produced those identifications could be traced -- requirement by requirement -- to the specific provisions of the regulatory framework under which the institution was supervised."

### 2.6 The Compliance Theatre Problem

The bottom-up process rollforward (Ch7):

> "In early 2017, several months into my role as Global Head of Risk Identification at a European G-SIB, I received the first round of bottom-up risk assessments from the business units... I opened the first one -- a major trading division -- and compared it against the prior year's submission... It was identical. Not similar. Identical. The same risks, in the same order, with the same scores, the same control descriptions, the same owner names -- one of whom had left the firm six months earlier. The dates had been updated. Nothing else had."

> "This is compliance theatre: the appearance of risk identification without the substance." -- Ch7

> "A bottom-up process that produces compliance theatre is worse than no process at all, because it creates a documented record suggesting that identification has occurred when it has not. The institution believes it has a comprehensive risk inventory. It does not. It has last year's inventory with this year's date." -- Ch7

### 2.7 The Annual Cycle Problem

> "One of the most common governance failures in risk identification is treating it as an annual event. The risk register is updated once a year, typically in the weeks before the Board needs to approve the ICAAP or the annual report, and then sits untouched until the next cycle. In a world where risks evolve continuously -- where a pandemic, a geopolitical crisis, a cyber attack, or a market dislocation can fundamentally reshape the risk landscape in days -- an annual cycle is not governance. It is a filing exercise." -- Ch3

### 2.8 Output vs Process Distinction

> "A process that runs once a year (violating Principle j), uses only top-down workshops (violating Principle f), and produces outputs that no one acts on (violating Principle c) will fail regulatory scrutiny, even if the content of the risk register looks reasonable." -- Ch2

> "The regulator does not penalise the crystallisation of risk -- risk is inherent in banking. The regulator penalises the failure to identify it." -- Ch15

## 4. Case Studies -- Identification Failures Leading to Regulatory Consequences

### 4.1 HBOS -- Risk Identifier Fired for Doing His Job

Paul Moore, Group Head of Regulatory Risk, identified concentration risk in the corporate banking division. He was fired. Three years later, HBOS collapsed with losses exceeding GBP 10 billion. (Ch3)

> "Moore did what a Head of Risk is supposed to do. He raised the concern. He documented it. He escalated it to the Board. The Board's response was not to investigate the risk. It was to remove the person who had identified it." -- Ch3

> "The HBOS case is not primarily a story about credit risk. It is a story about governance. The risk was identified. The methodology, such as it was, worked. What failed was everything around it: the escalation path was blocked, the Board received filtered information, the person responsible for independent challenge was removed, and the cultural incentive to suppress uncomfortable findings overwhelmed the structural obligation to act on them." -- Ch3

Source: House of Commons Treasury Committee, *Banking Crisis: Reforming Corporate Governance and Pay in the City*, HC 519, 15 May 2009; FSA, *The Failure of HBOS plc*, November 2015.

### 4.2 Standard Chartered -- Identification Overruled

> "Standard Chartered's compliance function identified the sanctions risk associated with processing approximately $250 billion in transactions for Iranian clients. The risk was identified at the operational level. But senior management in London overruled the compliance function, treating the Iranian business as commercially important." -- Ch3

Result: $667 million fine and compliance monitorship. (Ch3, Ch16)

Source: NYDFS Consent Order, 6 August 2012; DOJ Deferred Prosecution Agreement, 10 December 2012.

### 4.3 Wells Fargo -- Risk Signals Treated as HR Issues

> "At Wells Fargo, front-line employees knew that the aggressive cross-selling targets were driving fraudulent account openings. Many raised complaints. But the governance structure treated these as HR matters -- employee dissatisfaction -- rather than risk signals." -- Ch3

Result: 3.5 million potentially unauthorised accounts; $3 billion DOJ settlement; Federal Reserve asset-growth restriction 2018-2025. (Ch3)

### 4.4 AIB/Allfirst -- No Standardised Bottom-Up Process

John Rusnak concealed $691 million in losses through fictitious option trades. (Ch7)

> "Every element of this failure maps to a bottom-up identification deficiency: No standardised template... No structured technique applied... No independent control effectiveness assessment... Geographic separation." -- Ch7

> "What was missing: A standardised bottom-up process requiring Allfirst to complete the same risk assessment template as every other AIB business unit, using the same taxonomy, the same scoring methodology, and the same control effectiveness assessment." -- Ch7

### 4.5 UBS/Adoboli -- RCSA Failed to Identify Control Gap

$2.3 billion in losses from unauthorised trading, nine years after the Rusnak case. (Ch7)

> "The RCSA for the Delta One desk -- which UBS undoubtedly conducted, given the regulatory requirements -- had not identified that the control framework assumed trade authenticity rather than testing it. The control assessment said reconciliation processes existed. It did not ask whether those processes could detect deliberately fabricated trades." -- Ch7

FSA fined UBS GBP 29.7 million. (Ch7)

### 4.6 Societe Generale/Kerviel -- Process Frequency and Role Design Failure

EUR 4.9 billion loss from unauthorized trading. (Ch3)

> "The governance lesson is not just about segregation of duties -- though that was plainly inadequate. It is about the assumption embedded in the risk identification process that certain risk scenarios were implausible. The process did not contemplate a trader who knew both sides of the control chain. There was no mechanism for identifying this as a risk, because the governance framework had not defined it as something to look for." -- Ch3

### 4.7 Credit Suisse/Mozambique -- No New Product Risk Identification

$475 million coordinated global settlement (SEC, DOJ, FCA). (Ch16)

> "The sovereign lending risk identification process focused on credit analysis of the Mozambican state. It did not identify the corruption risk embedded in the transaction structure, despite red flags that a structured new-product approval process would have surfaced." -- Ch16

> "The Mozambique loans were a new structure. They were never assessed." -- Ch16

### 4.8 Cooperative Bank -- Board Could Not Challenge

> "When the Co-op Bank attempted to acquire Britannia Building Society in 2009, the merger due diligence failed to identify the true scale of Britannia's commercial property loan impairments. But the deeper failure was structural: the Co-op's mutual governance model had placed individuals without banking expertise in Board oversight roles. The chairman at the time had no financial services background. The Board could not challenge what it could not understand." -- Ch3

Result: GBP 1.5 billion rescue, bondholder bail-in. Source: Kelly Review, 30 April 2014.

### 4.9 Silicon Valley Bank -- Failed to Re-Identify During Rate Cycle

> "Silicon Valley Bank failed to re-identify interest rate risk as the Federal Reserve raised rates by 500 basis points over eighteen months -- a rate-rising cycle that was publicly signalled, widely discussed, and directly relevant to a bank holding a $91 billion HTM securities portfolio. The CRO position was vacant for eight months during this period." -- Ch16

### 4.10 Alpha Bank/Bank of Cyprus -- Regulatory Framework Masked Concentration

> "Under the Capital Requirements Regulation, eurozone sovereign debt carried a zero risk weight -- a regulatory treatment that, in effect, told risk identification processes there was nothing to identify. Alpha did not flag domestic sovereign debt as a concentration risk because the regulatory framework explicitly stated it was not one." -- Ch16

> "A risk identification process that simply accepts zero risk weights for home-sovereign debt is not identifying risks -- it is transcribing regulatory assumptions." -- Ch16

### 4.11 Bear Stearns -- Ignored Its Own Warning

> "In June 2007, two Bear Stearns hedge funds collapsed from CDO exposure. The parent bank treated this as an isolated subsidiary event -- a contained loss that did not require re-examination of the bank's own retained structured credit positions. Nine months later, Bear Stearns itself collapsed from the same concentration risk." -- Ch16

## 5. What Good Looks Like -- What a Regulator Would Want to See

### 5.1 Documented Standards Traceability

> "When a regulator examines your risk identification process and asks what standards it is built on, you need to be able to point to each phase and say: This phase implements these specific provisions of these specific standards. That traceability is what distinguishes a defensible methodology from an ad hoc collection of risk workshops." -- Ch2

> "I could. Not because I had prepared for the question, but because the methodology had been designed with regulatory traceability built in from the start. The risk identification process at the second institution mapped every major regulatory requirement to a specific phase and activity within the methodology. When the supervisor asked, I did not need to construct an answer -- I needed only to open the regulatory mapping appendix." -- Ch15

### 5.2 The Full Standards Architecture

A process built on all four frameworks simultaneously (Ch2):

| Framework | What It Provides | Gap If Missing |
|---|---|---|
| **ISO 31000** | The architecture -- principles, framework, process structure | No systematic process design; ad hoc approach |
| **ISO 31010** | The techniques -- which methods to use | Reliance on a single technique; systematic blind spots |
| **COSO ERM** | The enterprise lens -- four objective categories, completeness check | Risks identified only for financial/operational categories; strategic and reporting risks missed |
| **BCBS Principles** | The banking mandate -- bank-wide coverage, ongoing frequency, Board responsibility | Process not designed for regulatory examination; gaps in governance and frequency |

### 5.3 Named Ownership and Governance

> "Ambiguity in role definition is one of the most common governance failures in risk identification. When everyone is responsible, no one is accountable." -- Ch3

Seven specific roles must be defined: Board Risk Committee, CRO, Risk Identification Lead, Business Unit Heads, Risk Assessors, Risk Owners, Front-Line Employees. (Ch3)

> "Every risk in the inventory has one named owner. This is a deliberate design choice. Committee ownership dilutes accountability." -- Ch3

### 5.4 Four Frequencies of Identification

The methodology requires (Ch3):

1. **Annual full re-identification** -- complete process from scratch, not rolled forward
2. **Quarterly re-identification** -- consistent with Fed SR 15-18; active re-identification, not just re-assessment
3. **Monthly KRI monitoring** -- leading indicators against defined thresholds
4. **Event-driven updates** -- immediate, outside any regular cycle

### 5.5 Multi-Technique Approach

> "A bank risk identification process cannot rely on a single technique. Brainstorming alone will not identify emerging risks -- it amplifies groupthink. Checklists alone will not identify novel risks -- they only find what is already on the list. Scenario analysis alone will not identify operational risks in individual business units -- it operates at too high a level." -- Ch2

### 5.6 Top-Down and Bottom-Up with Reconciliation

> "The reconciliation between top-down and bottom-up outputs is where the real value of risk identification lives." -- Ch7

> "Most banks fail. They perform either top-down or bottom-up, but not both -- and they almost never iterate between them." -- Ch7

### 5.7 The Regulatory Traceability Matrix

The sixteen-framework mapping to the six-phase methodology (Ch15):

| Framework | Phase 1 | Phase 2 | Phase 3 | Phase 4 | Phase 5 | Phase 6 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| BCBS Principle 7 | Bank-wide scope | Entity/portfolio/BU coverage | -- | -- | Board reporting | Ongoing identification |
| BCBS 239 | Data infrastructure | -- | DQ Rating | 14-field metadata | Aggregated reporting | Ad-hoc capability |
| PRA SS31/15 | Context for stress testing | Comprehensive identification | Materiality, stress scenarios | -- | Capital planning, reverse stress | Ongoing + event-driven |
| Fed SR 15-18 | -- | Quarterly re-identification | -- | Material Risk Inventory | Map to capital/scenarios | Quarterly cycle |
| OCC Heightened Standards | -- | Front-line + IRM dual ID | -- | -- | Effective challenge reporting | Talent risk monitoring |

(Full matrix available in Chapter 15)

### 5.8 The Principal Risk Report to Board

> "The Board Risk Committee receives a comprehensive risk report at each meeting. This report contains: all material risks with current residual risk scores; the consequence dimension driving each impact score; trend direction for each risk; risks approaching or breaching appetite; new or emerging risks; risks removed or reclassified; the KRI dashboard; data quality distribution across the portfolio; the enterprise portfolio view showing aggregate position against appetite; and process performance indicators showing whether the identification process itself is functioning." -- Ch3

> "This is not a summary document that the CRO prepares from memory. It is drawn directly from the risk inventory. Every data point is traceable. Every score has a documented basis." -- Ch3

### 5.9 Three Forms of Independence

> "Structural independence. The Risk Identification Lead and the CRO function must not report to the business."
> "Operational independence. The risk identification process must be able to operate without requiring business unit cooperation to function."
> "Intellectual independence. The people conducting risk identification must be free to reach conclusions that the business does not agree with." -- Ch3

## 6. Practitioner Insights -- First-Person Observations

### 6.1 The Author's Core Thesis

> "Risk identification is the most important and least understood step in the entire risk management cycle." -- Ch1

> "After 20 years of doing this work at six institutions, I became convinced of two things. First, that risk identification is the most important and least understood step in the entire risk management cycle. And second, that there was no comprehensive methodology available anywhere -- no book, no standard, no framework -- that told a practitioner exactly how to build one." -- Ch1

### 6.2 Building from Scratch at a G-SIB

> "At Institution A, when I built the risk identification process in 2016, one of the first things I had to establish was the standards architecture. Not because I thought ISO 31000 or COSO ERM would tell me exactly how to identify risks in a global investment bank -- they would not -- but because every regulator with jurisdiction over the bank would assess our process against those standards." -- Ch2

> "If our methodology could not trace its key design decisions back to specific provisions of ISO 31000, ISO 31010, and COSO ERM, we would fail the first supervisory test before anyone even looked at the content." -- Ch2

### 6.3 The COSO Cube as Gap Detector

> "When I built the process at Institution A, we used the COSO cube as a completeness check. We mapped every identified risk against the matrix and looked for empty cells -- combinations of component, objective, and entity where no risks had been identified. The empty cells were not proof that no risks existed in those spaces. They were proof that we had not looked. And when we looked, we invariably found risks that the standard workshop-and-brainstorm approach had missed." -- Ch2

### 6.4 Establishing the Risk Identification Lead Mandate

> "At Institution A, when I took on this role, one of the first things I had to establish was that the Risk Identification Lead was not there to rubber-stamp what business units wanted to report. The role existed to ensure that the risk inventory reflected reality, not comfort. That required a clear mandate from the CRO, visible support from the Board Risk Committee, and a willingness to have uncomfortable conversations with senior people who did not appreciate being told that their risk self-assessments were incomplete." -- Ch3

### 6.5 The Material Risk Inventory and CCAR Integration

> "At Institution A, integrating the risk identification process with CCAR was a defining requirement. The CRO mandated that the Material Risk Inventory be the foundation for stress scenario design -- if we identified a risk as material, the scenario had to address it. If the scenario revealed a risk not in the inventory, the inventory had to be updated. This bidirectional linkage between identification and capital planning is what Chapter 12 described as the methodology's integration architecture." -- Ch15

### 6.6 Multi-Jurisdictional Reality

> "At a European G-SIB, when the London operation was building its risk identification process, we knew that the Swiss entity's supervisor -- FINMA -- conducted a SREP-equivalent assessment. The methodology had to satisfy that assessment alongside the Fed's CCAR requirements for the US operations and the PRA's expectations for the London branch." -- Ch15

> "At Institution A, this divergence was acute. The London operation was supervised by the PRA. The Swiss entity by FINMA. The US operations by the Fed. Different divisions maintained different numbers of risk categories -- different classifications for the same exposures. The regulatory mapping table was not an optional administrative exercise -- it was the mechanism that made multi-jurisdictional compliance possible without maintaining separate risk identification processes for each jurisdiction." -- Ch15

### 6.7 Standards vs Craft

> "Standards provide the architecture. They do not provide the craft... They will not tell you how to run a SWIFT workshop with a room of twenty senior bankers who do not want to be there. They will not tell you how to handle the political dynamics when the Chief Risk Officer and the Head of Investment Banking disagree about whether a risk is material." -- Ch2

### 6.8 Fixing the Bottom-Up Process

> "When I redesigned the bottom-up process at Institution A, I introduced a requirement that every business unit must identify at least three new risks or materially changed risks each annual cycle. Not because three is a magic number, but because the external environment, the business model, the regulatory landscape, and the technology infrastructure all change continuously. An honest assessment will always find something new. If it does not, the assessor has not looked." -- Ch7

### 6.9 Data Quality Defaulting

> "In my experience, business units default to 'Medium' for everything. The Risk Identification Lead must challenge this -- a risk with no quantitative data and no historical precedent is not 'Medium' quality. It is 'Low' or 'Very Low,' and that information matters." -- Ch7

## Cross-Reference: The Core Regulatory Argument for the Article

The article's thesis -- that regulators consistently find risk identification *process* missing, not risk identification *output* -- is supported by these converging lines of evidence from the book:

1. **Every major framework requires a documented process, not just a register.** ISO 31000 Principle e requires systematic, structured, timely processes. BCBS Principle 7 requires ongoing, bank-wide identification. Fed SR 15-18 requires a quarterly Material Risk Inventory tied to capital planning. PRA SS31/15 requires comprehensive identification including stress testing. OCC Heightened Standards require dual identification (front-line + independent) with effective challenge.

2. **The author's direct experience at two G-SIBs confirms the gap.** Neither institution had a documented identification process when the author arrived. The first had no methodology, no owner, no audit trail. The second had elements but fundamental gaps.

3. **179 bank failures validate the consequence.** In every case, the risk was identifiable. What was missing was the process.

4. **The penalties are not for risk crystallisation -- they are for failure to identify.** PRA imposes Pillar 2A scalars up to 40% for inadequate identification. Fed issues qualitative objections blocking capital distributions. SM&CR holds individuals personally liable for failure to take reasonable steps to identify risks.

5. **The finding is the same across all jurisdictions.** PRA, FINMA, Fed, EBA -- the conversation follows the same pattern. The regulator asks how risks were identified, not just what risks exist.
