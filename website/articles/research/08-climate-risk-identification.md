# Research Material: Article 8 — Climate Risk Identification: It's Not a Standalone Category

**Category:** Industry
**Slug:** `climate-risk-identification`
**Source:** *Bank Risk Identification: The Complete Methodology* (sanitized book chapters)

---

## Article Thesis

Why treating climate as a separate risk type misses the point. Climate risk is not a standalone category -- it is a transmission channel that amplifies existing risk types (credit, market, operational, reputational). The right approach is to map how climate transmits through your existing taxonomy, not to create a separate climate risk register.

---

## 1. Climate as Transmission Channel

### The Core Principle: Climate Risks Manifest Through Existing Risk Types

From **Chapter 5 (Foundation Setting)**, the book is explicit that climate is not standalone:

> "Climate risks are not a standalone risk category in the taxonomy. They manifest through existing risk types -- credit risk from impaired collateral values, market risk from stranded asset repricing, operational risk from extreme weather events, reputational risk from perceived inaction. The institution must map each climate transmission channel to its internal risk taxonomy and include identified climate-related risks in the central risk inventory, feeding through to the risk appetite statement."
> -- Chapter 5, Climate and ESG Risk Identification Framework

### The Six Transmission Channels

Chapter 5 defines a structured framework of six transmission channels through which climate risks enter the banking system:

| Channel | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical risk -- acute** | Discrete climate events | Extreme weather damaging collateral, supply chain disruption |
| **Physical risk -- chronic** | Long-term climate shifts | Sea-level rise reducing property values, water stress affecting agricultural lending |
| **Transition risk -- policy** | Climate policy changes | Carbon pricing, emissions restrictions, stranded asset regulation |
| **Transition risk -- technology** | Low-carbon technology shifts | Disruption of fossil-fuel-dependent sectors in the lending book |
| **Transition risk -- market** | Changing market preferences | Shifts in consumer and investor sentiment, ESG-linked repricing |
| **Transition risk -- reputational** | Perceived inaction on climate | Loss of customers and investors due to insufficient climate commitments |

> -- Chapter 5, Climate and ESG Risk Identification Framework table

### Unique Characteristics Requiring Dedicated Framework

Chapter 5 explains why climate risk requires a specific identification framework despite not being a standalone category:

> "Climate-related financial risks are assessed through a dedicated framework due to their unique characteristics: long time horizons that exceed normal business planning periods, non-linear transmission mechanisms, and pervasive effects that cut across all traditional risk categories."
> -- Chapter 5, Environmental and Climate section

### Time Horizon Challenge

> "Climate risks must be assessed across three time horizons: short-term (1-3 years), medium-term (3-10 years), and long-term (10-30 years). The long-term horizon exceeds the normal business planning period but must be considered for strategic and capital planning purposes. A bank with significant exposure to commercial real estate in coastal regions may face minimal short-term physical risk but material long-term risk from sea-level rise and changing flood insurance availability. That long-term risk affects the credit quality of the portfolio today, because it affects the residual value of the collateral over the life of the loan."
> -- Chapter 5, Climate and ESG Risk Identification Framework

## 3. Taxonomy Integration

### Climate as L1 with Transmission Through Existing Categories

The book's taxonomy (Chapter 4) includes climate as a distinct L1 category, but this is a design choice to ensure completeness -- not to create a silo:

> "Climate and Environmental Risk: Risk from physical and transition climate impacts across all transmission channels."
> -- Chapter 4, L1 Taxonomy

The critical insight is the phrase "across all transmission channels." The L1 category exists to ensure coverage (the "collectively exhaustive" MECE requirement), but the actual risk identification work happens by mapping climate through credit, market, operational, liquidity, strategic, and reputational risk.

### The MECE Problem Applied to Climate

Chapter 4 establishes the MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) principle for taxonomy design:

> "Mutually Exclusive -- each identified risk maps to one and only one taxonomy node. A risk cannot simultaneously be classified as credit risk and market risk."
> -- Chapter 4

This creates an inherent tension for climate risk: a climate-driven credit impairment is simultaneously a credit risk and a climate risk. The book resolves this by making climate an L1 category that captures the driver/source, while the specific risk manifestation (credit, market, operational) is the primary classification for assessment and capital purposes.

### The Collectively Exhaustive Imperative

Chapter 4's warning about risks that fall outside the taxonomy applies directly to the climate debate:

> "If a risk type does not exist in the taxonomy, the institution is structurally incapable of identifying it -- no matter how sophisticated its workshops, how rigorous its bottom-up templates, or how capable its people. The taxonomy defines the universe of what can be found."
> -- Chapter 4

This is the argument for including climate as a named L1 category -- not because it is standalone, but because without it, climate transmission channels might not be systematically assessed.

### How Climate Entered Taxonomies: The Evolutionary Pattern

Chapter 4 describes the historical pattern of how emerging risks enter taxonomies:

> "Climate risk did not exist as a distinct taxonomy entry in most banks before 2015. Cyber risk was buried inside 'IT risk' or 'operational risk -- systems' until regulators began requiring dedicated assessment. Conduct risk was invisible until a series of scandals made it impossible to ignore."
> -- Chapter 4, The Emerging Risk Challenge

> "In each case, the risk existed long before the taxonomy recognised it. Institutions that added the category early -- because their horizon scanning or Delphi processes identified the emerging threat -- were better positioned to identify specific exposures before they crystallised. Institutions that waited for regulatory mandate or industry loss events were, by definition, late."
> -- Chapter 4

### The Parallel with Conduct Risk

The PPI case study in Chapter 4 provides an instructive parallel for climate risk:

> "The risk identification frameworks in use at the time did not include 'customer suitability' as a material risk category. Conduct risk, as a distinct taxonomy entry, did not exist in most institutions' risk classifications."
> -- Chapter 4

Climate risk followed the same pattern: it manifested through existing risk types (credit losses from stranded assets, market losses from carbon repricing) but was invisible until the taxonomy was updated to include it explicitly.

### The Reputational and Sustainability Risk Assessment (RSR)

Chapter 7 describes the RSR specialist sub-process, which is directly relevant to climate risk identification:

> "RSR identifies risks affecting public perception and long-term viability, including ESG factors. Reputational risk is unusual in the taxonomy because it rarely materialises independently -- it is almost always a consequence of another risk crystallising."
> -- Chapter 7, Specialist Sub-Process 3

> "The ESG dimension -- environmental, social, and governance factors -- maps to the climate risk transmission channels established in Chapter 5 and the sustainability taxonomy categories from Chapter 4."
> -- Chapter 7

This reinforces the transmission channel approach: ESG/climate risks are identified through the RSR process precisely because they transmit through existing risk types.

## 4. The Interaction Matrix Approach

### Why Risk Interaction Analysis Matters for Climate

Chapter 10 (Risk Interaction) establishes the methodology for mapping how risks amplify and trigger each other -- directly applicable to climate transmission channels:

> "Risk interaction analysis is the systematic examination of how risks relate to each other -- which risks trigger other risks, which risks amplify the impact of other risks, and where the institution's aggregate exposure exceeds what any individual risk score would suggest."
> -- Chapter 10

### The Interaction Matrix Structure

The interaction matrix maps directional relationships between all material risks:

> "The risk interaction matrix is a square matrix with the institution's material risks listed on both axes. For each pair of risks, the matrix records whether a directional relationship exists: does Risk A trigger, amplify, or have no effect on Risk B? The matrix is not symmetric."
> -- Chapter 10

Three relationship types are recorded:

| Relationship | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Triggers** | Crystallisation of Risk A directly causes Risk B to materialise | Credit losses on concentrated portfolio trigger counterparty confidence withdrawal (liquidity risk) |
| **Amplifies** | Crystallisation of Risk A increases the impact or likelihood of Risk B | Market volatility amplifies funding cost risk through collateral calls |
| **Correlated** | Risks A and B share a common driver and would crystallise simultaneously | Interest rate shock affecting both trading book positions and pension fund obligations |

> -- Chapter 10, Risk Interaction Matrix

### Climate Risk Application to the Interaction Matrix

Climate risk identification benefits directly from this framework. Each climate transmission channel can be mapped as a trigger or amplifier:

- Physical risk (acute) **triggers** operational risk (property damage, business disruption)
- Physical risk (chronic) **amplifies** credit risk (collateral value decline over time)
- Transition risk (policy) **triggers** market risk (stranded asset repricing)
- Transition risk (technology) **amplifies** strategic risk (business model disruption)
- Transition risk (reputational) **amplifies** reputational risk and liquidity risk (investor withdrawal)

### Propagation Nodes and Vulnerable Nodes

> "Risks with many outgoing trigger relationships are propagation nodes -- their crystallisation cascades across the portfolio. Risks with many incoming relationships are vulnerable nodes -- they are exposed to crystallisation from multiple sources."
> -- Chapter 10

Climate risk is a classic **propagation node** -- it triggers and amplifies multiple existing risk types simultaneously. The interaction matrix makes this visible and auditable.

### The Concentration Analysis Connection

Chapter 10's concentration analysis is directly relevant to climate:

> "Structural concentration is less visible but often more dangerous. This is concentration not in a single name but in a structural assumption -- a funding model, a correlation assumption, a regulatory interpretation, a technology platform."
> -- Chapter 10

Climate risk creates structural concentration: a bank with heavy exposure to fossil fuel sectors has a structural concentration in carbon-intensive assets that the interaction matrix and concentration analysis should surface.

### Identifying Hidden Concentration Through Climate Lens

> "The most dangerous concentrations are those disguised by apparent diversification."
> -- Chapter 10

This principle applies directly to climate: a portfolio that appears diversified across geographies and sectors may have hidden climate concentration if many of those exposures share a common climate vulnerability (e.g., coastal real estate across multiple countries, or multiple sectors dependent on carbon-intensive supply chains).

### The Lehman Analogy: Climate as Cascade Risk

The Lehman Brothers case study in Chapter 10 demonstrates the same cascade dynamic that climate risk creates:

> "Lehman Brothers did not fail because of credit risk. It did not fail because of liquidity risk, or market risk, or operational risk, or reputational risk. It failed because all five crystallised simultaneously, each amplifying the others in a chain reaction that no individual risk assessment had captured."
> -- Chapter 10, opening

Climate risk has the same cascade potential: physical events trigger credit losses, which trigger market repricing, which triggers liquidity pressure, which triggers reputational damage. The interaction matrix is the tool that makes these chains visible.

## 5. Emerging Risk Methodology

### The Delphi Method for Climate Risk Identification

Chapter 6 describes how the Delphi Method identified climate risk before regulatory mandates:

> "Climate risk provides a historical example. Before climate and environmental risk became an established L1 taxonomy category, institutions that used Delphi-style processes were identifying physical risk to asset portfolios, transition risk from regulatory changes, and stranded asset risk from energy policy shifts. These identifications -- emerging from anonymous expert consultation rather than workshop consensus -- provided the evidence base for adding climate risk to the taxonomy years before regulatory mandates required it."
> -- Chapter 6, Delphi's Double Duty

### Delphi's Dual Purpose: Risk Identification and Taxonomy Evolution

> "The Delphi Method serves two purposes within the methodology. Its primary purpose is to identify specific emerging risks that the institution should be monitoring and, where appropriate, including in its risk inventory. Its secondary purpose is to provide intelligence for taxonomy evolution -- when multiple Delphi panellists independently identify a risk that does not fit within the current taxonomy structure, that is a signal that the taxonomy needs to be updated."
> -- Chapter 6

This is precisely what happened with climate risk: Delphi panels identified it as an emerging risk, which led to taxonomy updates that included climate as a named category.

### Horizon Scanning Through PESTLE

Chapter 5's PESTLE framework includes "Environmental and Climate" as one of six assessment dimensions:

> "Each dimension is assessed for its relevance to the institution's specific business model, geographic footprint, and risk profile. The findings must be mappable to the risk taxonomy established in Chapter 4 -- a PESTLE finding that cannot be connected to a taxonomy node is either an indication that the taxonomy is incomplete or that the finding is not relevant to this institution."
> -- Chapter 5

### The Quarterly Re-Identification Cycle and Climate

Chapter 13 describes how the quarterly cycle captures evolving climate risks:

> "The external context is updated. The PESTLE assessment from Phase 1 (Chapter 5) is refreshed to reflect material developments since the prior quarter. This is not a full PESTLE exercise -- it is a targeted update focusing on what has changed: new regulatory announcements, macroeconomic shifts, geopolitical developments, technology disruptions, legal precedents, environmental events."
> -- Chapter 13, Quarterly Re-Identification

Note the explicit inclusion of "environmental events" in the quarterly PESTLE refresh -- this ensures climate-related developments are systematically captured at least quarterly.

### SWIFT Workshop Application to Climate

Chapter 6's SWIFT guide words can be directly applied to climate risk identification:

> | **What has changed since last year that could...** | Identifies emerging and evolving risks |
> | **What are we assuming that might not be true?** | Challenges embedded assumptions |

Applied to climate: "What has changed in climate regulation since last quarter that could affect our lending book?" or "What are we assuming about the long-term value of our commercial real estate collateral that might not be true under a 2-degree warming scenario?"

### Event-Driven Triggers for Climate

Chapter 13 lists six categories of events that trigger immediate risk identification updates. Several apply directly to climate:

> "A significant change in the external environment occurs. Regulatory announcements, macroeconomic shifts, geopolitical events, technology disruptions."
> -- Chapter 13, Event-Driven Updates

New climate regulations (e.g., carbon pricing announcements), extreme weather events affecting portfolios, or peer institution climate-related losses would all constitute event-driven triggers for climate risk re-identification.

## 6. Case Studies

### The Broader Pattern: Risk Types That Were "Not Standalone" Until They Were

The book provides several case studies of risks that manifested through existing categories but were initially invisible because the taxonomy did not name them. These serve as direct parallels for the climate risk debate.

#### Conduct Risk -- PPI Scandal (Chapter 4)

> "PPI sales were classified as a profitable cross-selling activity within normal business operations. The risk identification frameworks in use at the time did not include 'customer suitability' as a material risk category. Conduct risk, as a distinct taxonomy entry, did not exist in most institutions' risk classifications."
> -- Chapter 4

**Cost:** Over GBP 50 billion to the UK banking industry; GBP 22 billion to Lloyds Banking Group alone.

**Parallel to climate:** Like climate risk, conduct risk manifested through existing categories (product risk, legal risk, reputational risk) but was invisible until named. The argument for including climate as a named category (while still assessing it through transmission channels) mirrors exactly how conduct risk was eventually handled.

#### Cyber Risk -- The Evolutionary Pattern (Chapter 4)

> "Cyber risk was buried inside 'IT risk' or 'operational risk -- systems' until regulators began requiring dedicated assessment."
> -- Chapter 4

**Parallel to climate:** Cyber risk followed the same evolutionary path. It was initially treated as a sub-type of operational risk. Institutions that created dedicated identification processes (while still mapping cyber through operational risk for capital purposes) were better positioned than those that waited.

#### Northern Rock -- External Context Not Mapped to Specific Vulnerabilities (Chapter 5)

> "Northern Rock funded 75% of its mortgage lending through securitisation and wholesale markets rather than retail deposits. [...] The external context was changing throughout 2006 and into 2007. [...] What was missing was a systematic assessment that connected these external changes to Northern Rock's specific business model."
> -- Chapter 5

**Cost:** Nationalisation in 2008; approximately GBP 2 billion taxpayer loss.

**Parallel to climate:** Just as Northern Rock's wholesale funding dependency was a known business model feature that needed external context mapping, banks' carbon-intensive lending portfolios are known exposures that require climate scenario mapping. The methodology's PESTLE framework, applied to climate, forces this connection to be made explicitly.

#### Dexia -- Identification Exists but Integration Does Not (Chapter 12)

> "The risk identification outputs existed. The sovereign debt positions were visible. The wholesale funding dependency was documented. What was missing was the integration that would have connected those identification outputs to the institution's capital planning."
> -- Chapter 12

**Cost:** Two government bailouts; broken up entirely in 2011.

**Parallel to climate:** Many banks today have identified climate risks in some form but have not integrated them into capital planning, stress testing, or strategic decision-making. The transmission channel approach ensures climate risks flow through to the same capital and Board processes as any other material risk.

#### SVB -- When the Ongoing Cycle Fails (Chapter 13)

> "SVB did not fail because it lacked a risk identification process. It failed because the ongoing cycle -- the quarterly re-identification, the event-driven updates, the continuous recalibration that keeps the process current -- had degraded to the point of irrelevance."
> -- Chapter 13

**Cost:** $209 billion in assets; largest US bank failure since 2008.

**Parallel to climate:** Climate risk identification requires the same ongoing cycle discipline. A bank that assesses climate risk once (e.g., in a TCFD report) but does not quarterly re-identify climate transmission channels as conditions evolve (new regulations, shifting physical risk patterns, stranded asset developments) will face the same degradation.

## 7. Practitioner Insights

### First-Person Observation: Climate Risk Entered Through Delphi

The author's direct experience at a European G-SIB informs the climate framework:

> "Climate risk provides a historical example. Before climate and environmental risk became an established L1 taxonomy category, institutions that used Delphi-style processes were identifying physical risk to asset portfolios, transition risk from regulatory changes, and stranded asset risk from energy policy shifts."
> -- Chapter 6

### The Framing of Identification vs Assessment

The author's emphasis on the distinction between identification and assessment is critical for climate:

> "Re-assessment asks whether existing risks have changed. Re-identification asks whether the risk landscape has changed. The first updates a list. The second updates an understanding."
> -- Chapter 13

Applied to climate: most banks are doing climate risk *assessment* (scoring known climate exposures) but not climate risk *identification* (discovering which transmission channels create new risks they haven't previously recognized).

### The Straw Man Lesson

> "At a European G-SIB, I learned this lesson in the first cycle. When the straw man was presented as 'the proposed risk list' rather than 'the starting point for discussion', participants deferred to it. When it was presented as 'here is what the evidence shows -- now tell me what is wrong with it', the workshops produced genuine challenge, debate, and identification of risks the straw man had missed."
> -- Chapter 5

Applied to climate: presenting climate as a pre-defined "separate risk register" invites deference and compliance theatre. Presenting climate transmission channels as prompts within the existing risk identification process invites genuine identification.

### The Enterprise Portfolio View

> "At one institution, the risk inventory contained thirty-seven material risks. The ICAAP addressed twelve. The gap was not deliberate -- it was structural. Different teams, different timelines, different reporting lines, different systems."
> -- Chapter 12

This observation applies directly to climate risk: if climate is identified separately from the main risk inventory, it creates exactly this structural gap. The transmission channel approach integrates climate into the existing inventory, ensuring it reaches capital planning.

### Building the Interaction Matrix

> "At a European G-SIB where I first built this process, building the first interaction matrix revealed that counterparty credit risk was connected -- through trigger or amplification relationships -- to eleven other material risks. Market risk connected to nine. These were not surprises to experienced practitioners. What was new was making the connections explicit, documented, and traceable. The interaction matrix turned intuition into auditable methodology."
> -- Chapter 10

Applied to climate: practitioners intuitively understand that climate affects multiple risk types, but the interaction matrix makes these connections explicit and auditable -- precisely what regulators require.

### The Compliance Theatre Warning

> "A bottom-up process that produces compliance theatre is worse than no process at all, because it creates a documented record suggesting that identification has occurred when it has not."
> -- Chapter 7

Applied to climate: a standalone climate risk register that exists separately from the main risk inventory creates the appearance of climate risk identification while potentially missing the actual transmission channels through existing risk types.

## Article Construction Notes

### Core Argument Structure

1. **The problem:** Banks are treating climate risk as a separate register, creating silos, double-counting, and integration gaps.
2. **The evidence:** Regulatory guidance (ECB, EBA, PRA, Fed) all point to transmission channels, not standalone categories. The book's methodology explicitly states climate is "not a standalone risk category."
3. **The methodology:** Map climate through six transmission channels into existing L1 risk types using the interaction matrix. Use PESTLE for horizon scanning, Delphi for emerging risk identification, SWIFT workshops for structured questioning.
4. **The parallel:** Conduct risk and cyber risk followed the same pattern -- initially invisible, then treated as standalone, eventually integrated properly.
5. **The practical steps:** Build a climate transmission map using the interaction matrix. Include climate prompts in quarterly SWIFT workshops. Add climate scenarios to PESTLE assessments. Ensure climate risks flow through to ICAAP/CCAR via the same inventory as all other risks.

### Potential Opening Hook

The Delphi finding from Chapter 6 -- institutions using Delphi were identifying climate risks "years before regulatory mandates required it" -- provides a strong opening that positions the article's argument in the practitioner's experience.

### Key Differentiation from Generic Climate Risk Articles

This article's unique value is the **transmission channel mapping methodology**: not just arguing that climate isn't standalone (many articles say this), but showing exactly how to map it through existing risk types using the interaction matrix, bow-tie analysis, and concentration analysis from the book's methodology.
