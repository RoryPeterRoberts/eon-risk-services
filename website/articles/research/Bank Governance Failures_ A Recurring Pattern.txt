Barings to Credit Suisse: Why Governance Bypass Keeps Killing Banks
The Systemic Illusion of Control
The architecture of modern global banking is predicated on the assumption of rational control. Financial institutions deploy highly sophisticated risk management frameworks, multi-tiered lines of defense, algorithmic trade surveillance, and independent compliance functions, all operating under the stringent oversight of international regulatory bodies. Yet, the persistent recurrence of catastrophic, multi-billion-dollar banking failures reveals a fundamental vulnerability that lies not in the design of these risk systems, but in their execution. From the collapse of Barings Bank in 1995 to the dual implosions of Archegos and Greensill at Credit Suisse in 2021, a definitive organizational pathology emerges. This pathology is best understood as "governance bypass."
Governance bypass is not the absence of internal controls, nor is it the failure of risk identification systems to detect anomalies. Rather, it is an active or passive organizational failure mode wherein risks are accurately identified, quantified, and escalated through proper channels, but governance structures systematically fail to act upon them, rationalize them away, or actively suppress them.1 It represents a scenario in which the institutional immune system successfully identifies a pathogen, but the central nervous system of executive management intentionally suppresses the immune response because the pathogen is, temporarily, generating extraordinary revenue.
This report provides an exhaustive forensic analysis of seven landmark banking failures. By examining specific internal audit findings, whistleblower escalations, and the corresponding management responses, this analysis isolates the mechanics of governance bypass. Furthermore, it synthesizes academic literature—specifically Diane Vaughan's concept of the "normalisation of deviance"—and international regulatory frameworks, including the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) Corporate Governance Principles and Financial Stability Board (FSB) publications, to demonstrate why institutions continuously neutralize their own warnings.
Theoretical Framework: The Normalisation of Deviance and Governance Bypass
To understand why sophisticated financial institutions ignore internal warnings, one must look beyond pure financial incentive and examine the sociology of risk. The academic literature identifies "governance bypass" as a phenomenon where the creation of a plurality of norms by informal power structures subverts established official rules and standards.2 In the high-stakes environment of global banking, this subversion is intrinsically linked to the "normalisation of deviance," a term coined by sociologist Diane Vaughan in her seminal analysis of the Challenger Space Shuttle disaster.1
The normalisation of deviance describes the insidious, gradual process through which unacceptable practices or standards become organizationally acceptable.1 When deviant behavior—such as the repeated breaching of trading limits or the overriding of compliance alerts—is repeated without immediate catastrophic results, it becomes the socially accepted norm.1 It ceases to be viewed as a violation and is instead integrated into the routine operational friction of the business.6 Over time, the boundary between safe and unsafe operations is continually renegotiated, blinding the organization to the accumulation of systemic risk.8
In the banking sector, this normalisation is accelerated by the "profit paradox." When a business unit operates outside of its risk mandate but generates outsized profits, the formal governance structures designed to rein in that behavior are often bypassed by informal organizational consensus.9 The risk management function, rather than acting as an independent police force, is slowly culturally subordinated, transforming into an administrative function that merely documents the deviance rather than halting it.6 The subsequent case studies illustrate exactly how this theoretical framework manifests in catastrophic financial reality.
Barings Bank (1995): The Blueprint for Matrix Failure
The collapse of Barings Bank, the United Kingdom's oldest merchant bank, remains the foundational blueprint for modern governance bypass. Triggered by £860 million (approximately $1.3 billion at the time) in unauthorized trading losses by Nick Leeson, the failure was not a sudden, unpredictable event.11 It was the culmination of a two-and-a-half-year period of sustained, concealed trading in Nikkei futures, Japanese Government Bond (JGB) futures, and options on the Singapore International Monetary Exchange (SIMEX) and Osaka exchanges.13
The Specific Internal Warning: The 1994 Audit Report
The critical failure mode at Barings was the structural suppression of glaring internal audit findings. In July and August 1994, James Baker, an internal auditor for Baring Securities Limited (BSL), conducted a comprehensive review of the Baring Futures Singapore (BFS) office where Leeson operated.14 Baker's investigation resulted in a report, finalized in October 1994, which explicitly mapped the exact vulnerabilities that Leeson was actively exploiting.14
The Baker report flagged a catastrophic structural flaw: there was a complete lack of segregation of duties between the front office (trading) and the back office (settlements), both of which were entirely controlled by Leeson.14 This arrangement violated the most fundamental principles of financial risk control, as it allowed Leeson to execute unauthorized trades and subsequently manipulate the settlement data—specifically through the infamous "Account 88888"—to conceal his losses.11 Furthermore, the internal audit report recommended a comprehensive review of Leeson's funding requirements, which were highly anomalous, and strongly advised the imposition of strict position limits on his activities.15
Management Response and Matrix Accountability Void
The October 1994 report was distributed to the highest echelons of Barings' management, including Peter Norris (Chief Executive Officer of BSL), Simon Jones (Director of BFS and Regional Operations Manager for South Asia), and James Bax (Managing Director of Baring Securities Singapore).11 The management response was characterized by systemic paralysis, facilitated by a matrix-based reporting system that created a massive accountability void.13
Under this matrix structure, Leeson nominally reported through different management lines simultaneously, making the responsibility for his oversight highly ambiguous.13 Simon Jones, who possessed direct local responsibility for Barings' operations in Singapore, took absolutely no significant steps to implement the recommended segregation of duties.18 Jones was highly protective of the Singapore operations, which were reporting massive (albeit fictitious) profits, and actively shielded Leeson from deeper scrutiny.19
James Bax demonstrated a complete misunderstanding of Leeson's role, operating under the assumption that Leeson was exclusively handling settlements and that London management was monitoring his trading.11 Consequently, Bax did not see a need to supervise Leeson's trading activities locally.11 In London, Peter Norris received memorandums regarding the lack of formal controls and anomalous funding requests but failed to act decisively, partly because he allowed soured personal relationships with other executives to cloud his judgment regarding their concerns.11
Timeline and Collapse
The timeline of the Barings collapse demonstrates a rapid acceleration of risk following the ignored warnings. The risk was explicitly identified internally by the audit team in August 1994 and formalized in the October 1994 report.14 Despite the audit's stark warnings, no remedial action was taken over the subsequent four months.15 In January 1995, the Kobe earthquake severely impacted the Japanese markets, causing Leeson's unhedged long positions on the Nikkei to hemorrhage value.20 To mask the losses, Leeson doubled down, selling immense volumes of options to fund margin calls.19
By late February 1995, just four months after the internal audit explicitly detailed his lack of supervision, the losses ballooned beyond the bank's total capital reserves.13 The Bank of England’s Board of Banking Supervision (BoBS) report, published in July 1995, concluded that the disaster was the result of a "virtual total failure of risk management systems and controls, and managerial confusion".13 The BoBS report noted that management failed to question the high levels of apparent profit generated from supposedly low-risk arbitrage, highlighting how outsized revenue generation successfully disabled executive skepticism.13
Société Générale (2008): The Normalisation of Systemic Deviance
Thirteen years after the collapse of Barings, the French banking giant Société Générale (SocGen) suffered a €4.9 billion loss due to massive unauthorized directional positions taken by a 31-year-old junior trader, Jérôme Kerviel.22 Operating within the equity derivatives division on the Turbo warrants desk, Kerviel amassed a €49 billion long position on European index futures by January 2008.22 Much like Leeson, Kerviel possessed intimate knowledge of back-office operations, allowing him to conceal his directional bets by booking fictitious offsetting transactions.22
The Saturation of Internal Alerts
The SocGen case represents a severe evolution in governance bypass: the failure mode shifted from ignoring a single comprehensive audit report to normalizing an overwhelming volume of continuous system alerts. According to the internal Mission Green summary report and subsequent independent investigations, Kerviel's trading triggered at least 75 distinct internal alerts from risk control officers, compliance officers, and accountants over a two-year period spanning 2006 to early 2008.23
These warnings were not subtle. Kerviel's activities repeatedly tripped the bank's ELIOT (management and control) system and regulatory calculation metrics.22 For instance, on January 15, 2008, regulatory calculations flagged a massive and anomalous Cooke ratio for a specific counterparty (Bank E) that Kerviel was utilizing to mask his trades.22 When confronted by risk agents regarding eight forward transactions with highly abnormal nominal amounts, Kerviel falsely claimed the transactions were canceled or netted.22
Crucially, the warnings extended beyond internal systems. In November 2007, the Frankfurt-based derivatives exchange, Eurex, explicitly contacted the bank's compliance department seeking explanations for Kerviel's highly unusual trading patterns.24 This external inquiry led to internal compliance contacting Kerviel's immediate supervisor, yet the investigation was easily deflected.24
The Illusion of Compliance and Forged Explanations
The Mission Green report concluded that the failure to detect the fraud was deeply rooted in a culture of superficial compliance. Operating staff and control functions generally followed basic procedures, generating alerts when thresholds were breached.22 However, they failed entirely to carry out systematic, detailed, or extended checks when those alerts were triggered.22
Governance bypass at SocGen was facilitated by Kerviel's aggressive use of forged documentation. On at least seven documented occasions between April 2007 and January 2008, Kerviel utilized forged emails purporting to be from counterparties at five different major banks to justify his fictitious transactions or to explain away discrepancies to controllers.22 Because the controllers operated in a siloed environment focused on clearing alerts rather than investigating root causes, they accepted these fabricated documents without independent verification.22
This dynamic is the quintessential manifestation of Diane Vaughan's normalisation of deviance. Because the alerts were frequent and the trader consistently provided plausible (albeit fabricated) explanations that were never deeply scrutinized, the continuous triggering of high-risk flags became normalized organizational background noise.1 The €4.9 billion loss was realized between January 21 and 23, 2008, when the bank was forced to rapidly unwind the €50 billion in unauthorized bets in highly unfavorable market conditions.22
UBS London Whale / Kweku Adoboli (2011): Supervisory Proximity and Revenue Blindness
In 2011, the Swiss banking group UBS suffered a $2.3 billion loss resulting from unauthorized trading by Kweku Adoboli on the Exchange Traded Funds (ETF) desk within the Global Synthetic Equities (GSE) division in London.25 The regulatory investigation conducted by the UK Financial Services Authority (FSA) resulted in a £29.7 million fine for the bank, citing profound failures in organizing and controlling its affairs responsibly and effectively.25
Internal Control Failures and the GBSOV Break
The FSA enforcement notice meticulously detailed how the computerized risk management systems at UBS were highly deficient, creating systemic vulnerabilities that Adoboli actively exploited.25 The Front Office risk system possessed fatal structural flaws: it allowed internal futures trades to be booked to a generic "internal" counterparty without requiring an automatic mirror trade or the identification of a specific internal counterparty.25 Furthermore, there were no automatic filters in the trade input systems designed to identify off-market prices or unusually large notional transactions.25 Adoboli utilized these loopholes to disguise his underlying loss-making positions by booking unmatched trades, executing late bookings to manipulate profit and loss (P&L), and utilizing fictitious trades with deferred settlement dates.25
The most glaring internal warning occurred during the Global Balance Sheet Ownership Verification (GBSOV) process. In July 2011, this reconciliation process identified a massive break of CHF 209 million arising directly from Adoboli's desk.25 This severe anomaly was escalated to senior personnel and was formally discussed at the Finance London Operational Risk Committee.25 In a stark display of governance bypass, the committee concluded that no actual amounts were at risk. The CHF 209 million break remained uncleared for weeks until Adoboli himself fraudulently cleared it on August 31, 2011.25 The back office demonstrated a complete lack of capability to effectively identify, challenge, and escalate sustained control issues, operating under the dangerous assumption that their main role was the facilitation of the front office.25
Governance Failure: The Breakdown of Supervisory Proximity
The governance bypass at UBS was heavily influenced by a breakdown in supervisory proximity and acute "revenue blindness." Following a corporate restructuring, the ETF desk was moved to the GSE division, and its direct supervision was shifted from a London-based supervisor to one based in the United States.25 The previous London supervisor continued to receive reports but held no formal ongoing responsibility.25 This geographical and operational disconnect created an environment of inadequate supervision.
More damning was the management's willful blindness to inexplicable revenue generation. Adoboli's desk saw its net revenue surge from $9 million in 2010 to $52 million by the second quarter of 2011.25 This geometric increase was several times greater than the desk's permitted risk limits.25 Yet, line management failed to make any substantive inquiries into how exactly this revenue was being generated.25 When the desk breached its formal risk limits four times between June and July 2011, these breaches were escalated. However, rather than initiating disciplinary or corrective action, a supervisor actually congratulated Adoboli on the profit generated during one of the breaches.25 The FSA concluded that UBS had failed to implement a "culture of challenge," allowing Adoboli a free rein to amass a $2.3 billion loss before his arrest in September 2011.25
JPMorgan Chase London Whale / Bruno Iksil (2012): Epistemic Denial and Model Manipulation
The 2012 JPMorgan Chase "London Whale" incident illustrates how governance bypass can be executed at the highest executive levels through the deliberate manipulation of quantitative risk metrics. Traders in the Chief Investment Office (CIO) in London, primarily Bruno Iksil, amassed massive, highly illiquid positions in the synthetic credit derivatives market, ultimately resulting in an estimated $6.2 billion loss for the bank.28
The Internal Warnings: Continuous Limit Breaches
A comprehensive bipartisan investigation and subsequent 300-page report by the U.S. Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations (PSI) revealed that the CIO's Synthetic Credit Portfolio (SCP) was routinely and massively breaching its risk limits.30 These breaches were not hidden in a secret account; they were continuously and accurately reported through standard internal risk reports.30 Data detailing these breaches was disseminated to CIO management, risk personnel, traders, and even externally to the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) on an ongoing basis.30
However, the internal warnings did not spark in-depth reviews or immediate remedial actions to lower the bank's risk profile. Instead, the breaches were either largely ignored or resolved administratively by simply raising the risk limits to accommodate the oversized positions.30 This reflects a culture where limits were viewed as flexible guidelines rather than hard operational boundaries.
Governance Failure: Attacking the Metric
When the sheer size of the positions began to trigger bank-wide Value at Risk (VaR) limits, JPMorgan engaged in a profound form of "epistemic denial." Rather than reducing the risk to comply with the metric, the bank's management decided to change the metric itself.30 Internal CIO traders and risk personnel frequently attacked the accuracy of the existing risk metrics, arguing that they were too conservative and overstated the true riskiness of credit derivatives.30
In late January 2012, while actively breaching both CIO and bank-wide VaR limits, management hurriedly adopted a new VaR model specifically for the SCP.30 This unapproved model implementation had an immediate and desired effect: it lowered the SCP’s reported VaR by 50%.30 By artificially suppressing the risk metric, the CIO technically ended its limit breaches overnight, providing the false justification required to engage in substantially more risky derivatives trading.30
The Senate report found that the governance failure extended to deliberate portfolio mischaracterization. CIO quantitative analysts pressured the bank’s analysts to categorize trades in a way that produced the lowest possible Risk-Weighted Asset (RWA) total to satisfy Basel III capital requirements.29 The bank also actively misinformed investors, regulators, and the public by claiming the SCP was a "macro hedge," despite a complete absence of basic documentation regarding hedging objectives or strategies.30 When the new VaR model was later determined to be plagued by formulaic errors and manual data entry flaws, the bank was forced to revoke it in May 2012, rapidly exposing the unmitigated disaster that led to the $6.2 billion loss.30 The OCC, the bank's primary regulator, was also cited for severe supervisory failures, having received the breach data but failing entirely to review it or investigate the trading activity.30
Wells Fargo (2016): The Weaponization of the Ethics Hotline
While trading floor failures often involve passive negligence or the manipulation of mathematical models, the Wells Fargo fake accounts scandal represents a hostile, active form of governance bypass. Between 2011 and 2015, driven by extreme sales pressure and unattainable quotas, Wells Fargo employees opened approximately 3.5 million unauthorized bank and credit card accounts without customer consent.31 This systemic fraud ultimately cost the bank over $4.5 billion in regulatory fines and civil settlements, including a $3 billion settlement with the Department of Justice and the Securities and Exchange Commission, and a $185 million fine from the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).31
The Saturation of Whistleblower Reports
The timeline of internal warnings at Wells Fargo is staggeringly long, demonstrating that executive management was well aware of the fraudulent practices years before the scandal broke publicly. As early as 2009, former Wells Fargo employees began filing formal whistleblower complaints with the Department of Labor’s Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA), alleging retaliation after reporting the creation of unauthorized accounts.34
Internally, the bank's third-party operated EthicsLine was inundated with reports. According to a subsequent independent investigation conducted by the law firm Shearman & Sterling, Wells Fargo's HR legal team identified at least 115 potential whistleblower cases specifically related to sales practice misconduct between 2011 and 2016.35 These internal warnings explicitly detailed how branch managers were threatening termination if personal bankers did not meet "Gr-eight" cross-selling quotas, forcing employees to resort to forging customer signatures and manipulating account funding to survive.34
The Governance Failure: Institutional Betrayal
The governance bypass at Wells Fargo was characterized by a decentralized corporate structure that granted excessive autonomy to Community Bank senior leadership, who actively resisted outside scrutiny and minimized the scale of the problem when forced to report.35 The sales culture was described by employees as a "soul-crushing" environment of fear and daily intimidation.35
More disturbingly, the internal compliance mechanisms designed to protect the bank were effectively weaponized against the employees attempting to use them. Instead of utilizing the EthicsLine data to investigate the root cause of the fraud, management allegedly monitored the tipsters and utilized trivial infractions—such as minor tardiness—as pretexts for immediate termination.37 In one highly publicized instance, a whistleblower personally emailed CEO John Stumpf to report the unethical sales tactics and was promptly fired.37 Another senior manager in the commercial banking unit escalated concerns to the ethics hotline regarding the falsification of customer information and was fired without reason in 2019; the manager later won a $22 million OSHA whistleblower retaliation judgment.40
The Shearman & Sterling report concluded that the performance scorecards and intense pressure created by leadership were the root causes of the scandal.41 In response, the board of directors initiated historic clawbacks, taking back $41 million from CEO John Stumpf and $19 million from the head of community banking, Carrie Tolstedt, recognizing that the executive incentive structures directly fueled the protracted, systemic fraud and the subsequent suppression of internal dissent.41
Credit Suisse — Archegos (2021): Client Advocacy Over Risk Control
The collapse of the Archegos Capital Management family office in March 2021 cost Credit Suisse $5.5 billion, making it the largest trading loss in the bank's history.42 It stands as a profound example of a first-line business unit completely neutralizing and overriding second-line risk controls.
Internal Risk Warnings and Extreme Exposures
Archegos, managed by Sung Kook "Bill" Hwang, utilized total return swaps heavily leveraged through Credit Suisse's Prime Financing division to build massive, concentrated equity positions.42 According to the independent investigation conducted by the law firm Paul, Weiss, the architecture of risk controls at Credit Suisse was not inherently flawed; the existing systems successfully identified and quantified the critical risks.42
Junior employees within Credit Risk Management (CRM) repeatedly "sounded the alarm" regarding the severe inadequacy of Archegos’s swap margins relative to the bank's Potential Exposure (PE) and scenario exposure models.43 The data was irrefutable: by February 2021, Archegos held the largest notional exposure of any Credit Suisse Prime Financing client, generated among the largest scenario exposures of all global hedge fund portfolios, and was characterized by the largest and most frequent limit breaches.43 The portfolio was explicitly identified as being among the most concentrated, leveraged, and volatile in the bank's entire book.43
Governance Failure: The Subordination of Risk Management
The Paul Weiss report explicitly noted that the massive losses were the result of a "fundamental failure of management and controls" wherein senior managers persistently failed to address the risks connected with Archegos's trades.42 Governance bypass occurred because the Prime Services Risk (PSR) unit—which should have been evaluating the client objectively—effectively morphed into an advocate for Archegos, routinely taking the client's side against the bank's own internal limits.43
In 2019, the business unit successfully argued on Archegos’s behalf to drastically lower standard margin levels on swaps from an average of 20% to just 7.5%.43 The justification for this accommodation was that the portfolio maintained a market-neutral short bias. However, even when that justification vanished and the portfolio became heavily long-biased and deeply volatile, the business failed to restore the margin levels, allowing them to drop to an astonishingly low 5.9% by September 2020.43
Senior risk management enabled this indulgent orientation. Rather than enforcing strict remediation deadlines for the continuous limit breaches, senior risk officers accepted the business unit's justifications and permitted a highly accommodative approach.43 The bank suffered from a systemic "blind spot" regarding counterparty risk, failing to insist on disclosures from Archegos that would have revealed the family office held highly concentrated positions in the exact same single-name equities across multiple rival banks.43 Ultimately, the Counterparty Oversight Committee (CPOC) failed to exercise its authority to rein in the excesses across both the first and second lines of defense.43
Credit Suisse — Greensill / Supply Chain Finance Funds (2021): The Suspension of Due Diligence
Occurring almost simultaneously with the Archegos disaster, the collapse of the British financial firm Greensill Capital exposed Credit Suisse to a $10 billion supply-chain finance fund blow-up.44 This dual failure decimated the bank's reputation, resulted in massive executive turnover, and accelerated the bank's ultimate demise and forced acquisition by UBS.44
Credit Suisse had heavily marketed four connected supply-chain finance funds, pitching them to wealthy private banking clients as low-risk, highly liquid investments.44 However, an internal review known as the Paul Horta report found that the bank's asset managers were profoundly "too trusting" of Greensill.46 Internal due diligence protocols were effectively bypassed; the bank failed to scrutinize the underlying assets and neglected to monitor where the Greensill funds were actually deploying the capital.46 This suspension of basic due diligence allowed Greensill to concentrate its lending into highly opaque, high-risk industrial entities rather than distributing the risk across diverse, short-term corporate invoices.45
The Swiss financial regulator FINMA subsequently opened enforcement proceedings, concluding that Credit Suisse "seriously breached its supervisory obligations" by failing to implement adequate reporting and safeguards despite the complex and opaque nature of Greensill's business model.45 The governance bypass in this instance was rooted in a willing abdication of oversight: as long as the funds attracted massive fee-generating inflows, the second line of defense was culturally discouraged from asking the structural questions that would have exposed the underlying fragility of the funds.

Regulatory Architecture and the Future of Risk Escalation
The transition from a theoretical risk framework to applied risk mitigation relies entirely on the strength of an institution's governance culture. When analyzing the longitudinal data spanning twenty-six years from Barings to Credit Suisse, it is overwhelmingly evident that catastrophic banking failures are rarely caused by a lack of data. In every case examined, internal systems successfully generated alerts, quantitative limits were visibly breached, and human auditors, junior risk officers, or whistleblowers escalated their concerns.
The failure occurs at the apex of the governance structure. Recognizing this, international regulatory bodies have increasingly shifted their focus from establishing structural capital requirements to attempting to regulate the behavioral and cultural dynamics of bank boardrooms.
The BCBS Corporate Governance Principles
The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS) explicitly addressed the structural shortcomings of bank governance in its revised Corporate Governance Principles for Banks (2015).47 Principle 6 of the BCBS framework is designed specifically to prevent the type of bypass seen at Credit Suisse and Barings. It emphasizes the absolute necessity of an effective, independent risk management function under the direction of a Chief Risk Officer (CRO).47
Crucially, the BCBS dictates that the CRO must possess sufficient stature, independence, adequate resources, and direct, unfettered access to the board of directors.47 The failures at UBS, where the back office was viewed merely as a facilitation tool for the front office, and at Credit Suisse, where prime services risk officers enabled client advocacy, represent catastrophic violations of Principle 6. Furthermore, the European Central Bank's draft guide on governance highlights that the CRO must have the unquestioned authority to escalate, and upon discretion, veto any decision that entails increased risks—and that these veto cases must be formally documented.49 JPMorgan’s decision to bypass its standard VaR models to artificially cure limit breaches without regulatory approval directly contravenes the spirit of these escalation protocols.30
FSB Publications on Governance Culture and Incentives
The Financial Stability Board (FSB) has extensively analyzed how governance bypass is intrinsically linked to compensation and incentive structures. In its thematic reviews and guidance on supervisory interaction with financial institutions on risk culture, the FSB notes that financial and non-financial incentives are the primary drivers of human behavior, and by extension, misconduct risk.49
The FSB argues that an effective governance framework must include a clearly articulated Risk Appetite Statement (RAS) that encompasses not just quantitative measures, but qualitative statements addressing reputation, conduct risks, and unethical practices.47 The Wells Fargo scandal serves as the ultimate case study in misaligned incentives. The decentralized bonus structure heavily incentivized the opening of new accounts, creating an environment where extreme sales pressure drove systemic fraud.35 Simultaneously, the bank's culture punished those who reported the fraudulent bypass of consumer consent laws, destroying the psychological safety required for a compliance culture to function.34 The FSB maintains that effective governance requires setting the right "tone from the top" and establishing an environment where effective communication and challenge are rewarded, not subjected to HR retaliation.49
Synthesis: The Mechanics of Institutional Blindness
By synthesizing the organizational behavior observed across these seven multi-billion-dollar failures, three distinct mechanisms of institutional blindness—the tactical execution of governance bypass—become clear:
1. Metric Manipulation and Epistemic Denial: When hard quantitative data conflicts with desired profitability, institutions frequently alter the lens rather than the landscape. JPMorgan’s rapid adoption of a deeply flawed, unapproved VaR model simply to halve its apparent risk and end internal breach alerts is the purest modern example of epistemic denial.30 By attacking the validity of the risk metrics, management bypassed the difficult, costly necessity of executing market deleveraging.
2. Structural Attrition (The Matrix Defense): Complex, convoluted reporting lines actively dilute executive accountability. At Barings, Nick Leeson ruthlessly exploited the ambiguity of a matrix reporting structure, allowing both local Singapore management and London-based product heads to assume the other was monitoring his daily exposures.13 Similarly, at UBS, the geographic decay of supervisory proximity—moving the ETF desk to a different division while leaving its primary supervisor in a different country—created a fatal vacuum where responsibility evaporated.25
3. Active Compliance Weaponization: As demonstrated vividly by Wells Fargo, when a highly profitable business model is intrinsically tied to deviant behavior (such as impossible cross-selling quotas), internal risk mechanisms are decoupled from their protective function. Instead of being used to identify systemic risk, the data extracted from the Ethics Hotline was allegedly utilized to identify dissidents.37 This transforms a second-line defense into an instrument of governance bypass, weaponizing the compliance apparatus against the very whistleblowers it is mandated to protect.
Conclusion
The historical continuum from the 1995 collapse of Barings Bank to the 2021 implosions at Credit Suisse demonstrates a chilling reality: catastrophic banking crises are rarely unpredictable "black swan" events. They are overwhelmingly "grey rhinos"—highly probable, high-impact threats that are actively observed, documented, and escalated by internal systems long before the final loss is realized. Governance bypass is the critical failure mode that allows these explicit warnings to be systematically neutralized.
Addressing this endemic vulnerability requires a fundamental shift in how both regulatory bodies and internal boards of directors evaluate risk culture. First, explosive revenue growth within a specific desk or business line—such as the massive, uncharacteristic P&L spikes seen at Barings and UBS—must be treated with the same severe forensic skepticism as a catastrophic loss. Revenue generated inexplicably, or in excess of allocated risk limits, is rarely an indicator of pure alpha generation; it is almost always a primary indicator of control circumvention.
Second, the structural stature of the second line of defense must be aggressively fortified. Risk management functions cannot operate effectively if they are culturally expected to act as client advocates or deal facilitators, as tragically occurred within Credit Suisse's prime brokerage division. True operational independence requires that risk officers possess absolute veto power over business initiatives, and crucially, that their compensation structures are completely decoupled from the specific revenue generation of the trading desks they oversee.
Finally, boards of directors must evolve to monitor the meta-metrics of their compliance systems. An ethics hotline that generates 115 escalating complaints regarding sales practices over five years (Wells Fargo), or a trading surveillance system that logs 75 anomaly alerts from a single trader (Société Générale), indicates a systemic cultural decay that cannot be resolved at the line-management level. When the normalisation of deviance is allowed to take root, sophisticated internal controls shift from being preventative shields to mere forensic breadcrumbs, utilized only by regulators after the institution has collapsed. Preventing the next major banking failure relies not on engineering a better algorithm or a more complex VaR model, but on engineering a corporate culture where it is structurally and psychologically impossible for executive management to bypass the alarms those systems trigger.
