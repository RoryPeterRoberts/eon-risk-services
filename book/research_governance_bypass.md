# Research Document: Barings to Credit Suisse -- Why Governance Bypass Keeps Killing Banks

## Research compiled: 12 February 2026

---

## 1. Barings Bank (1995)

### The Internal Warning

In August 1994, Barings' Internal Audit department conducted an audit of Barings Futures Singapore (BFS) covering the period 19 July to 1 August 1994. The audit report explicitly identified a critical control weakness: Nick Leeson controlled both the front office (trading) and the back office (settlement) at BFS. The report recommended that "responsibility for the two offices should be separated to reduce the possibility of error and fraud." A further audit in October 1994 raised similar concerns.

### Who Received It

The August 1994 internal audit report was circulated to Barings' senior management in London, including the heads of the settlements and compliance functions.

### The Response

Barings management failed to implement the recommendation. The bank chose to retain Leeson's dual role because of his perceived experience and -- critically -- because of the profits he was generating. The 88888 error account, which Leeson used to conceal losses, was never investigated despite its anomalous balance. Concerns also circulated in the wider market in January and February 1995 about Barings' very large positions on the Osaka Securities Exchange (OSE), but management's focus remained on Leeson's apparent profitability.

### The Loss

GBP 827 million (approximately USD 1.4 billion) -- twice Barings' available trading capital. The bank was declared insolvent on 26 February 1995 and subsequently sold to ING for GBP 1.

### Key Official Report

The Board of Banking Supervision inquiry, set up by the Chancellor of the Exchequer on 27 February 1995 and published 18 July 1995, concluded that the losses were caused by "unauthorised and concealed trading activities" and that "the true position was not noticed earlier because of serious failures of controls and managerial confusion within the Barings group."

## 2. Societe Generale / Jerome Kerviel (2008)

### The Internal Warnings

Between June 2006 and January 2008, Societe Generale's internal control systems generated at least **75 separate alerts** flagging anomalies in Kerviel's trading activity. These red flags were raised by accountants, risk-control officers, and compliance staff. The alerts included transactions that appeared to settle on a Saturday, trades where the counterparty was not named or listed as "pending," and trades that exceeded authorised limits.

More specifically, between July 2006 and September 2007 alone, internal control systems raised **24 alerts** when the value of Kerviel's trades exceeded authorised limits.

### Who Received Them

Alerts were directed to back/middle-office operations, the global equity derivatives department, the accounting group, and the group risk office. Kerviel's direct supervisor also received information about anomalous positions.

### The Response

The bank's risk monitoring unit attributed the anomalies to "recurrent problems with the way the trading software recorded operations" and asked Kerviel's superiors to ensure he did not exceed limits again. Staff did not systematically conduct in-depth investigations when warning flags were raised. The "Mission Green" report (the General Inspection department's internal investigation, commissioned 24 January 2008) found that Kerviel used a variety of concealment techniques -- lies, forged documents (including seven forged emails), and rapid cancellation or modification of anomalous transactions -- that exploited a lack of cross-functionality in the organisation of control functions.

### The Loss

EUR 4.9 billion (approximately USD 7.2 billion at the time), realised when SocGen unwound Kerviel's positions over three days beginning 21 January 2008. Kerviel's peak unauthorised exposure reached EUR 49.9 billion -- more than the bank's entire market capitalisation. The French Banking Commission fined Societe Generale EUR 4 million for control failures on 4 July 2008.

## 3. UBS / Kweku Adoboli (2011)

### The Control Failures

The UK Financial Services Authority (FSA) found that UBS had systemic weaknesses in its systems and controls that allowed Adoboli to conduct unauthorised trading over a period of more than three years (from at least late 2008). Specific failings included:

- The trade capture and processing system had significant deficiencies: trades could be booked to an internal counterparty without sufficient details; there were no effective methods to detect trades at material off-market prices; and there was a lack of integration between systems.
- Front office supervision was "poorly executed and ineffective."
- UBS failed to question the increasing revenue of Adoboli's desk and failed to ensure corresponding increases in controls.
- Risk limits were repeatedly breached without adequate challenge.

### Who Should Have Acted

UBS management at the Global Synthetic Equities (GSE) desk and the Investment Bank's risk and compliance functions. The FSA found failures at multiple levels of the organisation.

### The Response (or Lack Thereof)

The inadequate controls persisted for years. The FSA's Final Notice described persistent failures in supervision, trade booking, and position reconciliation. Revenue growth was celebrated rather than questioned.

### The Loss

USD 2.3 billion from unauthorised trading. The FSA fined UBS GBP 29.7 million (approximately USD 47.6 million) on 26 November 2012 -- the third-largest fine in the FSA's history at that time.

## 4. JPMorgan Chase -- London Whale (2012)

### The Internal Warning (and Suppression)

The US Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations released a 300-page bipartisan report on 15 March 2013 titled "JPMorgan Chase Whale Trades: A Case History of Derivatives Risks and Abuses." The investigation reviewed over 90,000 documents and conducted over 50 interviews. Key findings:

- As early as January 2012, the rapid growth of the Synthetic Credit Portfolio (SCP) in JPMorgan's Chief Investment Office (CIO) breached Value-at-Risk (VaR) limits.
- After a 4-day breach was reported to top bank officials including CEO Jamie Dimon, who approved a temporary limit increase, CIO employees then **hurriedly pushed through approval of a new VaR model that overnight dropped the CIO's purported risk by 50%.**
- The new model was implemented without a truly independent review, without the standard parallel run of old and new models during a probationary period, and with the analytics suite remaining untested.
- The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) was regularly informed of the risk limit breaches and was notified in advance that the CIO VaR model change was projected to drop results by 44%, yet raised no concerns.

### The Pattern of Governance Bypass

The Senate report documented that when the CIO's SCP breached five key risk limits, rather than reduce risky trading activities, JPMorgan either: (a) increased the limits; (b) changed the risk models that calculated risk; or (c) turned a blind eye to the breaches. Additionally, the CIO mismarked its trading book to hide losses and misinformed investors, regulators, and the public.

### The Loss

USD 6.2 billion in trading losses from the Synthetic Credit Portfolio.

## 5. Wells Fargo (2016)

### The Internal Warnings

Internal warnings at Wells Fargo were extensive and sustained over many years:

- **EthicsLine complaints:** From 2006 through 2014, nearly half of all EthicsLine complaints investigated by the Corporate Investigations unit related to sales integrity violations -- employees manipulating or misrepresenting sales to meet targets. By Q4 2009, Wells Fargo had received 667 EthicsLine cases and 242 related terminations/resignations connected to gaming of sales incentive programmes.
- **Employee retaliation:** Employees who reported concerns through the ethics hotline were fired. Bill Bado, a banker in Pennsylvania, refused to open phony accounts, called the ethics hotline, and emailed HR in September 2013 flagging unethical practices. He was terminated eight days later.
- **5,300 employees** were fired over the course of the scandal for sales integrity violations, yet the underlying business model and sales pressure persisted.
- **Customer complaints:** From December 2013 through September 2015, the bank received at least 5,000 customer complaints about lack of consent for account openings.

### Who Received the Warnings

The Shearman & Sterling independent investigation (April 2017) found that Carrie Tolstedt, head of the Community Bank division, and other Community Bank leaders were aware of sales practice problems but were "unwilling to change the sales model or recognise it as the root cause of the problem." They "resisted and impeded scrutiny or oversight from corporate risk management and the Board." Former Chairman and CEO John Stumpf was found to have been "too slow to investigate or critically challenge the sales practices."

### The Shearman & Sterling Report (10 April 2017)

A 110-page report produced by a committee of independent directors, assisted by Shearman & Sterling LLP. The investigation involved 100 interviews and review of over 35 million documents. Root cause finding: "The distortion of the Community Bank's sales culture and performance management system, which, when combined with aggressive sales management, created pressure on employees to sell unwanted or unneeded products to customers."

### Total Penalties and Settlements

- **September 2016:** USD 185 million (CFPB, OCC, City of Los Angeles)
- **February 2020:** USD 3 billion (DOJ/SEC global settlement for criminal and civil violations)
- **December 2022:** USD 3.7 billion (CFPB -- the largest fine in CFPB history, comprising USD 1.7 billion civil penalty plus USD 2 billion consumer redress, covering 16 million affected accounts)
- **Additional settlements:** USD 480 million (shareholder class action); USD 575 million (50-state Attorneys General); USD 1 billion (April 2018, CFPB/OCC for auto-loan and mortgage issues)
- **Cumulative total (scandal-related):** Approximately USD 7--8 billion in direct fines and settlements

## 6. Credit Suisse -- Archegos (2021)

### The Internal Risk Warnings

The Paul, Weiss Special Committee report (published 29 July 2021, based on 80+ interviews and 10+ million documents) documented a pattern of persistent and escalating internal risk warnings that were overridden or ignored by the prime brokerage business:

- **Margin erosion:** In 2019, Archegos asked Credit Suisse to materially lower its swap margins from the 15--25% initial margin range to 7.5%. Credit Suisse agreed.
- **Scenario limit breaches:** By 16 July 2020, Archegos had over USD 600 million in net scenario exposure -- more than 240% of the USD 250 million scenario limit. By 22 July 2020, this had jumped to USD 828 million (330% of the limit). **From that point on, Archegos remained in breach of its scenario limits virtually every week until its March 2021 default.**
- **Stress loss projections:** In February 2021, scenario analysis predicted stress losses in the range of USD 1.4 billion under two scenarios, significantly exceeding the entity's USD 800 million scenario limit.
- **Dynamic margining blocked:** Risk managers intended to ask Archegos for additional margin to reflect the increased credit risk, but were **prevented from doing so** as this was "deemed not to be in the interests of the bank" by those responsible for managing the relationship.
- **Temporary risk appetite increases:** Archegos was awarded several temporary bespoke risk appetite increases, including one in October 2020 of USD 900 million.

### Who Received the Warnings

The Investment Bank's Prime Services business heads and the second-line risk function. The report found failures across both the first and second lines of defence, as well as a lack of escalation to senior management and the board.

### The Response

The Paul, Weiss report concluded that "the business was focused on maximising short-term profits and failed to rein in and, indeed, enabled Archegos' voracious risk-taking." Credit Suisse failed to address "a culture that encouraged aggressive risk-taking and injudicious cost-cutting, as well as a complex and silo-ed organisational structure that impeded the swift identification, understanding, and escalation of risk."

### The Loss

USD 5.5 billion when Archegos defaulted on 25 March 2021.

## 7. Credit Suisse -- Greensill (2021)

### The Internal Warnings

Credit Suisse launched four supply chain finance funds from 2017 in collaboration with Greensill Capital, ultimately holding approximately **USD 10 billion** of client money. The FINMA investigation (concluded February 2023), drawing on reports by Swiss law firm Wenger Plattner based on over 700 pages of internal documents, found:

- **Minimal due diligence:** In 2017, Greensill filled out a brief questionnaire that investigators described as "inadequate." Clear contradictions and red flags in Greensill's submissions were never scrutinised.
- **Risk manager recommendation overruled:** A Credit Suisse risk manager responsible for a loan to Greensill identified a number of risks in Greensill's business model and recommended internally that the bank **not grant the loan**. A senior manager overruled this recommendation.
- **Diligence report dismissed:** Credit Suisse's trade-finance bankers commissioned a Diligence report and escalated it to the bank's Swiss general counsel, Thomas Grotzer, who forwarded it to Michel Degen and Luc Mathys, writing that "the issues raised here should be clarified in detail." In mid-2019, a memorandum by Mathys and portfolio manager Lukas Haas dismissed the Diligence report's allegations as "unsubstantiated and incorrect."
- **Little knowledge or control:** FINMA's investigation showed that Credit Suisse's asset management company had "little knowledge and control over the specific claims" in the funds. It was not Credit Suisse as asset manager that selected and reviewed claims, but Greensill itself.

### The Loss / Investor Exposure

Approximately **USD 10 billion** in total investor exposure at the time of fund closure in March 2021. Credit Suisse has recovered approximately USD 7.4 billion. FINMA concluded that Credit Suisse "seriously breached its supervisory obligations" regarding risk management and organisational structures.

## 8. Academic and Regulatory Framework

### 8.1 Diane Vaughan -- "Normalisation of Deviance"

Sociologist Diane Vaughan coined the term in her 1996 book *The Challenger Launch Decision: Risky Technology, Culture, and Deviance at NASA* (University of Chicago Press). She described it as "the gradual process through which unacceptable practice or standards become acceptable. As the deviant behaviour is repeated without catastrophic results, it becomes the social norm for the organisation."

Key insight: "No fundamental decision was made at NASA to do evil; rather, a series of seemingly harmless decisions were made that incrementally moved the space agency toward a catastrophic outcome." Vaughan argued it was **conformity**, not deviance per se, that caused the Challenger disaster.

**Application to banking:** The concept applies directly to the cases documented above. In each instance, risk limit breaches, control deficiencies, and warning signals became normalised through repetition without immediate catastrophic consequences. The 75 ignored alerts at Societe Generale, the persistent scenario limit breaches at Credit Suisse/Archegos, and the years of EthicsLine complaints at Wells Fargo all exhibit classic normalisation of deviance -- each individual exception seemed manageable, until the accumulated risk became unmanageable.

### 8.2 BCBS Corporate Governance Principles for Banks (July 2015)

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision published 13 revised corporate governance principles in July 2015 (document reference: d328). Key provisions relevant to governance bypass:

- **Principle 1 (Board's overall responsibilities):** The board should monitor management adherence to the risk appetite and tolerance statement, and provide for effective issue resolution processes including systems to "report, track and, when necessary, escalate issues to ensure resolution."
- **Principle 6 (Risk management function):** The risk management function is responsible for developing and implementing the enterprise-wide risk governance framework, with board review and approval.
- **Risk culture and escalation:** The bank's corporate values "should recognise the critical importance of timely and frank discussion and escalation of problems to higher levels within the organisation."
- **Whistleblower protections:** "Employees should be encouraged and able to communicate, confidentially and without the risk of reprisal, legitimate concerns about illegal, unethical or questionable practices."

### 8.3 FSB Framework for Assessing Risk Culture (April 2014)

The Financial Stability Board published "Guidance on Supervisory Interaction with Financial Institutions on Risk Culture: A Framework for Assessing Risk Culture" in April 2014. Key elements:

- **Rationale:** "Weaknesses in risk culture were a root cause of the global financial crisis, as they led to failures in compliance."
- **Three foundational elements** of sound risk culture: risk governance, risk appetite, and compensation.
- **Definition:** Risk culture comprises "the institution's norms, attitudes and behaviours related to risk awareness, risk taking and risk management."
- The guidance does not define a "target" culture but provides a framework for supervisors to assess whether risk culture is sound.

### 8.4 Recent Regulatory Publications (2024--2026)

**ECB Draft Guide on Governance and Risk Culture (July 2024):**
The European Central Bank published a draft Guide on governance and risk culture in July 2024, replacing the 2016 SSM supervisory statement. The Guide sets out supervisory expectations regarding how management bodies and committees should be composed and function, the roles of internal control functions, the importance of risk culture, and expectations regarding risk appetite frameworks. Public consultation closed 16 October 2024.

**BCBS Working Paper 45 -- Lessons on Supervisory Effectiveness (2025):**
A Basel Committee working paper that reviews lessons from bank failures and supervisory practices. Key finding: since banks' "qualitative weaknesses" (flawed risk management and business models) were root causes of bank failures, quantitative regulatory requirements need to be complemented by timely qualitative supervisory measures. The paper acknowledges that "impediments to effective supervision persist, limiting the will and ability of supervisors to act."

## The Common Failure Mode

Across all seven cases, the same pattern recurs:

1. **Risk is identified internally** -- by auditors, control systems, risk managers, or front-line employees.
2. **The warning reaches a decision-maker** -- but the decision-maker has a revenue interest, a relationship interest, or institutional inertia that conflicts with acting on the warning.
3. **The governance mechanism fails** -- the warning is rationalised away, the risk model is changed, the whistleblower is punished, or the escalation pathway is blocked by the very people it is designed to challenge.
4. **Normalisation sets in** -- the breach becomes the new baseline. Each day without a catastrophe reinforces the decision not to act.
5. **The loss materialises** -- and post-event investigations invariably find that the information needed to prevent it was already inside the organisation.

This is not a failure of risk identification. It is a failure of governance to act on identified risk. As the BCBS Working Paper 45 (2025) acknowledges, the fundamental problem is that "impediments to effective supervision persist, limiting the will and ability" to act -- whether those impediments sit inside banks or inside regulators.
